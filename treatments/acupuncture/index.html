Acupuncture: What to Expect, How It Works, and What It Treats

Treatment Guide · 8 min read

If you've never had acupuncture, the idea of someone placing needles in your skin might sound uncomfortable — or even a little intimidating. You're not alone. Even in 2026, when acupuncture is offered at major hospitals, sports medicine clinics, and veteran's health programs across the US and UK, many people still hesitate because they're not sure what it actually feels like.

Here's the short version: it's nothing like getting a shot at the doctor's office. Most people describe the sensation as mild, brief, and surprisingly relaxing. Some even fall asleep during treatment.

This guide covers what acupuncture is, how it works (including what modern neuroscience has to say), which conditions it's most commonly used for, and exactly what to expect during a session.

Think of acupuncture as a dialogue with your nervous system. The needles don't "fix" anything directly. Instead, they signal your body to adjust its own activity — like tapping a phone that has frozen, not replacing the motherboard.

What Is Acupuncture?

Acupuncture is a technique from Traditional Chinese Medicine that involves inserting ultra-thin, sterile needles into specific points on the body. It has been practiced for over 2,000 years, and in that time, practitioners mapped a complex network of points — 361 main ones — each associated with particular physiological effects.

In TCM theory, the needles help regulate the flow of Qi (vital energy) along pathways called meridians. Think of a city subway system: when there's a blockage on one line, trains can't move, stations get overcrowded, and the system breaks down. Acupuncture points are like switching stations — inserting a needle at the right point can help unblock the flow and restore the system's balance.

In Western medicine terms, acupuncture points correspond to areas rich in nerve endings, blood vessels, and immune cells — which is part of why they produce such measurable biological effects.

How Does Acupuncture Work? (What Science Says)

The question "how does acupuncture work" has two answers: the TCM explanation and the modern scientific one. They're not contradictory — they're describing the same phenomenon at different levels of detail.

The TCM Explanation

In TCM, health depends on the smooth, balanced flow of Qi throughout the body. When Qi becomes blocked (stagnation), deficient (too little), or rebellious (flowing the wrong direction), symptoms appear. Acupuncture points are located along the body's meridian channels — think of them as access ports. Inserting a needle at the right point can:

  • Unblock stuck Qi (relieving pain, tension, and emotional stagnation)
  • Strengthen deficient Qi (improving energy and organ function)
  • Redirect Qi that's flowing abnormally (calming nausea, reducing inflammation)

What Modern Research Shows

Over the past 50 years, hundreds of studies have investigated acupuncture's biological mechanisms. Here's what the evidence points to:

🧬 Activates the Nervous System

Needle insertion stimulates sensory nerves under the skin. This sends signals to the brain that trigger the release of endorphins — your body's natural painkillers — and serotonin, which influences mood and relaxation. Some studies have shown measurable changes in brain activity on fMRI scans after acupuncture.

🩸 Modulates Inflammation

A landmark 2014 study published in Nature Medicine found that electroacupuncture (acupuncture with mild electrical stimulation) activated a neural pathway that reduced systemic inflammation in mice. Follow-up research in humans suggests certain acupuncture points can trigger vagal nerve activity, which has an anti-inflammatory effect.

🔬 Affects Blood Flow & Tissue Healing

Needling causes a local inflammatory response at the insertion site, increasing blood circulation to the area. Ultrasound studies show increased blood flow in tissues near acupuncture points. This may help with tissue repair and reducing muscle tension.

🧠 Influences Brain Chemistry

PET scans have shown that acupuncture increases opioid receptor binding in brain regions associated with pain processing. This is why some researchers describe acupuncture as a form of "triggered neuromodulation" — it convinces your brain to adjust its own chemical signaling.

Critically, the scientific consensus is not that "acupuncture is all in your head." The placebo effect plays a role in any medical treatment, but multiple large-scale trials — including those comparing real acupuncture to "sham" (non-penetrating) needles — have found that real acupuncture produces measurably superior results for certain conditions. The National Institutes of Health (NIH) and the World Health Organization (WHO) both recognize acupuncture as a legitimate treatment option for a range of conditions.

What Conditions Can Acupuncture Help With?

While acupuncture is sometimes marketed as a cure-all (which it isn't), the evidence is strongest for several specific conditions:

Pain Conditions (Best Evidence)

The strongest body of evidence supports acupuncture for chronic pain. A landmark 2012 meta-analysis of nearly 18,000 patients published in Archives of Internal Medicine found that acupuncture was significantly more effective than sham acupuncture and standard care for:

  • Chronic back and neck pain — one of the most common reasons people try acupuncture
  • Osteoarthritis — particularly knee osteoarthritis
  • Chronic headache and migraine — some patients find it reduces both frequency and intensity
  • Shoulder pain and frozen shoulder

Other Conditions (Moderate to Emerging Evidence)

  • Nausea and vomiting — especially post-operative or chemotherapy-induced nausea. The "P6" point on the wrist (often used in acupressure bands) has consistent trial support
  • Anxiety and stress — some studies suggest acupuncture can reduce cortisol levels and autonomic nervous system arousal
  • Allergic rhinitis (hay fever) — a 2015 systematic review found acupuncture may reduce symptoms and the need for antihistamines
  • Insomnia — small but promising studies suggest acupuncture may help improve sleep quality, particularly when addressing the underlying TCM pattern
Important: Acupuncture is a complementary therapy. It works best alongside conventional medical care, not instead of it. If you have a serious medical condition, see your doctor first. Acupuncture may help manage symptoms, but it should never delay or replace evidence-based treatment.

What to Expect During an Acupuncture Session

If you're considering acupuncture, knowing what to expect can make the first session much easier. Here's the typical experience, step by step.

1. The Initial Consultation (20–30 minutes)

Your first session will include a detailed health history. The acupuncturist will ask about your symptoms, medical history, lifestyle, diet, sleep patterns, and emotional state. In TCM, they'll also examine your tongue (its color, coating, and shape provide diagnostic clues) and feel your pulse at several positions on each wrist. This isn't mystical — it's a diagnostic method refined over centuries, similar to how a mechanic can tell a lot about an engine by listening to it run.

2. The Needle Insertion (30–45 minutes)

Here's what the needles themselves are like:

  • They're extremely thin. An acupuncture needle is about the width of a human hair — much thinner than a hypodermic needle used for injections or blood draws
  • Insertion is quick. Most points are inserted just a few millimeters deep. You may feel a brief pinch or nothing at all
  • The "de Qi" sensation. After insertion, you might feel a dull ache, heaviness, tingling, or a mild electrical sensation around the needle. In TCM, this is called "de Qi" (pronounced "day-chee") — the sign that the needle has engaged the Qi at that point. Some patients find this sensation pleasant; others find it odd but not painful
  • Multiple points. A typical session uses 6–15 needles, depending on your condition. They're left in place for 15–30 minutes

3. Rest and Stimulation (15–30 minutes)

Once all needles are in, you relax. The lights may be dimmed, soft music may play. Some acupuncturists will gently twist or stimulate the needles after a few minutes. Many patients report feeling deeply relaxed — even drowsy — during this period. It's common to fall asleep.

4. Removal and Follow-Up (5 minutes)

The needles are removed quickly and painlessly. There's usually no bleeding, though a tiny red mark may remain briefly.

Does Acupuncture Hurt?

This is the most common question. The honest answer: it's usually not painful, but "painless" isn't quite the right word either. Most patients describe the sensation as:

  • A brief, light pinch on insertion (a fraction of a second)
  • A dull ache or pressure once the needle is in — not sharp or stabbing
  • A mild tingling or warm feeling around the point

Compare this to getting a vaccine injection: the acupuncture needle is 10–20 times thinner, doesn't inject any liquid, and the sensation is generally much milder. Most people who are nervous about needles find acupuncture surprisingly tolerable.

Are There Side Effects or Risks?

When performed by a licensed, trained practitioner using sterile, single-use needles, acupuncture is extremely safe. Serious complications are rare — estimates suggest the risk of a serious adverse event is less than 1 in 10,000 treatments.

Minor side effects can include:

  • Slight bruising or soreness at needle sites
  • Mild drowsiness after treatment
  • Brief lightheadedness (especially during the first session)

⚠️ When to See a Doctor Instead

Acupuncture is not appropriate for every situation. Seek immediate medical attention if you have:

  • Severe, sudden, or worsening pain
  • Unexplained fever, chills, or signs of infection
  • New numbness, weakness, or loss of function
  • Chest pain, shortness of breath, or stroke-like symptoms
  • A bleeding disorder or are taking blood thinners (consult your doctor first)

If you're pregnant, have a pacemaker, or have a compromised immune system, talk to your healthcare provider before scheduling an acupuncture session.

How to Find a Qualified Acupuncturist

If you're considering acupuncture, quality matters. Look for:

  • Licensing. In the US, look for a licensed acupuncturist (L.Ac.) who has passed the NCCAOM (National Certification Commission for Acupuncture and Oriental Medicine) exam. In the UK, look for membership in the British Acupuncture Council (BAcC). Other countries have their own regulatory bodies
  • Sterile needles. The practitioner should use individually packaged, sterile, single-use needles. This is the standard of care
  • Experience with your condition. Some acupuncturists specialize in pain, others in fertility, anxiety, or digestive issues
  • A clear treatment plan. A good practitioner will explain how many sessions they recommend and what markers of progress to watch for

How Many Sessions Does It Take?

Acupuncture is not a one-and-done treatment. A typical course involves:

  • Initial phase: 1–2 sessions per week for 4–6 weeks
  • Maintenance: Gradually tapering to once every 2–4 weeks once symptoms improve
  • Acute conditions (like a recent injury) may respond faster — sometimes 3–5 sessions
  • Chronic conditions (long-standing pain or patterns) usually need a longer course

Progress may be gradual. Some patients notice improvement after the first session; for others, it takes 3–4 sessions before changes become noticeable. This is normal — your body needs time to adjust.

The Bottom Line

Acupuncture is one of the most research-backed complementary therapies available. For chronic pain especially, the evidence is strong enough that major medical institutions — including the American College of Physicians, the UK's National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), and the US Veterans Health Administration — include it in their clinical guidelines.

It's not magic. It's not a cure for everything. But for many people, it offers something that conventional medicine doesn't always provide: a way to influence the body's own healing mechanisms without medication, surgery, or significant side effects.

If you're dealing with a condition that hasn't responded well to standard treatments — or if you're simply curious about a different approach — acupuncture may be worth exploring. Just go in with realistic expectations, find a qualified practitioner, and keep your primary care doctor informed.
