Cupping Therapy: What It Is, How It Works, and What to Expect

Treatment Guide · 7 min read

If you've ever seen photos of athletes or celebrities with perfectly round, dark purple marks on their backs, you've encountered cupping therapy — even if you weren't sure what it was.

Those marks are the most visible sign of one of the oldest therapeutic techniques in Traditional Chinese Medicine. And despite how they look, they're not bruises in the usual sense. The reaction is intentional, temporary, and part of what makes cupping work.

This guide explains what cupping therapy is, how practitioners use it, what modern science has to say about its effects, and what you should know before trying it yourself.

Think of cupping as creating a "reverse massage." Instead of pressing into the tissue, cupping pulls it upward — creating space, increasing blood flow, and signaling the body to send repair resources to the area.

What Is Cupping Therapy?

Cupping is a therapeutic technique in which cups are placed on the skin to create suction. The practice dates back at least 2,000 years and appears in early medical texts from China, Egypt, and the Middle East. In TCM, it has been a standard treatment for muscle tension, respiratory congestion, and pain for centuries.

The basic mechanism is simple: a vacuum is created inside a cup (traditionally glass or bamboo, now often silicone or medical-grade plastic), which pulls the skin and superficial muscle layer upward into the cup. This creates a stretch on the fascia — the connective tissue that wraps your muscles — and increases blood flow to the area.

Dry Cupping vs. Wet Cupping

There are two main types of cupping used today:

  • Dry cupping — The cup is applied and left in place for 5–15 minutes, or moved across the skin (often called "gliding" or "moving" cupping with oil applied first). This is the most common form used in modern wellness clinics and sports medicine.
  • Wet cupping (hijama) — After a few minutes of dry cupping, the cup is removed, small superficial incisions are made on the skin, and the cup is reapplied to draw out a small amount of blood. This is less common in Western settings and is typically performed by practitioners trained specifically in the technique.

Dry cupping is what most people encounter when they try cupping for the first time. The round marks are the result of blood vessels in the skin being stretched by the suction force — not bleeding or tissue damage.

How Does Cupping Work? (TCM and Modern Views)

Like many TCM techniques, cupping has both a traditional explanation and a growing body of modern research that helps explain its effects in physiological terms.

The TCM Explanation

In TCM theory, pain and stiffness are often caused by Qi and Blood stagnation — a backup in the body's energy and circulatory systems. Think of a garden hose with a kink in it: water can't flow past the bend, and whatever lies beyond doesn't get the water it needs. Cupping works by creating a strong local suction that pulls stagnation to the surface, allowing fresh Qi and Blood to flow into the area. The practitioner often observes the color of the marks afterward — darker marks are interpreted as deeper or longer-standing stagnation.

Cupping is also used to release "Wind-Cold" or "Wind-Damp" — TCM terms for environmental factors that can invade the body's surface and cause muscle aches, joint stiffness, and feelings of heaviness. By drawing these factors to the surface, cupping helps the body expel them more quickly.

What Modern Science Says

Research into cupping has grown significantly in the last decade, particularly around its effects on pain and inflammation. Several proposed mechanisms have emerged:

  • Fascial decompression — The suction lifts and separates layers of fascia (the connective tissue that surrounds muscles). In conditions like chronic back pain or tight shoulders, this mechanical release can reduce tension and improve range of motion. A 2020 review in Sports Medicine found that cupping therapy may produce short-term improvements in pain and function, particularly for musculoskeletal conditions.
  • Increased blood flow — The suction draws blood into the area, which can accelerate the delivery of oxygen and nutrients while flushing out metabolic waste products like lactic acid. This is similar to why you apply heat to a sore muscle.
  • Pain-gate theory — The strong sensory input from the suction may "compete" with pain signals at the spinal cord level, reducing the perception of pain much in the way that rubbing a bumped elbow helps (the pain-gate theory, which is well-established in pain science).
  • Immune modulation — Some recent studies (including a 2021 paper in Scientific Reports) suggest that cupping may trigger a localized immune response. The micro-stretch on the skin signals immune cells to the area, which may help resolve chronic inflammation.

It's important to note that while the research is promising, many studies are small, and the quality of evidence varies. Cupping appears most effective for pain-related conditions, but more large-scale trials are needed.

What Does a Cupping Session Look Like?

If you're considering trying cupping, here's what a typical session involves:

  1. Assessment — The practitioner will ask about your symptoms and examine the area to be treated. They'll explain what type of cupping they recommend and what to expect.
  2. Application — Cups are placed on the skin. With traditional glass cups, the practitioner uses a flame to briefly heat the air inside the cup before placing it on the skin — the cooling air creates the vacuum. With modern pump cups, a handheld pump is used to create the suction, so no heat is involved.
  3. The sensation — Most people describe it as a tight, pulling sensation. It's not painful, but it can feel intense — like a deep tissue massage being held in one spot. The intensity depends on how much suction is applied. If it's too strong, tell your practitioner; they can reduce it.
  4. Duration — Cups are typically left in place for 5–15 minutes. For gliding cupping, oil is applied and the cups are moved across the skin in a massage-like motion.
  5. Aftercare — The cups are removed, and the practitioner may apply a soothing oil or balm. You'll be advised to drink plenty of water, avoid extreme temperatures, and rest the area for the next 24 hours.

What About the Marks?

The circular marks left by cupping are the feature that gets the most attention — and the most misunderstanding. Here's what to know:

  • They're not bruises. A bruise occurs when blood vessels rupture from blunt trauma (you bumped into something). Cupping marks are caused by blood vessels stretching and filling with blood under the controlled vacuum, not from rupture. This is why they're typically painless to the touch — unlike a real bruise.
  • They vary in color. Marks can range from light pink to deep purple depending on the suction level, the patient's circulation, and how much stagnation was present. In TCM, darker marks are read diagnostically, though there's no Western medical consensus on this.
  • They fade over a few days to a week. Unlike bruises that go through a yellow-green-brown cycle as hemoglobin breaks down, cupping marks typically fade more evenly and quickly.

If you're concerned about how the marks will look (and whether they'll be visible), talk to your practitioner before the session. They can adjust the suction strength or choose a location that's easier to cover.

Important safety note: Practitioners should use sterilized equipment and single-use cups whenever possible. Never reuse cups without proper sterilization between patients. Always check your practitioner's hygiene protocols before booking.

Which Conditions Is Cupping Used For?

Cupping is most commonly used for musculoskeletal issues, but practitioners apply it for a broader range of conditions:

Muscle pain and tension

Back pain, neck and shoulder stiffness, sports recovery, and fibromyalgia. This is the most common reason people seek cupping.

Respiratory congestion

Coughs, colds, bronchitis, and asthma. Cupping on the upper back is traditionally used to help loosen phlegm and open the lungs.

Digestive discomfort

Bloating, abdominal tension, and sluggish digestion. Cupping on the abdomen may help stimulate digestive motility in TCM theory.

Headaches and tension

Tension headaches and cervicogenic headaches (originating from the neck). Cupping on the shoulders and upper back may help reduce referred tension patterns.

Safety, Side Effects, and When to Avoid Cupping

Cupping is generally safe when performed by a trained, licensed practitioner using clean equipment. Minor side effects can include:

  • Temporary marks (as discussed above)
  • Mild soreness or tenderness in the treated area for 1–2 days
  • Lightheadedness (rare, and usually related to the relaxation response)

However, cupping is not appropriate for everyone. You should avoid cupping or speak to your doctor first if any of the following apply to you:

  • You have a bleeding disorder or take blood-thinning medication (including warfarin, apixaban, or high-dose aspirin)
  • You have a skin condition in the area to be treated (eczema, psoriasis, open wounds, sunburn)
  • You are pregnant (cupping on the abdomen and lower back is generally avoided during pregnancy)
  • You have a fever or acute infection
  • You have a history of deep vein thrombosis or blood clots
  • You have fragile skin or take corticosteroids long-term (skin may tear)
⚠️ When to see a doctor: Cupping is a complementary therapy. If you have persistent or worsening pain, unexplained symptoms, or a condition that hasn't been diagnosed by a medical doctor, seek a medical evaluation before pursuing cupping. Cupping is not a replacement for emergency or primary care.

The Bottom Line

Cupping therapy has been used for thousands of years, and its popularity has grown substantially in the West — thanks in part to visibility in sports medicine and celebrity endorsements. While the evidence base is still developing, the available research supports its use primarily for musculoskeletal pain, tension, and recovery.

The marks are the most visible signal of what's happening beneath the surface: increased blood flow, fascial release, and a shift in how the body is managing that area of tension or pain. For many people, cupping offers a different kind of relief than massage or stretching alone — especially for stubborn knots, chronic tightness, and post-exercise recovery.

If you're considering cupping, find a qualified practitioner, ask about their training and hygiene protocols, and go in with realistic expectations. It's not a magic bullet, but for the right person with the right condition, it can be a surprisingly effective tool.
