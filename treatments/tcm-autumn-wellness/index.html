Autumn Wellness in TCM: Nourishing Your Lungs for the Dry Season

Treatment Guide · 7 min read

Why Your Body Feels Different When the Air Turns Cool

You notice it before you can name it. The air feels thinner, crisper. Your skin feels tighter after a shower. Your lips feel drier by midday. That tickle in your throat that wasn't there last week.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), these changes aren't random — they signal a seasonal shift that directly affects a specific organ system: your Lungs.

Think of your Lungs as the most delicate membrane in your body — the interface between you and the outside world. Every breath brings the external environment into direct contact with your internal tissues. In TCM theory, autumn's dryness (Dryness as a pathogenic factor) specifically targets this interface, like a dry wind stripping moisture from exposed soil.

The Lung-Autumn Connection: More Than Breathing

In TCM's Five Element framework, autumn corresponds to the Metal element, and Metal governs the Lung and Large Intestine organ systems. This isn't abstract philosophy — it describes a predictable seasonal pattern:

  • Autumn energy moves inward and downward — just as trees pull energy from leaves into roots, your body naturally shifts from outward summer activity toward internal conservation
  • Dryness becomes the dominant environmental factor — the same dryness that cracks autumn leaves can deplete your body's fluids, particularly in the Lungs and respiratory tract
  • Wei Qi (Defensive Qi) gets tested — your immune system faces the transition from warm to cold, making you more vulnerable to seasonal bugs

In TCM, the Lungs govern Qi and respiration — but they also govern the skin, body hair, and the defensive barrier (Wei Qi) that protects you from external pathogens. When autumn dryness weakens Lung Yin (the moistening, cooling aspect of Lung function), you may notice not just a dry cough, but also dry skin, a tight chest, low-grade anxiety, or difficulty letting go of things.

The TCM saying goes: "The Lungs are like a delicate canopy" — they sit at the top of the body and get exposed first. Autumn is the season to protect that canopy.

Your Seasonal Wellness Checklist

1. Eat for Moisture and Warmth

As temperatures drop, your body craves foods that are warming, moistening, and easy to digest. TCM dietary therapy for autumn focuses on building Lung Yin and protecting digestive fire:

Foods to Prioritize

Moistening foods: Pears (the quintessential autumn fruit), apples, almonds, sesame seeds, honey, mushrooms, tofu, eggs, milk, and bone broth — these gently build body fluids without creating dampness

Warming foods: Cooked root vegetables (sweet potatoes, carrots, squash), oats, rice congee (congee/jook), ginger, cinnamon, and small amounts of lamb or chicken — these support digestive warmth as the weather cools

Sour foods (in moderation): Lemon, vinegar, sauerkraut, sour plums — the sour flavor, traditionally said to astringe and consolidate, can help hold fluids in the body during autumn's drying phase

Foods to Reduce

Raw and cold foods: Salads, iced drinks, raw vegetables — these require more digestive energy to process, energy that's already being redirected inward

Spicy and drying foods: Excess chili, garlic, and alcohol — these further deplete body fluids in an already dry season

2. Acupressure Points for Autumn Lung Health

These three points can be stimulated daily during autumn to support Lung function and respiratory resilience:

LU7 (Lieque) — Lung Meridian Master Point

Location: On the thumb side of your forearm, about 1.5 thumb-widths above the wrist crease, in the shallow groove between the two tendons

Traditional use: LU7 is the Luo-connecting point of the Lung meridian and one of the most commonly used points for respiratory support. It's traditionally used for coughs, sore throats, and head and neck tension — all common autumn complaints.

How to stimulate: Press firmly with your thumb for 1-2 minutes while breathing deeply. Repeat on both arms.

LU9 (Taiyuan) — Lung Yuan-Source Point

Location: At the wrist crease on the thumb side, just below the base of the thumb, where you can feel the radial artery pulse

Traditional use: LU9 is the Source point of the Lung meridian — in TCM theory, Source points are where the organ's original Qi pools. It's traditionally used to tonify Lung Qi and nourish Lung Yin, making it ideal for the dry season.

How to stimulate: Use your thumb to press gently yet firmly into the small hollow at the wrist crease. Hold for 1 minute per side.

ST36 (Zusanli) — Stomach Meridian, General Qi Builder

Location: Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width toward the outer edge of the shin bone

Traditional use: While not directly on the Lung meridian, ST36 is the most famous point in TCM for building overall Qi and strengthening the immune system (Wei Qi). Supporting ST36 in autumn helps the body maintain robust defenses against seasonal pathogens.

How to stimulate: Press firmly with your thumb or knuckle. A slight dull ache is normal. Alternate legs, 1-2 minutes each side.

3. Breathing Practices for the Dry Season

Autumn is the natural time to focus on the breath. Here are two simple practices:

The Six-Second Breath: Inhale through your nose for 3 counts, exhale through pursed lips for 3 counts. Do this for 3-5 minutes in the morning before you get out of bed. This gently exercises the Lung system without straining dry airways.

The "Ssss" Lung Sound (Liu Zi Jue): TCM's Six Healing Sounds include a specific exhalation for the Lungs — making a long, soft "Ssssss" sound as you exhale fully. This is traditionally believed to release stagnant heat from the Lung system. Try it 6 times after your evening shower.

4. Lifestyle Adjustments for Autumn

TCM's seasonal lifestyle guidance is remarkably practical:

  • Go to bed a little earlier, wake a little later — align with the shortening daylight. Even 30 minutes earlier can make a difference
  • Dress for the neck and chest — in TCM, the "Wind Gate" (the nape of the neck, where GB20 gall bladder point sits) is especially vulnerable to wind and cold invasion. A scarf is your best autumn accessory
  • Movement, not exertion — autumn favors moderate exercise (walking, yoga, tai chi, qigong) rather than high-intensity sweating. Heavy sweat in dry weather further depletes body fluids
  • Practice "letting go" — the emotion associated with the Lungs in TCM is grief, and autumn is traditionally seen as a season of release. This can be as literal as decluttering your home or as subtle as journaling what no longer serves you

What the Research Says

While much of TCM's seasonal framework is traditional, some areas are supported by modern research:

  • Seasonal eating patterns — A 2021 review in Nutrients found that eating seasonally aligns with circadian biology and may support metabolic health through natural variations in phytochemical intake
  • Acupressure for respiratory symptoms — A 2018 systematic review in Medicine found that acupressure at specific points (including LU7 and ST36) may help reduce cough frequency and improve quality of life in people with chronic respiratory conditions
  • Wei Qi and the immune system — A 2020 paper in Frontiers in Immunology noted that certain TCM herbal formulas traditionally used to strengthen Wei Qi (such as Yu Ping Feng San or "Jade Windscreen") have demonstrated immunomodulatory effects in clinical trials

These findings are preliminary and should not replace standard medical care. They suggest, however, that TCM's seasonal recommendations have a rational basis worth further study.

When to See a Doctor

Seasonal wellness is about supporting your body through predictable changes. But some symptoms require medical attention:

  • A cough lasting more than 3 weeks
  • Shortness of breath or wheezing
  • Fever above 101°F (38.3°C)
  • Chest pain when breathing
  • Coughing up blood
  • Unexplained weight loss

A note on seasonal allergies: Autumn allergies (ragweed, mold spores) affect millions of people. If your "autumn dryness" symptoms are accompanied by sneezing, itchy eyes, or a runny nose, you may be dealing with allergic rhinitis rather than simple seasonal dryness. TCM has approaches for both — but an accurate diagnosis is the first step. See our Respiratory Health Guide for more.

Practical Takeaways

Your 3-Step Autumn Wellness Plan:

  1. Eat warm and moist. Swap salads for cooked root vegetables. Add a sliced pear to your morning oatmeal. Drink warm water with lemon instead of iced beverages.
  2. Stimulate LU7 and ST36 daily. Two minutes on each point, each side. Do it while waiting for your morning coffee to brew.
  3. Protect your neck. Wear a scarf on cool mornings. It's a small habit with an outsized effect in TCM thinking — preventing wind and cold from entering the body's defensive layer.

The Bottom Line

Autumn in TCM is not a season to fear — it's a season to align with. The same forces that turn leaves gold and pull sap downward are working through your body. By eating warm, moistening foods, supporting your Lungs with acupressure and breathwork, and adjusting your daily rhythm, you can move through autumn with resilience rather than catching every cold that comes your way.

Your body knows the seasons. TCM just gives you a language to understand what it's already telling you.
