Acupressure for Digestion: 5 Points to Relieve Bloating, Gas & Indigestion

Treatment Guide · 7 min read

You eat a balanced meal — vegetables, lean protein, whole grains. But within an hour, your stomach feels like a balloon. You're bloated, gassy, and that comfortable fullness has turned into uncomfortable pressure.

Sound familiar? You reach for an antacid or a digestive enzyme, and maybe it helps — temporarily. But the next meal brings the same cycle.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), digestive discomfort isn't just about what you eat. It's about whether your body can transform food into energy efficiently. And one of the simplest ways to support that process requires nothing more than your own fingertips.

Acupressure — a non-invasive technique based on the same meridian system as acupuncture — uses targeted pressure on specific points to unlock stuck energy and support digestive function. Here are the five most effective points for bloating, gas, and indigestion, based on centuries of traditional use and growing research interest.

Why TCM Views Digestion Differently

In TCM, the Spleen-Stomach system is your body's energy factory. Think of it like this:

  • The Stomach acts as the receiving chamber — it breaks down food into a kind of "ingredient mash" (called Gu Qi, or Food Qi)
  • The Spleen is the power plant — it transforms that mash into usable Qi and Blood that every organ and tissue depends on
  • The Liver is the traffic controller — it regulates the smooth flow of Qi through the digestive system

When Spleen Qi is weak — a pattern TCM calls Spleen Qi Deficiency — it's like a laptop plugged in but not charging. Food goes in, but the conversion to energy doesn't happen efficiently. The result? Bloating, sluggish digestion, brain fog, and lingering fullness after meals.

And when Liver Qi is stagnant — think a garden hose with a kink — it squeezes the digestive system, causing gas, cramping, and irregular digestion. Acupressure works by releasing these kinks and restoring the smooth flow of energy through the digestive tract.

5 Acupressure Points for Better Digestion

Each of these points has a specific role in supporting digestion. You can work through all five in a 10-minute session, or focus on the points that feel most tender — sensitivity often indicates where your body needs the most attention.

1. ST36 — Leg Three Miles (足三里, Zúsānlǐ)

Location: Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width outward from the shin bone (tibia), on the outside of the leg. You'll feel a small depression between the tibialis anterior and the shin.

Why it works: ST36 is the single most important point in TCM for digestive health. It's the primary point for strengthening the Spleen and Stomach, and it's traditionally used for everything from bloating and poor appetite to low energy after meals. Research has shown that stimulating ST36 may influence gastric motility and vagal nerve activity — offering a possible mechanism for its digestive benefits. Acupressure at ST36 is also well-tolerated, making it an accessible option for daily self-care.

How to press: Use your thumb to press firmly — you should feel a dull, aching sensation, not sharp pain. Hold for 30 seconds, then use small, circular kneading motions for 1-2 minutes. Repeat on the other leg.

2. CV12 — Central Duct (中脘, Zhōngwǎn)

Location: On the midline of your abdomen, halfway between the bottom of your sternum (breastbone) and your belly button. It's directly over the stomach area.

Why it works: CV12 is the Front-Mu (alarm) point of the Stomach — a point known in TCM for directly influencing the Stomach organ. When you feel bloated, heavy, or full after eating, CV12 is typically the first point to press. It's traditionally used to support healthy downward movement of Qi through the digestive tract — something that naturally slows when you're bloated or backed up.

How to press: Lie down if possible. Place two fingers (index and middle) on the point, then press gently but firmly with your fingertips. Use small, slow circular movements for 2-3 minutes. Breathe deeply. You may feel warmth or a gurgling sensation — that's the Qi starting to move.

3. ST25 — Celestial Pivot (天枢, Tiānshū)

Location: Two finger-widths to the left and right of your belly button. You have two ST25 points, one on each side.

Why it works: ST25 is the Front-Mu point of the Large Intestine, making it the go-to point for lower abdominal bloating, gas, and irregular bowel movements. In TCM theory, this point helps regulate the function of the intestines — encouraging healthy motility and the smooth passage of Qi through the lower digestive tract.

How to press: Press both sides simultaneously using your index and middle fingers — one hand on each side. Use firm, circular pressure for 1-2 minutes. You may notice an urge to pass gas — that's a good sign. Your body is moving trapped Qi.

4. CV6 — Sea of Qi (气海, Qìhǎi)

Location: On the midline, about 1.5 finger-widths below your belly button. (Some versions use the name "Gate of the Original Qi" or Guānyuán CV4 for a slightly lower point — CV6 sits just below the navel and is more directly associated with supporting digestive Qi transformation.)

Why it works: CV6 is traditionally used to tonify Qi — to strengthen the body's fundamental energy in the lower abdomen. For digestion, this point is valuable when bloating comes with signs of digestive weakness: fatigue after meals, poor appetite, or loose stools. By warming and strengthening the lower abdomen, CV6 supports the Spleen's ability to transform food into energy.

How to press: Lie down and find the point. Use the palm of one hand (not just fingers) to apply gentle, sustained pressure — the palm covers a broader area and delivers a warming, grounding sensation. Hold for 2-3 minutes while breathing deeply. You can also rub your palms together first to warm them before pressing.

5. PC6 — Inner Gate (内关, Nèiguān)

Location: On the inner forearm, about three finger-widths below the wrist crease, between the two visible tendons. This is the same point used for motion sickness and nausea.

Why it works: PC6 is the Pericardium channel's Luo (connecting) point, and it's traditionally used for nausea, reflux, and the kind of upper digestive discomfort that feels like food "sitting" in your chest. It also has a calming effect — important because stress and tension directly impact digestion through the gut-brain axis. When Liver Qi is stagnant and squeezing the digestive system, PC6 helps release that tension.

How to press: Use your opposite thumb to press into the groove between the two tendons. Apply firm pressure while taking slow, deep breaths. Hold for 30 seconds, then use small circular motions for 1-2 minutes. Repeat on the other arm.

How to Build a Daily Acupressure Routine

For best results, consistency matters more than intensity. Here's a simple routine you can incorporate into your day:

Time Points to Focus On Duration
Morning (before breakfast) ST36 (both legs) + CV6 3-5 minutes
30 min after a heavy meal CV12 + ST25 (both sides) 3-5 minutes
When feeling nauseous or stressed PC6 (both arms) 2-3 minutes
Evening (before bed) CV6 (warm palm) + ST36 5 minutes
A note on technique: Acupressure should never be painful. If a point feels sharp or bruising, ease off the pressure. The goal is a "de qi" sensation — a dull ache, slight warmth, or a feeling of energy moving. Avoid pressing directly on surgical scars, varicose veins, or inflamed areas. Stop if you feel dizzy or nauseous.

What the Research Says

While acupressure is rooted in a 2,000-year-old medical system, modern research has begun investigating its effects on digestion:

  • Gastric motility: A 2021 systematic review in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine found that acupoint stimulation (including acupressure) may increase gastric motility and accelerate gastric emptying — relevant for bloating, early satiety, and functional dyspepsia.
  • Nausea and vomiting: PC6 is one of the most extensively studied acupoints. The Cochrane Review (2015, 44 RCTs, 6,500+ participants) concluded that PC6 stimulation reduces postoperative nausea and may be comparable to antiemetic medications. Many integrative medicine programs now offer PC6 wristbands for pregnancy-related nausea.
  • Functional dyspepsia: A 2020 study in Medicine reported that acupressure at ST36, CV12, and ST25 over 4 weeks significantly improved symptoms of functional dyspepsia — including postprandial fullness, early satiety, and epigastric pain — compared to a sham control group.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS): Research published in Digestive Diseases and Sciences (2018) found that self-administered acupressure for 6 weeks reduced IBS symptoms and improved quality of life, with particular benefit for abdominal pain and bloating.
Important: These studies suggest potential benefits, but the overall evidence base is still developing. Acupressure is best used as a complementary practice alongside professional medical care — not as a replacement for diagnosed digestive conditions.

When to See a Medical Doctor

Occasional bloating and digestive discomfort is common. But certain signs warrant professional evaluation:

  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Blood in your stool or black, tarry stools
  • Severe or worsening abdominal pain that doesn't resolve
  • Persistent vomiting or inability to keep food down
  • New onset of digestive symptoms after age 50
  • Difficulty swallowing or feeling like food gets stuck in your throat

If you have any of these symptoms, please consult a healthcare provider before trying any self-care approach.

Practical Takeaways

  • Start with ST36: Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width outward from the shin. Press firmly for 1-2 minutes on each leg — this is the most important point for overall digestive support.
  • For bloating after meals: Press CV12 (midline between sternum and belly button) and ST25 (two finger-widths to each side of the belly button) with gentle circular motions.
  • For nausea or stress-related digestive upset: PC6 on the inner forearm, three finger-widths below the wrist crease between the tendons.
  • Consistency over intensity: A daily 5-minute session is more effective than occasional longer sessions.
  • Combine with diet and lifestyle: Acupressure works best as one part of a broader digestive support strategy. Warm, cooked meals, eating without distractions, and managing stress all play important roles in TCM digestion.
