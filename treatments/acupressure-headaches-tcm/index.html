Acupressure for Headaches: Pressure Points for Tension, Migraine & Sinus Relief

Treatment Guide · 7 min read

You know the feeling. The slow creep of pressure behind your eyes. The band tightening around your skull. The pulsing at your temples that makes you want to close the blinds and disappear.

Headaches are one of the most common health complaints worldwide — and one of the most frustrating. You reach for painkillers, they work for a few hours, and then the pain creeps back. Or maybe they don't work at all, leaving you waiting out the clock until the headache decides to release its grip on its own schedule.

But what if you had a tool you could use anywhere, at any time, with zero side effects and no prescription? A tool that works with your body's own pain-relief systems rather than overriding them with chemistry? That's exactly what acupressure offers for headaches — and the tool is simply your own hands.

Think of acupressure as your body's built-in reset buttons. Each point is like a keyboard shortcut — press the right one, and your body responds with a predictable effect. You don't need to understand how the computer works to use Ctrl+Z, and you don't need to become a TCM expert to stop a tension headache at your desk.

Why Acupressure Works for Headaches: A TCM Lens

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, most headaches are understood as a blockage of Qi (vital energy) and Blood moving through the channels that cross the head and neck. In TCM theory, the smooth flow of Qi and Blood through these channels is essential for health — and when that flow gets disrupted, stagnation builds up, pressure accumulates, and pain follows.

Think of it like a garden hose with a kink in it. Water builds up behind the kink, pressure increases, and the hose stiffens. The water (your Qi and Blood) hasn't run out — it's just stuck. Acupressure is essentially finding the kink and releasing it so everything can flow again.

Several major meridian pathways cross through the head, neck, and shoulders. Each carries Qi from a different organ system, which is why TCM looks at where your headache is located — temples, back of the head, forehead, or behind the eyes — as a clue to which channel is affected. This is why choosing the right acupressure point matters, and why a one-size-fits-all approach to headaches often falls short.

The Top 5 Acupressure Points for Headache Relief

Each of these points targets a different headache mechanism. For the best results, learn all five — then use the one (or combination) that matches your headache pattern.

1. LI4 (Hegu) — The Master Pain Point

Location: In the webbing between your thumb and index finger. Squeeze the fleshy V-shaped area from above and below with your opposite thumb and index finger. The point is at the highest spot of the muscle when the thumb and index finger are brought together.

Why it works for headaches: LI4 is one of the most powerful points on the body for moving Qi and Blood. In TCM theory, the Large Intestine meridian connects to the face and head, making LI4 effective for frontal headaches, sinus pressure, and facial pain. It's traditionally considered a "command point" for the head and face.

How to apply: Press firmly into the fleshy web for 30–60 seconds while breathing slowly. You may feel a dull ache or radiating sensation. Alternate hands. ⚠️ Avoid during pregnancy — LI4 may stimulate uterine contractions.

2. GB20 (Fengchi) — The Tension Headache Release

Location: In the hollow at the base of your skull, between the two large vertical neck muscles. Run your fingers up from the back of your neck until you feel the skull bone — then slide outward into the natural depressions just below the skull ridge.

Why it works for headaches: GB20 is the go-to point for tension headaches that start in the neck and shoulders and radiate upward. The Gallbladder meridian runs along the sides of the head and neck, and this point sits at a major junction where tension tends to accumulate. It's also traditionally used for sinus congestion, eye strain, and headaches triggered by wind or weather changes.

How to apply: Use both thumbs simultaneously to press upward and inward into the hollows at the base of your skull. Apply steady pressure for 1–2 minutes while tilting your head back slightly into your thumbs. You'll likely feel a satisfying release of tension.

A 2017 study in Autonomic Neuroscience found that pressure at points on the neck and upper back (including the GB20 region) significantly reduced stress markers and muscle tension in healthy volunteers.

3. Taiyang (EX-HN5) — The Temple Point

Location: In the hollow at your temples, about one finger-width from the outer edge of your eyebrow. You'll feel a small depression when you press there.

Why it works for headaches: The temple region is where the Gallbladder and San Jiao meridians cross, making Taiyang an effective point for headache pain focused at the sides of the head. It's particularly useful for stress headaches and migraines where you feel throbbing or pressure at the temples. In TCM theory, this point helps sedate rising Liver Yang — a common pattern in tension and migraine headaches where energy rises excessively to the head.

How to apply: Use your middle or index fingers to apply gentle, circular pressure for 1–2 minutes on both temples simultaneously. Start lightly and increase pressure gradually. Many people find that paired with deep breathing, this brings noticeable relief within a minute.

4. LV3 (Taichong) — The Stress-Induced Headache Point

Location: On the top of your foot, in the hollow between the big toe and second toe, about two finger-widths from the web. Slide your finger from the webbing up toward the ankle — it settles into a natural depression.

Why it works for headaches: LV3 is the main point for soothing the Liver system in TCM theory. The Liver meridian is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body — and when stress, frustration, or suppressed emotions disrupt that flow, Qi can stagnate and rise upward, causing headaches. LV3 helps "drain" that excess energy downward. If you notice your headaches are worse during stressful periods, or if you tend to clench your jaw and shrug your shoulders when tense, LV3 is likely your most useful point.

How to apply: Press firmly into the hollow with your thumb for 30–60 seconds. Many people find this point tender — that tenderness often decreases with regular use. Repeat on both feet.

5. GB21 (Jianjing) — The Shoulder-Tension Connection

Location: At the highest point of your shoulder, midway between the base of your neck and the tip of your shoulder. Pinch your shoulder muscle and you'll feel a tender spot about halfway along the curve.

Why it works for headaches: Shoulder tension and headaches are tightly linked — and GB21 sits right at the epicenter of that connection. The Gallbladder meridian runs through the shoulders and up the sides of the neck to the head. When your shoulders are tight (often without you even noticing), the upward flow of Qi through the neck is restricted, creating the conditions for a tension headache. Releasing GB21 can break this cycle at its physical source.

How to apply: Use your opposite thumb to press firmly into the highest point of your shoulder. Hold for 30–60 seconds while taking deep breaths. You can also apply circular kneading motions. ⚠️ Avoid during pregnancy — GB21 may stimulate uterine contractions.

Acupressure Protocols by Headache Type

Different headaches respond to different point combinations. Here are specific protocols for the three most common headache types:

Tension Headaches — The Slow Build

Tension headaches are the most common type. They feel like a dull, aching pressure — often described as a tight band around the head — and are frequently linked to stress, poor posture, or hours spent hunched over a screen.

Protocol:

  1. Start with GB21 — Release the shoulder tension that's likely pulling on your neck. 1 minute per side.
  2. Move to GB20 — The base of your skull is where tension accumulates. Press both sides for 1–2 minutes.
  3. Finish with Taiyang — Gentle circular pressure at the temples for 1 minute. Breathe deeply throughout.

This sequence takes about 5 minutes and can be done at your desk. The key is to move from the body toward the head — releasing the physical source of tension before addressing the head pain directly.

Migraine Headaches — The Throbbing Wave

Migraines involve more complex mechanisms than tension headaches — they typically include throbbing pain (often on one side), sensitivity to light and sound, and sometimes nausea or visual disturbances. In TCM theory, migraines often involve patterns like Liver Yang Rising or Liver Fire Blazing, where energy surges upward uncontrollably.

Protocol:

  1. Start with LV3 — This is the most important point for migraines from a TCM perspective. Pressing LV3 "draws" excess energy downward, counteracting the upward surge. Hold for 1–2 minutes on each foot.
  2. Add GB20 — The base-of-skull release helps calm the nervous system. 1–2 minutes.
  3. Finish with LI4 — As a general pain-modulating point. 30–60 seconds per hand.

For migraine prevention with known triggers (such as hormonal shifts or weather changes), some studies suggest regular acupressure may help reduce frequency. A 2021 review in Complementary Therapies in Clinical Practice noted that acupressure was associated with reduced headache frequency and intensity in migraine patients when practiced consistently over several weeks.

Sinus Headaches — The Pressure Behind Your Face

Sinus headaches feel like deep, dull pressure behind your forehead, eyes, and cheekbones — often accompanied by nasal congestion. In TCM theory, these headaches involve Dampness and Phlegm obstructing the channels in the head, often related to the Spleen and Lung systems.

Protocol:

  1. Start with LI4 — The master point for facial and sinus congestion. 1 minute per hand.
  2. Add GB20 — Helps clear the head and relieve sinus pressure. 1–2 minutes.
  3. Add facial points — Use your index fingers to apply gentle pressure at the inner corners of your eyebrows (BL2, or Cuanzhu) and the indents on either side of your nostrils (LI20, or Yingxiang). 20–30 seconds each.
A practical note on timing: For acute headaches, apply acupressure as soon as you feel the pain starting. The earlier you catch it, the more effective the points tend to be. For chronic or recurring headaches, daily practice of even 5 minutes can help reduce frequency over time. Consistency matters more than intensity.

Research and What Studies Suggest

The research on acupressure specifically for headaches is growing, though still less extensive than acupuncture research. Here's what current evidence suggests:

  • Pain-modulating effects: A 2020 systematic review in BMJ Open (13 RCTs, 1,090 participants) found that acupoint stimulation — including acupressure — may improve pain outcomes for several headache types. The proposed mechanisms include endorphin release, modulation of pain-gating pathways, and activation of the descending pain-inhibitory system.
  • Reduced headache frequency: A 2021 review in Complementary Therapies in Clinical Practice noted that consistent acupressure practice (over 4–8 weeks) was associated with reduced headache frequency and intensity, particularly for tension-type headaches and migraines.
  • Muscle tension release: Many acupressure points overlap with what Western medicine calls myofascial trigger points — tender knots in muscle tissue that can refer pain to the head. Sustained pressure on these points is a well-established physiotherapy technique called ischemic compression, which can release muscle spasms and break the tension-pain cycle.
  • Parasympathetic activation: A 2017 study in Autonomic Neuroscience found that firm pressure on specific acupoints can shift the nervous system from sympathetic (fight-or-flight) to parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) dominance, reducing the stress response that often triggers or worsens headaches.

It's worth noting that while these findings are encouraging, the research is preliminary in many areas. Acupressure is best used as a complementary self-care tool — not as a replacement for professional medical care.

When to Use Acupressure vs. When to See a Professional

Acupressure is most effective for common headache types — tension headaches, mild migraines, and sinus-related pressure. But not all headaches are the same, and some require medical attention.

Acupressure is a good first response when:

  • You feel a familiar tension headache building after a long day at your desk
  • You recognize the onset of your typical migraine pattern and want to reduce its intensity
  • You have mild sinus pressure with nasal congestion
  • Your headaches are triggered by known factors (stress, poor posture, skipped meals) and follow a predictable pattern

⚠️ When to See a Doctor Instead

Acupressure may help with common headaches, but it is not appropriate for serious or undiagnosed conditions. Seek medical attention if you experience:

  • A sudden, severe headache — especially if it's the "worst headache of your life" (thunderclap headache)
  • Headache accompanied by fever, stiff neck, confusion, or seizure
  • Headache after a head injury or fall
  • New-onset headache after age 50
  • Headache with vision changes, slurred speech, or weakness on one side of the body
  • Persistent headache that doesn't respond to any self-care measure and lasts more than a few days

If you are pregnant, have a pacemaker, or have a chronic medical condition, talk to your healthcare provider before practicing acupressure routinely.

Practical Takeaways

Your 5-Minute Headache Relief Routine

  1. Identify your headache type and location — Use the location table above to choose your primary point
  2. Apply steady pressure — Use your thumb or index finger, pressing slowly and firmly until you feel a dull ache or warmth (not sharp pain)
  3. Breathe deeply — Inhale for 4 counts, exhale for 6 counts. Each exhale, sink a little deeper into the pressure
  4. Hold each point 30–60 seconds — Or longer if you feel the point releasing (the sensation may change from "tight" to "open")
  5. Work in sequence — For tension headaches: GB21 → GB20 → Taiyang. For migraines: LV3 → GB20 → LI4

Acupressure won't solve every headache problem, and it shouldn't replace medical care when you need it. But as a tool for managing the everyday headaches that stress, posture, and life throw at you, it's remarkably effective — and it's always with you.
