Acupressure for Fatigue: Points to Boost Energy and Restore Vitality

Treatment Guide · 8 min read

You wake up already tired. The coffee gets you through the morning, but by 2 PM your brain is foggy and your body feels like it's moving through wet cement. By evening, you're too exhausted to do anything — but not relaxed enough to sleep.

If this sounds familiar, you're not lazy. You're not broken. And the problem may not be that you need more sleep — it's that your body's energy system isn't converting rest into usable vitality the way it should.

Acupressure offers a different approach. Instead of stimulating your nervous system with caffeine (which borrows energy from tomorrow), acupressure works with your body's natural energy pathways — the same channels used in acupuncture — to restore flow where it's stuck and top up reserves where they're running low.

Here's how to match the right points to your specific type of fatigue.

The TCM View of Fatigue: Four Different Kinds of "Tired"

In TCM, fatigue isn't one problem — it's a signal that something in your energy system isn't working efficiently. The same symptom ("I'm tired") can stem from four completely different root causes, and the right acupressure points depend on which one fits your experience:

1. Spleen Qi Deficiency — "The Battery Isn't Charging"

You might have this if: You feel most tired after eating. Your digestion is sluggish — bloating, loose stools, heavy meals sit like a stone. You crave sugar or carbs for quick energy, but the crash comes fast. Your mind feels foggy, especially after lunch.

TCM view: Your Spleen system (the body's food processor and power plant rolled into one) isn't converting food into usable Qi efficiently. The fuel goes in, but the energy doesn't come out. Like a phone plugged into a faulty charger — the light says "charging" but the battery stays flat.

Key acupressure target: Build digestive Qi and transform food into energy more efficiently.

2. Kidney Qi Deficiency — "The Battery Bank Is Running Low"

You might have this if: Your fatigue feels deep — like your bones are tired, not just your muscles. You have lower back ache or weak knees. You feel colder than others, especially in your hands and feet. You wake up frequently during the night, or your fatigue was triggered by overwork, chronic stress, or aging.

TCM view: Your Kidney system is the body's battery bank — you're born with a certain charge (Jing/essence), and every day draws from it. When Kidney Qi is deficient, the entire energy system runs on a depleted reserve. No amount of surface-level stimulation (caffeine, sugar) can fix a drained battery.

Key acupressure target: Tonify the deep energy reserves and support the body's constitutional foundation.

3. Qi Stagnation Fatigue — "The Energy Is There, But Stuck"

You might have this if: You feel a different kind of tired — wired but exhausted. Your shoulders are tight, your jaw is clamped, and stress is a constant companion. You have energy bursts (anxiety-driven) followed by crashes. Exercise helps briefly but the fatigue returns. You sigh frequently without noticing.

TCM view: Your Qi isn't actually low — it's stuck. Like a traffic jam on a highway: there's plenty of fuel in the cars, but nothing is moving. Your Liver system (the body's traffic controller) is overloaded, and the stagnation itself creates fatigue because energy can't reach where it's needed.

Key acupressure target: Move stuck Qi, release tension, and restore smooth flow through the channels.

4. Blood Deficiency Fatigue — "The Body Has No Fuel to Deliver"

You might have this if: Your fatigue is paired with dizziness, pale complexion, brittle nails, or poor memory. Your vision feels slightly blurry or your eyes are dry. You feel lightheaded when standing up quickly. Women may notice lighter periods or irregular cycles.

TCM view: In TCM, Blood (Xue) is not just red cells — it's the dense, nourishing form of Qi that physically sustains the body's tissues. When Blood is deficient, organs and muscles receive less nourishment. The body has the energy signal (Qi), but the delivery system lacks the substance to carry it.

Key acupressure target: Nourish Blood and support its production and circulation through the body.

Before You Start: How to Use Acupressure Correctly

Acupressure is simple but technique matters. Follow these guidelines for best results:

  • Use your thumb or index finger — apply firm, steady pressure, not quick pokes. The sensation should be "comfortable discomfort" (deep pressure that feels meaningful, not painful).
  • Breathe slowly — inhale as you locate the point, exhale as you apply pressure. Hold for 30–60 seconds per point, breathing evenly throughout.
  • Circular massage — once you locate the point, gently massage in small circles (clockwise) while maintaining pressure.
  • Both sides — most points are bilateral (exist on both sides of the body). Stimulate both sides for full effect, one side at a time.
  • Regular practice — acupressure works cumulatively. One session may bring temporary relief, but consistent daily practice (2-3 minutes per point) delivers the most benefit over 2-4 weeks.
Safety note: Avoid acupressure over broken skin, bruises, varicose veins, or recent scars. If you are pregnant, consult your healthcare provider before using acupressure — some points (LI4, SP6, GB21, BL60, BL67) are traditionally considered contraindicated during pregnancy. If you have a chronic health condition or are taking blood-thinning medication, check with your doctor first.

Point-by-Point Guide: Acupressure for Different Types of Fatigue

For Spleen Qi Deficiency (Battery-Not-Charging Fatigue)

If meals leave you more tired than energized and your brain fogs after eating, focus on these points that strengthen digestive Qi.

🔑 ST36 (Zusanli) — "Leg Three Miles"

Location: Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width outward from the shin bone. On the outside of the lower leg, in the muscle belly.

Technique: Use your thumb to press firmly into the muscle belly. Angle slightly toward the shin bone. Hold 45-60 seconds per leg. A mild aching or warm sensation is normal and indicates the point is active.

Why it works: ST36 is the most famous energy-building point in TCM — it's traditionally used to tonify Spleen Qi and build general vitality. Research suggests stimulating this point may influence digestive function and energy metabolism pathways. It's the go-to point for post-meal fatigue and general Qi deficiency.

🔑 CV6 (Qihai) — "Sea of Qi"

Location: On the midline of the lower abdomen, about 1.5 finger-widths below the belly button (navel).

Technique: Use two or three fingers together to apply gentle, downward pressure. Breathe deeply into your belly as you press. Hold 60 seconds. A deep sensation of warmth spreading through the lower abdomen is a good sign.

Why it works: CV6 is one of the body's primary Qi reservoirs. In TCM, the lower abdomen (Dantian) is considered the body's energy storage center, and CV6 is the point through which that stored Qi can be accessed and circulated.

🔑 SP6 (Sanyinjiao) — "Three Yin Intersection"

Location: About four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, just behind the shin bone. On the inside of the lower leg.

Technique: Press firmly with your thumb into the depression behind the shin bone. Hold 30-45 seconds per leg. This point can be quite tender — ease pressure if it's painful.

Why it works: SP6 is where three Yin channels (Spleen, Liver, Kidney) meet. Stimulating it harmonizes these systems and supports the Spleen's role in transforming food into Qi. It's particularly useful when fatigue is paired with digestive weakness.

Pregnancy caution: SP6 is traditionally contraindicated during pregnancy. Avoid if you are or may be pregnant.

For Kidney Qi Deficiency (Deep-Battery Fatigue)

If your fatigue feels bone-deep and comes with lower back ache or feeling cold, these points support the body's deep energy reserves.

🔑 KI3 (Taixi) — "Supreme Stream"

Location: In the depression midway between the inner ankle bone (medial malleolus) and the Achilles tendon. On the inside of the ankle.

Technique: Use your thumb to press into the hollow between the ankle bone and tendon. Angle slightly toward the Achilles. Hold 45-60 seconds per ankle. This point is quite accessible and responds well to firm, sustained pressure.

Why it works: KI3 is the source point (Yuan-Source point) of the Kidney channel — it's where the Kidney's primordial Qi surfaces. In TCM, stimulating KI3 is believed to tonify Kidney Qi and Yuan Qi (original Qi), making it a primary point for deep, constitutional fatigue.

🔑 CV4 (Guanyuan) — "Gate of Origin"

Location: On the midline of the lower abdomen, about four finger-widths below the belly button (navel).

Technique: Place two fingers together and apply gentle, warm pressure. Breathe deeply into the lower abdomen. Hold 60-90 seconds. The warmth your palm creates over the area is part of the therapy.

Why it works: CV4 is one of the most important energy-reserve points in TCM — traditionally associated with storing and protecting Jing (essence). It's where the body's deepest energetic resources are said to be housed. For fatigue after chronic illness, overwork, or aging, this point is a primary choice.

🔑 BL23 (Shenshu) — "Kidney Shu Point"

Location: On the lower back, about two finger-widths from the spine, level with the space between the 2nd and 3rd lumbar vertebrae. The lower back dimple area.

Technique: This point is harder to reach yourself — use your thumbs pressing from behind, or lie on a tennis ball placed at the point. Hold 30-45 seconds on each side. You can also ask a partner to apply pressure.

Why it works: BL23 is the Back-Shu point of the Kidney — these are special points where the organ's Qi surfaces on the back. Stimulating BL23 is believed to directly tonify Kidney Qi and strengthen the lower back, which is the Kidney's anatomical domain in TCM.

For Qi Stagnation Fatigue (Wired-and-Exhausted Fatigue)

If you feel stressed, tight, and tired all at once, focus on points that move stuck Qi and release tension.

🔑 LV3 (Taichong) — "Great Surge"

Location: On the top of the foot, in the depression between the 1st and 2nd metatarsal bones (about one hand-width from the webbing between big toe and second toe).

Technique: Use your thumb to press into the fleshy hollow between the two bones. Angle slightly upward (toward the ankle). Hold 45-60 seconds per foot. This point can feel tender when stagnation is present.

Why it works: LV3 is a primary point for moving Liver Qi stagnation — it's the most widely used point in TCM for stress-related tension. When Qi is stuck, fatigue results from energy not reaching where it's needed. LV3 acts like opening a valve, allowing the flow to resume.

Research context: fMRI studies have shown that stimulating LV3 modulates activity in brain regions associated with emotional regulation and pain processing, paralleling its traditional use for stress-related symptoms.

🔑 GB34 (Yanglingquan) — "Yang陵 Spring"

Location: On the outside of the lower leg, in the depression just below the head of the fibula bone (the bony prominence on the outer side of the knee).

Technique: Use your thumb to press into the depression just below and forward of the fibula head. Hold 30-45 seconds per leg. This point connects to the lateral aspect of the body and releases tension along the sides.

Why it works: GB34 is one of the most important points for smooth Qi flow throughout the body. In TCM, the Gallbladder (paired with the Liver) governs decision-making and the free flow of Qi. When fatigue is accompanied by frustration, indecision, or a feeling of being "stuck in life," GB34 can help restore movement.

🔑 LI4 (Hegu) — "Union Valley"

Location: On the back of the hand, in the webbing between the thumb and index finger. Press the thumb and index finger together — the highest point of the muscle mound is LI4.

Technique: Use the thumb of your opposite hand to press firmly into the fleshy mound between thumb and index finger. Hold 30-45 seconds per hand. This point can be quite sensitive.

Why it works: LI4 is one of the most versatile acupressure points, traditionally used to move Qi in the head, neck, and face. For stagnation fatigue, it helps release the tension that builds up in the upper body (shoulders, neck, jaw) when Qi can't flow freely. It's also a classic point for stress and tension headaches.

Pregnancy caution: LI4 is traditionally contraindicated during pregnancy. Avoid if you are or may be pregnant.

For Blood Deficiency Fatigue (Pale-and-Dizzy Fatigue)

If your fatigue is paired with pale complexion, dizziness, or brittle nails, these points support Blood production and circulation.

🔑 ST36 (Zusanli) — "Leg Three Miles"

Location: Same as above — four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width outward from the shin bone.

Technique: Same as above. Use thumb pressure, hold 45-60 seconds per leg.

Why it works (for Blood deficiency): ST36 is not only a Qi-tonifying point — it's also traditionally used to support the production of Blood. In TCM, the Spleen and Stomach systems create both Qi and Blood from food. Strengthening the Stomach channel at ST36 supports the entire digestive-nourishment cycle.

🔑 SP10 (Xuehai) — "Sea of Blood"

Location: On the inner thigh, about 2-3 finger-widths above the upper edge of the kneecap. When the knee is bent, it's on the bulge of the inner thigh muscle (vastus medialis).

Technique: With the knee slightly bent, use your thumb to press into the fleshy area above the inner knee. Hold 30-45 seconds per leg. A light pressure is sufficient — this area can be sensitive.

Why it works: SP10's name translates to "Sea of Blood" — it's one of the primary points in TCM for Blood health. Stimulating SP10 is traditionally believed to help the body produce, store, and circulate Blood more effectively, addressing the root of Blood deficiency fatigue.

🔑 BL17 (Geshu) — "Diaphragm Shu Point"

Location: On the upper back, about 1.5 finger-widths from the spine, level with the space between the 7th thoracic vertebra (roughly at the lower edge of the shoulder blades).

Technique: This point is difficult to reach alone. Ask a partner to apply thumb pressure, or lie on a massage ball placed at the point. Hold 30-45 seconds on each side.

Why it works: BL17 is the influential point (Hui point) of Blood in TCM — meaning all channels and organ systems that relate to Blood converge here. It's traditionally used to nourish and regulate Blood throughout the body, making it a foundational point for Blood deficiency patterns.

A 5-Minute Daily Fatigue Relief Routine

Not sure which pattern fits? Start with this simple daily routine that covers all four types of fatigue. Do it once in the morning and once in the afternoon (around 2-3 PM when energy naturally dips):

  1. ST36 (Zusanli) — 45 seconds per leg. Foundational energy-building point.
  2. CV6 (Qihai) — 60 seconds. Access the body's Qi reservoir in the lower abdomen.
  3. KI3 (Taixi) — 45 seconds per ankle. Support deep energy reserves.
  4. LV3 (Taichong) — 45 seconds per foot. Ensure whatever energy you have is actually flowing.

If you recognize yourself in one of the four patterns above, replace the general routine with the pattern-specific points for 2-3 weeks and notice how your energy responds.

What the Research Says

While acupressure is an ancient practice, modern research has begun investigating its effects on fatigue and energy:

  • Acupressure for cancer-related fatigue: A 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis (Supportive Care in Cancer, 15 RCTs, 1,200+ participants) found that acupressure significantly reduced cancer-related fatigue compared to usual care, with ST36 and CV6 among the most commonly used points.
  • Acupressure for chronic fatigue: A 2017 study (Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine, n=66) found that 6 weeks of self-administered acupressure improved fatigue, sleep quality, and quality of life in people with chronic fatigue syndrome.
  • ST36 and energy metabolism: Laboratory research has shown that stimulating ST36 may influence the autonomic nervous system and increase vagal tone, which is associated with improved digestive function and energy regulation.
  • Acupressure vs. regular touch: A 2014 RCT (Journal of Clinical Nursing, n=74) found that acupressure at specific points (ST36, SP6, KI3) was significantly more effective than light touch or sham points for reducing fatigue in postpartum women.
Note on the research: These studies are small to moderate in size, and most have methodological limitations. They suggest promise but do not constitute proof. Consider acupressure as a complementary practice alongside, not a replacement for, medical care.

Practical Takeaways

🧘 Match your fatigue to the right points: Spleen Qi = ST36 + CV6 + SP6. Kidney Qi = KI3 + CV4 + BL23. Qi Stagnation = LV3 + GB34 + LI4. Blood Deficiency = ST36 + SP10 + BL17.

🖐️ Consistency over intensity: Two minutes of acupressure twice a day is more effective than a single 20-minute session once a week. Build it into your routine — while waiting for coffee, at your desk, before bed.

🌙 Acupressure works best alongside good sleep, not instead of it: Acupressure can support your energy system, but chronic fatigue often requires addressing sleep quality, diet, stress, and underlying medical conditions. For more on sleep, see our Acupressure for Better Sleep guide.

🔄 Not feeling better after 2-3 weeks? Fatigue can have many causes — iron deficiency, thyroid conditions, sleep apnea, autoimmune issues, and more. If consistent acupressure doesn't shift your energy levels, or if your fatigue is severe or worsening, consult a healthcare provider for a thorough evaluation.

When to See a Doctor Immediately

While fatigue is common, certain patterns require prompt medical attention:

  • Unexplained weight loss alongside fatigue
  • Shortness of breath or chest pain with minimal exertion
  • Fever, night sweats, or unexplained bruising/bleeding
  • Fatigue so severe you can't get out of bed or perform daily activities
  • Thoughts of self-harm or suicidal ideation — contact a crisis line or emergency services immediately
  • Fatigue following a head injury

If your fatigue persists for more than 2-3 weeks despite addressing sleep, diet, and stress, see a primary care provider for blood work and a medical evaluation. Numerous treatable conditions (anemia, thyroid disorders, vitamin deficiencies, sleep disorders) can cause fatigue and are best addressed early.
