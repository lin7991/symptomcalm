Acupressure for Allergy Relief: Pressure Points for Clear Breathing

Cluster Guide · 6 min read

It starts the same way every year. Your eyes itch. Your nose runs like a faucet. You sneeze five times before you've even had your morning coffee. The antihistamines help — but they also make you drowsy, foggy, or jittery.

If you're looking for something you can do between doses — or on days when you'd rather not take another pill — there's a simple, drug-free technique worth knowing: acupressure. It costs nothing, has no side effects, and you can apply it yourself anywhere: at your desk, in the car (at a stoplight), or on the couch when the pollen count is high.

Here are the pressure points traditionally used in TCM for allergy support, where to find them, and how each one works.

What Is Acupressure?

Acupressure is essentially acupuncture without the needles. Instead of inserting fine needles, you apply gentle but firm pressure using your fingers or thumb on specific points along the body's energy pathways — meridians. In TCM theory, these points regulate the flow of Qi (vital energy). When seasonal factors like Wind, Cold, or Dampness invade the body's surface defenses (called Wei Qi — the protective Qi), the result is what we experience as allergy symptoms: sneezing, congestion, watery eyes, and sinus pressure.

Western researchers have proposed that stimulating these points may influence the autonomic nervous system, reduce local inflammation, and encourage sinus drainage. Some studies suggest that acupressure applied to specific facial and hand points can reduce nasal congestion and sneezing frequency during allergy season. The research is still emerging, but the evidence is promising enough that it's worth trying — especially since there are no risks beyond mild soreness.

Important: Acupressure is a complementary self-care tool. It is not a replacement for prescribed allergy medications, immunotherapy, or emergency treatment for severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis). If you have trouble breathing, swelling of the lips or throat, or a known severe allergy, seek emergency care immediately.

The 5 Allergy Points

Each of these points targets a different aspect of the allergic response. You can use them individually as needed or combine them into a 10-minute routine during peak allergy season.

1. LI20 — Yingxiang (迎香) — Welcome Fragrance

Location: In the crease on the outside of each nostril, where the nose meets the cheek. Run your finger down the side of your nose until you feel a small indentation — that's it.

How to stimulate: Use your index fingers to press both sides simultaneously. Press upward and inward toward the bridge of the nose. Hold firm pressure for 1-2 minutes. You may feel a sensation of openness in the nasal passages almost immediately.

TCM rationale: LI20 is the classic point for nasal congestion, sinus pressure, and loss of smell. Its name literally translates to "Welcome Fragrance" — a clue that it was traditionally used to restore the sense of smell and open the nasal passages. In TCM, this point is believed to expel Wind (the external pathogen most associated with acute allergy attacks) and clear the nasal passages by regulating the Large Intestine meridian, which is paired with the Lungs in TCM's organ network theory. The Lungs control the skin and the nose, so clearing this meridian can directly affect breathing.

2. LI4 — Hegu (合谷) — Joining Valley

Location: In the fleshy web between your thumb and index finger. Bring your thumb and index finger together — the point is on the highest spot of the muscle bulge, roughly at the midpoint of the second metacarpal bone.

How to stimulate: Press firmly with the thumb of your opposite hand. Use a circular motion or steady pressure for 2-3 minutes per hand. This point is often quite tender, especially during allergy season or when you're fighting off a cold.

TCM rationale: LI4 is one of the most powerful points in TCM — the "command point" of the face and head. In TCM theory, it regulates the flow of Qi and Blood through the head and face, making it an important point for sinus headaches, facial pressure, and nasal congestion. It's also traditionally used to strengthen the body's Wei Qi (protective Qi) — the immune barrier that, when weak, allows allergens to trigger the overreactive response we call allergies. Additionally, LI4 is considered a major point for pain relief due to its influence on endorphin release, which may explain why some people find it helpful for sinus headache pain.

3. LU7 — Lieque (列缺) — Broken Sequence

Location: On the inner forearm, about 1.5 inches above the wrist crease. Here's the trick: interlock your thumb and index finger with the opposite hand, placing the index finger of the upper hand on the styloid process (the bony bump on the outer wrist). LI7 is under the tip of that index finger, in the hollow between the two tendons.

How to stimulate: Press into the hollow with your thumb for 2-3 minutes per arm. A mild tingling sensation traveling toward the thumb or index finger is a good sign.

TCM rationale: LU7 is the "confluent point" of the Conception Vessel (Ren Mai) and the primary window into the Lung meridian. In TCM, the Lungs govern Qi and respiration, and they control the skin and body hair — the first line of defense against external pathogens. LU7 is traditionally used to release the exterior (expel Wind-Cold or Wind-Heat from the surface of the body) and to descend Lung Qi. In plain language: it helps your body push out what shouldn't be there (pollen, dust, congestion) and lets your breathing settle. This point is particularly useful for allergy patterns that involve a tickly cough, scratchy throat, or frequent sneezing.

Modern research note: LU7 and LI4 are among the most commonly studied acupoints in respiratory research. Some studies suggest that stimulating these points may reduce nasal secretion volume and improve peak nasal inspiratory flow (how much air you can pull through your nose).

4. GB20 — Fengchi (风池) — Wind Pool

Location: In the hollows at the base of your skull, between the two large neck muscles. If you run your fingers up the back of your neck, just below the skull, you'll feel two indentations — one on each side. These are the Wind Pools.

How to stimulate: Use both thumbs to press firmly upward into the hollows. Tilt your head back slightly to increase the contact. Hold for 2-3 minutes, breathing deeply. This is one of the most satisfying points to press — the release of tension can be immediate.

TCM rationale: GB20's name says it all. In TCM, Wind is the primary external pathogen that causes acute onset conditions — including allergy attacks. The "Wind Pools" are traditionally used to expel Wind from the body and clear the head. When Wind invades the body's surface defenses, it dishes up symptoms that start suddenly: sneezing fits, itchy eyes, and an overall feeling of being "under attack." GB20 is the first line of defense. It's also the go-to point for tension headaches and stiff necks, both of which frequently tag along during allergy season, so this is a versatile point to hit.

5. ST3 — Juliao (巨髎) — Great Crevice

Location: Directly below the pupil, level with the bottom of the nose, in the depression on the cheekbone. If you look straight ahead and drop a line from the center of your pupil, ST3 is where that line meets the cheekbone.

How to stimulate: Press gently but firmly with your index fingers on both cheeks simultaneously. Hold for 1-2 minutes. This point sits directly over the maxillary sinus — the large sinus cavity below your eyes — so it can be particularly helpful for sinus pressure and facial pain.

TCM rationale: ST3 is a local point for sinus and facial issues. The Stomach meridian runs across the face, and in TCM the Stomach together with the Spleen produce the Qi and fluids that nourish the body's surface. When these fluids are disturbed by external pathogenic factors, they can accumulate in the sinus cavities as thick mucus. ST3 is traditionally used to regulate the flow of fluids through the face and clear sinus congestion. If your allergies involve a feeling of pressure behind the cheekbones, thick nasal discharge, or a dull ache under the eyes, this point is especially worth trying.

How to Build a Daily Allergy Acupressure Routine

Consistency is more important than intensity. Here's a routine that takes about 10 minutes:

  • Morning (before symptoms peak): Start with GB20 (Wind Pool) and LI20 (Welcome Fragrance) — 2 minutes each. These help stabilize your body's surface defenses before the allergens hit.
  • Midday (when symptoms flare): Add LI4 (Joining Valley) and LU7 (Broken Sequence) — 2 minutes each per side. These work on the immune response and help descend Qi so your breathing normalizes.
  • Evening (sinus pressure relief): Finish with ST3 (Great Crevice) for 2 minutes on both cheeks. This helps drain the sinus cavities before bed and can improve sleep quality during allergy season.
Pro tip: Don't press so hard that it hurts — the goal is a firm, steady pressure that creates a mild ache or tingling sensation. Hold each point while taking 5-6 slow, deep breaths. Focusing on slow, diaphragmatic breathing while pressing the point amplifies the calming effect on the nervous system.

When to Use Caution

Acupressure is very safe, but there are a few situations where you should be careful:

  • Pregnancy: LI4 and some other points are traditionally considered contraindicated during pregnancy because they may stimulate uterine contractions. If you are or may be pregnant, consult a licensed acupuncturist before trying acupressure.
  • Facial wounds or recent surgery: Avoid pressing LI20 and ST3 directly on areas with cuts, acne, or surgical sites.
  • Fragile skin or blood-thinning medication: Use lighter pressure to avoid bruising.

What Does the Research Say?

While acupressure for allergies is less studied than acupuncture, there is emerging evidence. A 2015 systematic review in the Annals of Allergy, Asthma & Immunology found that acupuncture — and by extension acupressure at the same points — showed promise for seasonal allergic rhinitis, with some studies reporting decreased symptom scores and reduced medication use.

Some studies suggest that acupressure may work by modulating the immune response — potentially reducing the release of histamine and other inflammatory mediators that cause the classic allergy cascade of sneezing, itching, and congestion. However, most studies are small, and the evidence is not strong enough to recommend acupressure as a standalone treatment. It works best as part of a broader symptom management plan that includes conventional care, avoidance strategies (keeping windows closed during high pollen days, using HEPA filters), and appropriate medication when needed.

Danger signals: If you experience shortness of breath, wheezing, swelling of the face or throat, difficulty swallowing, or a rapid onset of hives — these can be signs of a severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis). Do not rely on acupressure. Seek emergency medical care immediately. Acupressure is a complementary practice, not a substitute for emergency treatment or prescribed allergy medications like epinephrine auto-injectors.

The Bottom Line

Acupressure won't replace your antihistamine, and it won't make your allergies disappear. But it can give you something that no pill offers: a sense of agency. When your nose is running and your eyes are itching, pressing specific points on your own face and hands can provide immediate relief and a feeling of control over your body's reaction.

The next time allergy season hits, try starting with GB20 (Wind Pool) and LI20 (Welcome Fragrance) for two minutes each. Breathe slowly. Notice if the pressure in your face shifts. And then decide for yourself what it's worth.
