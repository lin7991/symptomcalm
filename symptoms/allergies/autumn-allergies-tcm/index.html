Autumn Allergies Through a TCM Lens: Dryness, the Lung and Seasonal Resilience

Symptom Guide · 8 min read

If you're one of the millions who experience autumn allergies, you've likely noticed something: spring allergy relief strategies don't always work in the fall. The antihistamines you relied on in May feel less effective. Your nose feels dry and irritated rather than runny. And the sneezing seems to come with a scratchy throat and a tickly cough that no amount of tea seems to soothe.

That's because autumn allergies aren't spring allergies with a different calendar — they are a different TCM story entirely.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, spring allergies are understood through Wind — a sudden, migrating pathogen that invades the upper body when the defenses are down. Autumn allergies, by contrast, center on Dryness (燥, Zào). Dryness is a distinct pathogen with its own character, season, and organ affinity. And understanding the difference can help you approach fall allergies with strategies that actually match what's happening in your body.

Think of it like caring for two different plants. One is wilting from too much wind (spring), and you'd put up a windbreak. The other is wilting from dry soil and air (autumn), and you'd water it and increase humidity. Same wilting, different cause — different remedy.

The Autumn Allergy Problem: What Makes Fall Different

From a Western medical perspective, autumn allergies are primarily driven by ragweed pollen, which peaks from mid-August through October in most temperate regions. Ragweed is one of the most potent allergens known — a single plant can produce a billion pollen grains, and those grains can travel hundreds of miles on the wind.

But ragweed isn't the only autumn trigger. Mold spores from fallen leaves and decaying vegetation peak in autumn as well. And as the weather cools, people spend more time indoors, where dust mites, pet dander, and indoor allergens circulate in heated, recirculated air that tends to be much drier than outdoor air.

The combination — potent outdoor allergens + dry indoor air + more time in enclosed spaces — creates an autumn allergy profile that feels distinct from spring's watery, sneezy episodes. Autumn allergies often present with:

  • Dry, scratchy throat and nasal passages
  • A tickly, non-productive cough
  • Itchy, dry eyes rather than watery ones
  • Sinus pressure with a sensation of tightness rather than congestion
  • Skin dryness and irritation alongside respiratory symptoms
  • Symptoms that worsen in dry environments (heated rooms, air-conditioned cars) and improve in humid conditions

This symptom profile is important because it points to a different mechanism — and a different TCM approach — than what works for spring allergies.

Dryness: Autumn's Signature Pathogen in TCM

In the TCM system of seasonal medicine, each season is associated with a specific environmental pathogen. Summer has Heat and Dampness. Spring has Wind. And autumn has Dryness.

The Dryness pathogen (燥邪, Zào Xié) has two main characteristics:

  • It dries up body fluids. Dryness depletes the body's moisture reserves — what TCM calls Jin Ye (体液, body fluids). This manifests as dry nose, dry throat, dry skin, dry cough, and thirst.
  • It affects the Lung first. The Lung is the most moisture-sensitive organ in TCM theory. It governs respiration, regulates the skin and body hair, and is responsible for dispersing Qi and fluids throughout the body. The Lung "dislikes dryness" — it needs a moist, clean environment to function properly.

Think of the Lung like the delicate tissue inside a humidifier. When the air is properly moist, the tissue works efficiently. When the air becomes dry, the tissue stiffens, cracks, and becomes irritated. The Lung behaves the same way when faced with autumn Dryness.

TCM distinguishes between two types of Dryness:

  • Warm Dryness (温燥) — common in early autumn, when lingering summer heat combines with the onset of dry air. Symptoms may include a dry cough with scanty phlegm, throat irritation, slight fever, and thirst. The Dryness carries residual Heat.
  • Cool Dryness (凉燥) — more common in late autumn as temperatures drop. Symptoms include a dry cough, chills, no sweating, and a sensation of cold along with the dryness. The Dryness carries Cold.

Understanding whether your autumn allergies lean toward Warm Dryness or Cool Dryness can help you choose the most appropriate relief approach.

The Lung-Autumn Connection: Why This Season Challenges Respiratory Health

In the TCM Five Element system, autumn corresponds to the Metal element — which governs the Lung and Large Intestine organ systems. This means autumn is the season when the Lung is naturally most active — and most vulnerable.

When the Lung is healthy and well-nourished, it handles Dryness without issue. The body's fluids are abundant enough to keep respiratory tissues moist, and defensive Qi (Wei Qi) circulates strongly at the body's surface, filtering out environmental irritants before they become a problem.

But when the Lung is already in a weakened state — common after a summer of excessive cooling foods (ice cream, cold drinks, air conditioning) that can subtly impair fluid metabolism — the Dryness of autumn can tip the balance.

The summer aftereffect: Many people enter autumn with what TCM calls "hidden Dampness" from summer — a residue of the summer Damp-Heat season that hasn't fully cleared. This creates a mixed Damp-Dry pattern: congested sinuses from the leftover Dampness combined with dry, irritated respiratory tissues from the autumn Dryness. Your sinuses feel full, but your throat feels parched — both things can be true at once.

The Lung also governs Wei Qi — the defensive energy that patrols the body's surface. When Lung Qi is weakened by Dryness, Wei Qi becomes less effective. This creates a double vulnerability: the body's moisture barrier (nasal passages, throat lining) is compromised and its immune perimeter is porous. Allergens and irritants penetrate more easily, and the body's response tends to involve both dryness (cough, scratchy throat) and sensitivity (sneezing, itchy eyes).

3 TCM Patterns of Autumn Allergies

Not everyone experiences autumn allergies the same way. Here are three common TCM patterns, organized from most to least common.

1. Lung Dryness (肺燥)

You might have this if: Your nose and throat feel dry and scratchy. You have a dry, tickly cough with little or no phlegm. Your lips may be chapped and your skin feels tight. You're thirsty but small sips don't seem to help much. Your symptoms feel worse in heated rooms and better in humid environments or after a warm shower.

TCM view: The Dryness pathogen has depleted the Lung's fluids (Lung Yin), impairing the organ's ability to moisten and protect the respiratory tract. The Lung's descending function is compromised, leading to a rebellious Qi cough — the characteristic tickly, non-productive cough of autumn.

Common triggers: Sudden temperature drops, indoor heating, dry climates, prolonged talking or breathing through the mouth.

2. Wei Qi Deficiency with Dryness (卫气虚弱兼燥邪)

You might have this if: You catch every cold that goes around. Your autumn allergies come with frequent sneezing, a runny nose that alternates between clear discharge and dry irritation, and a general sense of being "wiped out" after being outdoors. You may feel sensitive to wind and drafts, and you often feel chilly when others are comfortable.

TCM view: The defensive Qi (Wei Qi) is too weak to protect the body's surface. Normally, Wei Qi circulates just under the skin and around the external openings (nose, mouth, throat), filtering out environmental irritants. When it's deficient, allergens and Dryness penetrate easily. The Lung, having to work harder to defend the body, draws on its own moisture reserves — further depleting fluids and creating a cycle of dryness and vulnerability.

Common triggers: History of frequent colds or sinus infections, poor sleep, chronic stress, a diet low in nourishing, warm foods.

3. Damp-Dry Mixed Pattern (湿燥夹杂)

You might have this if: You feel both congested and dry at the same time. Your sinuses feel heavy and full, but your throat feels scratchy and dry. You may have thick nasal discharge that's hard to clear, along with a dry cough. Your brain feels foggy, and you notice your digestion is sluggish too — bloating after meals, loose stools, or a heavy sensation in your stomach.

TCM view: This pattern reflects the seasonal transition from summer to autumn. Residual Dampness from summer (from humid weather, cold drinks, and cooling foods) hasn't fully cleared from the body. As autumn Dryness arrives, it sits on top of this existing Dampness — creating a contradictory symptom picture. The Spleen (which governs fluid metabolism) is struggling to transform and transport fluids effectively, while the Lung is simultaneously being dried out.

Common triggers: A summer diet high in cold/raw foods and dairy, humid climate transition, underlying Spleen Qi deficiency.

Most people will recognize themselves in one of these three patterns. If you fit more than one, that's normal — TCM patterns often blend. The key is to identify which aspect of your experience is most dominant and focus your relief efforts there first.

TCM Approaches Traditionally Used for Autumn Allergies

The following are traditional TCM approaches for addressing the patterns behind autumn allergies. They are educational descriptions, not medical recommendations. Always work with a qualified practitioner for personalized guidance.

Moistening the Lung (Nourishing Body Fluids)

For the Lung Dryness pattern, the central strategy is to nourish Yin and moisten dryness — in TCM terms, "滋润肺燥." This can be approached through:

  • Moistening foods: Pears are the classic autumn fruit in TCM dietary therapy — they are traditionally believed to nourish Lung fluids and relieve dryness. Other moistening foods include apples, honey, almonds, sesame seeds, white fungus (雪花银耳), and lotus root. These foods are best eaten cooked (poached pears, pear soup, steamed apple) rather than raw — cooking makes them gentler on the digestive system.
  • Herbal teas: A simple tea made from dried pear slices, licorice root (甘草, Gān Cǎo), and a small amount of honey is traditionally used to soothe dry throat and cough. Lonicera (金银花) and mulberry leaf (桑叶) are sometimes added for the Warm Dryness variant.
  • Humidity management: Using a humidifier in the bedroom at night can dramatically reduce morning dry throat and nasal irritation. This aligns with the TCM principle of supporting the body's external environment to reduce the burden on internal systems.
  • Steam inhalation: Inhaling steam from a bowl of hot water (with or without a few drops of peppermint essential oil) can help moisten the nasal passages and soothe the respiratory tract — similar to the TCM concept of "moistening the Lungs through the nose."

Strengthening Wei Qi (Building Defensive Resilience)

For the Wei Qi deficiency pattern, prevention is the primary strategy — ideally starting in late summer before autumn allergies peak:

  • Acupressure points you can use at home:
    • Lung 7 (列缺, Liè Quē) — located on the inner forearm, about 1.5 thumb-widths above the wrist crease, in the depression between the radius bone and the tendon. Gently press for 1-2 minutes on each arm. This point is traditionally used to activate the Lung meridian and support defensive Qi.
    • Stomach 36 (足三里, Zú Sān Lǐ) — located on the lower leg, four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width outward from the shin bone. This is one of TCM's most important points for building overall Qi and immunity.
    • Large Intestine 4 (合谷, Hé Gǔ) — in the web between thumb and index finger. Used to influence the face, nose, and throat through the meridian system. Press firmly for 30-60 seconds.
  • Warm, nourishing meals: As the weather cools, shift from summer's light, raw foods to warm, cooked meals. Congee (rice porridge) with ginger and scallion is a traditional autumn breakfast in TCM dietary therapy — it supports both the Lung and the Spleen without burdening digestion.
  • Layered clothing: The neck and upper back are considered vulnerable to pathogen invasion in TCM. A scarf or collar in cool autumn weather helps protect the "Wind Pool" (GB-20) area at the base of the skull — the same principle that makes you instinctively pull your collar up on a windy day.

Clearing Residual Dampness (For the Mixed Pattern)

If you suspect the Damp-Dry mixed pattern, addressing the Dampness component first can often relieve the dryness:

  • Reducing damp-producing foods: Dairy, cold drinks, fried foods, and excessive sugar all contribute to Dampness in TCM theory. Reducing these during late summer and early autumn can help clear the residual Dampness before Dryness sets in.
  • Light, warming foods: Pumpkin, carrots, winter squash, and cooked leafy greens are excellent transition foods. They are warming enough to support the Spleen (helping it process fluids) without being heavy enough to create more Dampness.
  • Ginger and cinnamon: A small amount of fresh ginger in cooking or as a tea helps warm the Spleen and dispel residual Dampness. Cinnamon is another warming spice traditionally used to support digestion and fluid metabolism during seasonal transitions.
  • Gentle movement: Light exercise like walking, yoga, or qigong helps move Qi and fluids, preventing Dampness from stagnating. The key is gentle, sustained movement — not intense workouts that deplete fluids further.

The Autumn Transition: Prevention Before Dryness Peaks

TCM emphasizes seasonal transition periods as the most important time for preventive care. The shift from late summer to early autumn — roughly late August to mid-September in temperate climates — is the ideal window to start preparing the body for Dryness:

  • Start moistening early. Begin including pear, apple, and honey in your diet a few weeks before your usual autumn allergy season starts.
  • Reduce cooling foods gradually. Cut back on ice cream, cold drinks, and raw salads as autumn approaches. Your digestive system — and your Lung — will appreciate the gradual shift to warm, cooked foods.
  • Stay hydrated, but with warm fluids. Room-temperature or warm water with a squeeze of lemon is more hydrating in TCM terms than ice-cold water, which can shock the Spleen and impair fluid metabolism.
  • Adjust your environment. Air conditioning speeds up moisture loss. As autumn approaches, use AC less or set it to a higher temperature. Open windows during dry, moderate days to bring fresh air in.

What Research Says About TCM and Autumn Allergies

Seasonal allergy research in TCM has largely focused on spring patterns, but some studies are relevant to autumn allergy management:

  • Acupuncture for seasonal allergic rhinitis has been studied in several randomized trials. A 2015 JAMA Internal Medicine trial (n=422) found that acupuncture provided modest improvement in symptom scores compared to sham acupuncture over 8 weeks. While the study focused on spring allergies, the mechanism — modulation of immune response and inflammatory pathways — is season-independent.
  • Chinese herbal formulas for allergic rhinitis were evaluated in a 2020 systematic review, which suggested potential benefits for symptom reduction. However, the authors noted significant heterogeneity among studies and called for more standardized, high-quality research.
  • A 2018 study in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine examined the effects of Yu Ping Feng San (Jade Windscreen Powder) on allergic rhinitis in an animal model, finding reduced inflammatory markers and improved immune regulation. The formula is traditionally used for Wei Qi deficiency, which is relevant to autumn's Lung-Dryness pattern.
  • The concept of "seasonal pathogen" — that different environmental factors dominate in different seasons — is increasingly supported by research on seasonal variations in immune function. A 2017 Nature Microbiology study found that human gut microbiome composition shifts between summer and winter, suggesting seasonal factors influence immune regulation in ways that align with the TCM framework.

This research is preliminary. TCM is a complementary system — it works alongside, not instead of, conventional allergy management.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Know your autumn pattern. Is your fall allergy experience mainly dry and scratchy (Lung Dryness), frequent and draining (Wei Qi deficiency), or a confusing mix of both (Damp-Dry)? Your relief strategy changes depending on which fits best.
  2. Eat for the season. Poached pears, honey, almonds, warm congee, cooked apples — these aren't just cozy autumn foods. In TCM, they directly nourish Lung fluids and build resilience against the Dryness pathogen.
  3. Moisturize your environment. A bedroom humidifier can make a significant difference in morning symptoms. Steam inhalation and warm showers also help keep respiratory tissues moist.
  4. Protect your neck. A scarf in cool, windy autumn weather protects the vulnerable "Wind Pool" area at the base of the skull — a traditional TCM preventive measure.
  5. Start early. The best time to prepare for autumn allergies is late summer, by gradually shifting your diet and environment to support Lung health before Dryness peaks.
  6. Use acupressure for acute relief. LI 4 (between thumb and index finger) and LU 7 (inner forearm, above wrist) are accessible points you can press when symptoms flare.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ If you experience difficulty breathing, wheezing, chest tightness, or swelling of the face, lips, or throat — seek emergency medical attention immediately, as these may be signs of a serious allergic reaction or anaphylaxis. Also consult a medical professional if your allergy symptoms significantly interfere with sleep, work, or daily activities, or if over-the-counter medications are no longer providing relief. Chronic cough, especially if accompanied by fever, night sweats, or unexplained weight loss, should be evaluated by a doctor. TCM is a complementary approach and does not replace medical diagnosis, prescription medications, or emergency care.
