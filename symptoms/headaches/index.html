Headaches: A TCM Perspective on Tension, Migraines, and Triggers

Pillar Guide · 8 min read

If you're reading this, you've probably reached for the painkillers more times than you can count. Maybe they help — for a few hours. Then the pounding comes back. Maybe you've been told "it's just stress" or "drink more water" and left with nothing that actually addresses why your head keeps hurting.

You're not alone. Over 1 billion people worldwide experience headaches regularly. For many, conventional treatments offer relief but don't explain why it keeps happening.

The Western View: What Painkillers Miss

Modern medicine categorises headaches into tension-type, migraine, and cluster headaches. Treatment usually centres on analgesics, triptans, and preventive medications. These can be effective for acute relief, but they rarely answer the deeper question: what is causing this pattern?

This approach treats the headache like a broken alarm — silence it and move on. But what if the alarm is trying to tell you something about your body's balance?

A Different Lens: How TCM Sees Headaches

Traditional Chinese Medicine has analysed headaches for more than 2,000 years. The core insight? A headache isn't a single disease — it's a signal from your body that something is out of balance. And depending on where the headache is located and what kind of pain it is, the underlying cause may be completely different from person to person.

In TCM theory, headaches are broadly divided into two categories: headaches due to excess (something blocking the flow of Qi and Blood to the head) and headaches due to deficiency (the head isn't receiving enough nourishment). Treatment depends entirely on which pattern you're experiencing.

Reading the Map: Headache Location Matters

One of TCM's most elegant tools is using the location of your headache to trace it to a specific channel or organ. This isn't mystical — think of it like a doctor asking where it hurts to narrow down the diagnosis.

  • Temple or side of the head (migraine-like): Typically linked to the Gall Bladder and Liver channels. Often associated with rising energy or heat patterns.
  • Forehead or front of the head (sinus-like): Often connected to the Stomach channel. Indigestion, food triggers, or dampness may be involved.
  • Back of the head or neck (tension-like): Linked to the Bladder channel. Often related to external factors like wind and cold, or kidney imbalances.
  • Top of the head (vertex): Associated with the Liver channel. This location is commonly seen in emotional or stress-driven headaches.
  • Whole head (band-like pressure): Often reflects Spleen and Stomach patterns, especially when dampness or phlegm clouds the head.

5 TCM Patterns of Headaches

Which one resonates with your experience?

1. Liver Yang Rising (肝阳上亢) — The Migraine Pattern

You might have this if: Your headaches are throbbing, pulsing, and often on one side of the head (temple area). They come with irritability, ringing in the ears, dizziness, and sensitivity to light or noise. Stress, anger, or lack of sleep makes them worse.

TCM view: The Liver is responsible for keeping Qi flowing smoothly. When stress or frustration builds up over time, Liver Qi stagnates. If the stagnation persists, it can "turn to fire" — the Qi heats up and surges upward to the head, like steam building pressure in a pot.

Traditional approach: Calming the Liver and anchoring the Yang — using herbs and acupoints that gently redirect excessive energy downward. Think of it like releasing the steam valve rather than trying to stop the fire.

2. Qi and Blood Deficiency (气血两虚) — The Exhaustion Headache

You might have this if: Your headaches are dull, aching, and get worse when you're tired, hungry, or have been working long hours. The pain is mild but persistent — it feels like a heavy fog in your head rather than a sharp pain. You feel lightheaded or dizzy.

TCM view: The head needs a steady supply of Qi and Blood to function properly. When these are depleted — from chronic stress, poor diet, blood loss, or overwork — the head is undernourished. The brain literally doesn't have enough fuel.

Traditional approach: Nourishing Qi and Blood to give the head what it needs. This pattern responds particularly well to dietary changes: warm, cooked, nutrient-dense foods are traditionally recommended.

3. Phlegm and Dampness (痰湿内阻) — The Brain Fog Headache

You might have this if: Your head feels heavy and foggy, as if it's wrapped in cotton. You feel groggy, especially in the morning. The headache is dull and oppressive. You may have a feeling of nausea, a greasy coating on your tongue, or digestive bloating. A diet rich in dairy, fried foods, and sugar is common.

TCM view: When the Spleen (the body's digestive/transport system) is weak, it doesn't process fluids efficiently. Instead of being transformed into nourishing fluids, they become "Phlegm" — a thick, sticky substance that can rise up and cloud the head, obstructing the clear Qi pathway.

Traditional approach: Strengthening the Spleen and resolving Phlegm through both herbs and significant dietary changes. Lighter foods, less dairy, and warming spices are traditionally emphasised.

4. Blood Stasis (瘀血阻络) — The Chronic, Stabbing Headache

You might have this if: Your headaches are sharp, stabbing, and fixed in one spot. They may have started after a head injury. The pain can be intense and lasts a long time. Over-the-counter painkillers barely touch it. You may notice dark circles under your eyes or a dark tongue complexion.

TCM view: Blood Stasis means the blood isn't circulating properly in a certain area. Like a traffic jam in a specific intersection, the stagnation causes localised pain. Past injuries, surgery, or long-term Qi stagnation can all lead to Blood Stasis.

Traditional approach: Moving Blood and breaking up stagnation. This pattern is treated more aggressively in TCM — with stronger acupoint protocols and herbs traditionally used to invigorate blood circulation.

5. External Wind Invasion (外感风邪) — The Weather-Sensitive Headache

You might have this if: Your headaches come on when the weather changes — especially wind, cold, or damp days. You feel achy and may have a stiff neck or slight chills. The pain moves around or feels like it's right on the surface of your head.

TCM view: In TCM theory, the body's protective energy (Wei Qi) is like an immune shield. When it's weak, external factors — particularly Wind — can invade the channels of the head and neck, disrupting Qi flow. Wind is considered the "carrier" that brings other factors like Cold or Dampness into the body.

Traditional approach: Expelling Wind and protecting the body's surface. Warming acupoints and herbs like ginger and mint are traditionally used to "sweep out" the invading pathogen.

How TCM Approaches Headaches Differently

The fundamental difference is precision through pattern differentiation. In Western medicine, two people with migraines might get the same triptan prescription. In TCM, two people with identical-looking headaches could receive completely different treatments — because the underlying pattern (Liver Yang rising vs. Phlegm vs. Blood deficiency) is different.

Common tools a TCM practitioner might consider:

  • Acupuncture: Some studies suggest acupuncture may help reduce the frequency and intensity of tension-type headaches and migraines. The body of research is one of the more robust areas of TCM research — a 2016 Cochrane review found acupuncture to be effective for episodic migraines.
  • Herbal formulas: Formulas like Tian Ma Gou Teng Yin (天麻钩藤饮) have been traditionally used for Liver Yang rising headaches. Chuan Xiong Cha Tiao San (川芎茶调散) is a classic formula for Wind-related headaches.
  • Dietary guidance: In TCM, headache triggers aren't just "foods to avoid" — they're clues about your pattern. Greasy food aggravating your headache suggests Phlegm. Spicy food triggering pounding pain suggests Liver Yang.
  • Tui Na (therapeutic massage): Targeted massage of the head, neck, and shoulders can release tension in the channels commonly affected by headaches.
Important: These are traditional uses based on TCM theory, not medical claims. Results vary by individual. Always work with a qualified healthcare provider.

What Research Says

Headache research is one of the better-studied areas in TCM, with several notable reviews:

  • A 2016 Cochrane review of acupuncture for migraine prophylaxis found that adding acupuncture to routine care reduced migraine frequency compared to routine care alone.
  • A 2021 meta-analysis suggested that acupuncture is effective for tension-type headaches, with effects lasting beyond the treatment period.
  • Some clinical studies have indicated that certain TCM herbal formulas traditionally used for headache patterns (like Tian Ma Gou Teng Yin) may support symptom management, though more standardised research is needed.

This research is promising but has limitations. TCM is a complementary approach — it works alongside, not instead of, conventional medical care.

Practical Takeaways

If headaches are part of your life, here are things to discuss with your healthcare provider:

  1. Map your headache. Where does it hurt? When does it happen? What makes it better or worse? These details are gold in TCM diagnosis.
  2. Track your triggers. Weather changes, stress, certain foods, sleep disruption — noticing patterns can reveal your underlying TCM pattern.
  3. Consider a multi-modal approach. Acupuncture has a better evidence base for headaches than many other TCM applications. If you're curious about TCM, this may be a good starting point.
  4. Find a qualified practitioner. Look for a licensed TCM or acupuncture practitioner who takes the time to identify your specific pattern.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ If you experience the worst headache of your life (sometimes called a "thunderclap headache"), headaches after a head injury, headaches with fever and stiff neck, or headaches accompanied by vision changes, weakness, or confusion — seek emergency medical attention immediately. TCM is a complementary approach and does not replace emergency care or proper medical diagnosis.
