Tension Headaches: The TCM View on Muscle Tension and Qi Stagnation

Symptom Guide · 7 min read

The band tightens around your forehead. Your shoulders creep up toward your ears. By mid-afternoon, your head feels like it's being squeezed in a vice — not enough to stop you from working, but enough to make everything feel harder than it should.

You've tried stretching. You've tried ibuprofen. You've tried "just relaxing." And yet, tomorrow, the same dull ache will be back.

Tension headaches are the most common type of headache worldwide, affecting up to 78% of people at some point in their lives. But common doesn't mean well-understood. In TCM theory, the tension headache pattern offers a remarkably clear picture of what's happening — and points toward approaches that go beyond "take a pill and wait."

Think of it like this: Imagine a garden hose with a kink in it. Water flows up to the kink, but nothing gets past it. Pressure builds behind the blockage. The sides of the hose strain under the force. That's Qi stagnation — the flow is blocked, and the tension you feel in your head and neck is the physical manifestation of that blocked energy.

Releasing the kink — not just numbing the sensation — is what TCM traditionally aims to do.

For a broader overview of headache patterns in TCM, see our Headaches: A TCM Perspective guide. This article focuses specifically on tension headaches and their connection to Qi stagnation.

The Western View: Muscles and Stress

Conventional medicine classifies tension headaches as primary headaches — meaning they're not caused by another underlying condition. They're typically attributed to muscle tension in the scalp, neck, and shoulders, often triggered by stress, poor posture, fatigue, or eye strain.

Treatment usually involves over-the-counter pain relievers (ibuprofen, acetaminophen), muscle relaxants, and lifestyle adjustments. These can be effective for acute episodes. But when tension headaches become chronic — occurring 15 or more days per month — medication overuse can actually make things worse, creating a cycle of rebound headaches.

The gap in the conventional model is that it doesn't fully explain why the tension keeps returning. Why do some people under stress get tension headaches while others don't? Why does the same posture cause problems for one person and not another?

The TCM View: Qi Stagnation and Muscle Tension

In TCM theory, tension headaches are primarily understood as a Qi stagnation pattern (气滞, qì zhì). The key insight is that muscle tension and emotional stress are not separate problems — they're two sides of the same coin, both reflecting a disruption in the smooth flow of Qi through the body's channels.

The relevant channels for tension headaches are:

  • The Gall Bladder channel — runs along the sides of the head and down the sides of the neck and shoulders. When Qi stagnates here, it produces the classic "vice-like" tension headache with shoulder tightness.
  • The Bladder channel — runs down the back of the head and spine. Stagnation here often causes tension at the base of the skull and upper back.
  • The San Jiao (Triple Burner) channel — also traverses the shoulder and neck region, and is closely tied to emotional tension in TCM theory.
How Qi stagnation creates tension: In TCM theory, Qi is what moves your blood, nourishes your tissues, and keeps everything supple and responsive. When Qi flows smoothly, your muscles are relaxed, your circulation is good, and your head feels clear. When Qi stagnates — like a slow traffic jam on a highway — the muscles downstream don't get the nourishment or signals they need. They tighten up. The stagnated Qi itself creates a sensation of pressure and fullness. The result: tight shoulders, a stiff neck, and a headache that feels like a band around your head.

This explains why stretching alone often doesn't help with chronic tension headaches — the muscles are the symptom, not the root cause. The real issue is the Qi stagnation that keeps the muscles locked in a tight state.

Who Gets Tension Headaches in TCM?

In TCM theory, certain people are more prone to Qi stagnation patterns. Tension headaches are particularly common in people who:

  • Hold stress in their body — you notice your jaw clenching, shoulders tensing, or fists balling up when you're under pressure
  • Work at a desk — prolonged sitting, especially with poor ergonomics, physically obstructs Qi flow through the neck and upper back
  • Are prone to worrying or overthinking — in TCM, excessive mental activity can tie up Qi in the head, preventing it from circulating freely
  • Have a "carry the world on your shoulders" personality — the TCM concept of Liver Qi stagnation links unexpressed emotions to physical tension patterns
  • Lack regular movement — physical activity is one of the most direct ways to move Qi; a sedentary lifestyle allows stagnation to build
  • Have poor sleep posture — sleeping in a position that strains the neck can create a physical blockage that compounds Qi stagnation

Tension Headache vs. Liver Fire Headache: Key Differences

It's easy to confuse tension headaches with the Liver Fire pattern (the migraine type), especially because both are linked to stress. But the quality of the pain and the associated symptoms are quite different:

Feature Tension Headache (Qi Stagnation) Liver Fire / Migraine
Pain quality Dull, pressing, band-like tightness Throbbing, pulsating, pounding
Location Bilateral, forehead, back of head, wrapping around Usually one-sided, temple area
Associated signs Neck/shoulder tension, fatigue, difficulty concentrating Nausea, light sensitivity, sound sensitivity, irritability
Emotional tone Worry, mental overload, "holding it together" Frustration, repressed anger, irritability
TCM approach Move Qi, relax the channels, release tension Clear Fire, calm the Liver, redirect energy downward

How TCM Traditionally Approaches Tension Headaches

The goal in TCM is not just to relieve the headache but to address the Qi stagnation that keeps producing tension. Here are the approaches commonly used:

Acupuncture

Acupuncture for tension headaches typically focuses on points that move Qi in the neck, shoulders, and head channels. Common points include:

  • Jian Jing (GB-21) — on the top of the shoulder, a major point for releasing neck and shoulder tension
  • Feng Chi (GB-20) — at the base of the skull, traditionally used for headaches affecting the back of the head and neck
  • Bai Hui (DU-20) — on the top of the head, used to clear the head and lift the spirit
  • He Gu (LI-4) — between the thumb and index finger, one of the most commonly used points for headache and facial pain

A 2021 meta-analysis suggested that acupuncture may be effective for tension-type headaches, with benefits that appear to persist beyond the treatment period. A 2016 Cochrane review also found evidence supporting acupuncture for tension-type headache prophylaxis.

Tui Na (Therapeutic Massage)

Tui Na is particularly well-suited to tension headaches. Unlike a general relaxation massage, Tui Na targets specific acupoints and channels with techniques designed to break up Qi stagnation. The neck, shoulders, and scalp are common focus areas. Some studies suggest that massage therapy may help reduce the frequency and intensity of tension headaches, though more research is needed.

Herbal Support

Several traditional formulas have been used for tension headaches with Qi stagnation patterns:

  • Chuan Xiong Cha Tiao San (川芎茶调散) — a classic formula traditionally used for headaches, particularly those with a wind-cold or stagnation component. It contains Ligusticum chuanxiong, an herb historically used for headache and to promote circulation.
  • Xiao Yao San (逍遥散) — traditionally used for Liver Qi stagnation with emotional symptoms. It's commonly considered for tension headaches that are closely tied to stress and mood.
  • Ge Gen Tang (葛根汤) variants — Kudzu root (Ge Gen) is traditionally used for neck and upper back tension. Some TCM practitioners recommend it for tension headaches with significant neck involvement.

These formulas should only be used under the guidance of a qualified practitioner. Self-prescribing herbal formulas without a proper TCM diagnosis carries risks.

Dietary Considerations

While tension headaches are less diet-dependent than other headache patterns, TCM dietary theory offers some general guidance for Qi stagnation:

  • Foods that move Qi: mint, basil, tangerine peel, radish, scallion, ginger, turmeric — these are considered to have a "moving" quality that supports smooth Qi flow
  • Foods to moderate: excessive dairy, fried foods, and processed foods — in TCM theory, these can create dampness that makes stagnation worse
  • Stay hydrated: dehydration can make tissues stiff and less responsive, compounding Qi stagnation
A simple kitchen remedy: In TCM tradition, a warm compress with ginger or mint tea on the neck and shoulders is sometimes suggested for mild tension headaches. The warmth helps relax the muscles, and the aromatic herbs are believed to help move stagnant Qi in the channels.

Lifestyle Approaches

Because tension headaches are so closely tied to how you hold stress, lifestyle changes are often the most impactful part of a TCM approach:

  • Movement breaks. Qi stagnation accumulates in stillness. Standing up and walking every 45 minutes, or doing a few shoulder rolls and neck stretches, can prevent stagnation from building to headache level.
  • Ergonomics. Your desk setup matters more than you think. If your screen is too low, your head is constantly tilted down — a direct physical obstruction to Qi flow through the neck.
  • Sleep position. In TCM theory, the neck should be supported in a neutral position during sleep. A pillow that's too high or too low can create chronic low-grade stagnation in the Bladder and Gall Bladder channels.
  • Stress expression. "Don't bottle it up" is not just folk wisdom — it's the core of the TCM approach to Qi stagnation. Finding a safe way to express frustration (movement, creative work, conversation) is traditionally considered preventive medicine for tension headaches.

What Research Says

Research on TCM approaches to tension headaches has grown in recent years:

  • A 2021 meta-analysis published in BMJ Open found that acupuncture may be more effective than sham acupuncture and conventional drugs for tension-type headaches, with effects maintained at follow-up.
  • A 2018 systematic review of 12 trials found that massage therapy (including Tui Na) may provide short-term relief for tension-type headaches, though higher-quality studies are needed.
  • Some Chinese-language studies have explored specific TCM herbal formulas for tension headaches classified as Qi stagnation patterns, with preliminary findings suggesting symptom improvement. As with much TCM research, larger, more rigorous trials are needed to draw firm conclusions.

This research is promising but not definitive. TCM is a complementary approach that works alongside conventional medical care — not a replacement for it.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Recognise the pattern. If your headache feels like a band around your head, comes with neck and shoulder tension, and worsens as the day progresses — you may be dealing with a Qi stagnation pattern.
  2. Address the stagnation, not just the pain. TCM approaches aim to restore flow, not just numb sensation. Movement, massage, and stress expression are as important as any treatment.
  3. Consider acupuncture. Of all the TCM tools, acupuncture has the strongest evidence base for tension headaches. If you're curious about TCM, this may be a good starting point.
  4. Don't ignore ergonomics. Your daily posture might be maintaining the very stagnation that causes your headaches. Small adjustments to your desk setup can have outsized effects.
  5. Work with a professional. A qualified TCM practitioner can identify the specific pattern affecting you and create an individualised plan that goes far beyond one-size-fits-all advice.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Seek emergency medical attention if you experience: the worst headache of your life (sometimes called a "thunderclap headache"), headache after a head injury, headache with fever and stiff neck, headache accompanied by vision changes, confusion, weakness on one side of the body, or trouble speaking. While tension headaches are generally not dangerous, new or changing headache patterns should always be evaluated by a healthcare provider to rule out other causes. TCM is a complementary approach and does not replace emergency care or proper medical diagnosis.
