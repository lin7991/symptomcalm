Migraine Diet: A TCM Guide to Foods That Trigger and Soothe Headaches

Cluster Guide · 8 min read

You know the pattern. That aged cheese on your plate — and two hours later, the familiar throbbing behind your eye. A glass of red wine at dinner that you knew you shouldn't have had. Or the skipped lunch that turned into a full-blown migraine by 4 PM.

If you live with migraines, you've probably noticed that certain foods reliably trigger attacks. But the relationship between food and migraines is more nuanced than a simple list of "bad" foods. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), what you eat — and when you eat it — is understood through the lens of your body's internal energy patterns.

This article focuses specifically on migraine dietary approaches from a TCM perspective. For a broader view of dietary triggers across all headache types — including tension, sinus, and weather-sensitive headaches — see our general guide to headaches and diet.

Why Migraines Are Different: The Unique Role of Diet

Not all headaches respond to diet the same way. Migraines — with their throbbing pain, sensitivity to light and sound, and frequent nausea — have a particularly strong dietary connection. Western research has identified over a dozen common food triggers for migraine, from tyramine in aged cheese to nitrates in processed meats.

TCM offers a framework for understanding why these foods trigger migraines in some people but not others. The answer lies in your body's core energy patterns — and different patterns react to different foods.

Key idea: In TCM, the painkiller approach is like turning off a fire alarm. A dietary approach is like finding out what started the fire. The right food adjustments work with your body's logic — not against it.

The Three Migraine Patterns: Understanding What Your Body Needs

In TCM theory, migraines can arise from several underlying patterns. The food that triggers one person's migraine may be neutral — or even helpful — for someone with a different pattern. Identifying which pattern fits your experience is the first step toward a more targeted dietary approach.

Pattern 1: Liver Yang Rising (肝阳上亢) — The Throbbing Migraine

You might have this if: Your migraine is pounding or throbbing, often on one side of the head. You feel irritable, wound-up, or hot. Bright lights and loud noises aggravate it. You may notice your face feels flushed.

TCM view: Liver Yang rising happens when Liver Qi — energy that should flow smoothly — becomes stuck and then surges upward, carrying heat into the head. Think of it like a steam pipe with too much pressure: the heat has to escape somewhere, and the head is the release valve.

Key dietary triggers: Alcohol (especially red wine), spicy foods (chilli, hot sauce), aged cheeses, chocolate, excessive coffee, and deep-fried foods. These are considered "hot" or "pungent" in TCM food energetics — they add heat and upward-moving energy to a system that already has too much of both.

Pattern 2: Phlegm-Damp Obstruction (痰湿内阻) — The Heavy, Clouded Migraine

You might have this if: Your head feels heavy, foggy, and wrapped in cotton wool. Nausea or a sensation of fullness in your stomach is common. The pain may be dull and pressing rather than sharp. Your tongue often has a thick, sticky coating.

TCM view: When the digestive system (Spleen and Stomach) struggles to transform food into clean energy, it creates "Dampness" and "Phlegm" — thick, heavy fluids that rise and cloud the head. It's like fog rolling into a valley: the clarity is gone, replaced by a heavy, suffused pressure.

Key dietary triggers: Dairy (milk, cheese, yoghurt in large amounts), fried and greasy foods, sugar and refined carbohydrates, cold raw salads, ice cream, beer, and excessive fruit juice. These foods burden the Spleen, creating the raw material for Phlegm.

Pattern 3: Blood Deficiency (血虚) — The Exhaustion Migraine

You might have this if: Your migraine comes on when you're tired, hungry, or after a stressful week. The pain is dull rather than sharp. You may feel dizzy or light-headed. Your sleep quality is poor, and your energy levels feel consistently low.

TCM view: Blood in TCM is not just the red fluid in your veins — it's the material foundation that nourishes your brain, muscles, and organs. When Blood is deficient, the head doesn't get enough nourishment. It's like a laptop running on reserve battery: everything slows down, and the system may crash under load.

Key dietary triggers: Skipping meals, restrictive diets, raw and cold foods eaten in large quantities, and foods with low nutritional density (white bread, sugary snacks). The trigger isn't a single food but a pattern of insufficient nourishment.

Pattern-by-Pattern: Foods Traditionally Used for Migraine Support

Just as certain foods may trigger migraines, others are traditionally considered supportive — when matched to the right pattern. The key is choosing foods that work with your body's dominant pattern, not against it.

For Liver Yang Rising: Cooling and Calming Foods

Think: Cooling compress on a steam pipe. The goal is to gently redirect upward-moving energy and clear heat.

Foods to emphasise: Cucumber, celery, watermelon, lettuce, mint, green tea (moderate), mung beans, tofu, pear, peppermint tea, and chrysanthemum tea (ju hua). These are classified as "cooling" in TCM food energetics.

Preparation tip: A cup of chrysanthemum tea at the first sign of a throbbing headache is a traditional go-to. Steep 5-6 dried chrysanthemum flowers in hot water for 5 minutes and sip slowly. Laboratory studies suggest chrysanthemum extracts have anti-inflammatory and vasodilatory effects, which may align with its traditional use for vascular-type headaches.

For Phlegm-Damp Obstruction: Light, Drying, and Aromatic Foods

Think: Clearing fog by opening windows and removing the moisture source. The goal is to "resolve Dampness" and lighten the digestive load.

Foods to emphasise: Barley, pumpkin, radish, daikon, celery, green tea with lemon, fresh ginger tea, turnip, seaweed, asparagus, and small amounts of vinegar. These foods are traditionally considered to "drain Dampness" in TCM.

Preparation tip: A simple ginger tea (3-4 thin slices of fresh ginger simmered in 2 cups of water for 10 minutes) is traditionally used to warm the Spleen and move fluids. A 2014 RCT in Phytotherapy Research found ginger powder as effective as sumatriptan in reducing migraine severity — the active compounds (gingerols) are also present in fresh ginger tea preparations.

For Blood Deficiency: Warm, Nourishing, and Building Foods

Think: Refuelling a car that keeps running on empty. The goal is to add nourishing density to every meal, not to restrict foods.

Foods to emphasise: Bone broth, congee (rice porridge), slow-cooked soups, oats, dates, goji berries, dark leafy greens (lightly cooked), lean red meat in moderation, eggs, yams, sweet potatoes, and black sesame seeds. Red dates (hong zao) and longan (long yan rou) are traditionally used to nourish Blood.

Preparation tip: Start your day with a warm, nourishing breakfast — oatmeal cooked with a few chopped dates and goji berries, or a simple rice congee. A warm breakfast gives the Spleen easily convertible energy. Many people with Blood Deficiency migraines find that a consistent, warm breakfast is the single most impactful dietary change they can make.

When You Eat Matters: Meal Timing and Migraine Prevention

One of the most overlooked dietary factors in migraine management is when you eat — not just what you eat. Skipping meals is one of the most consistently reported migraine triggers in Western research, and TCM offers a clear explanation for why.

In TCM theory, the Spleen and Stomach operate on a predictable energy schedule. The Stomach meridian is most active between 7 AM and 9 AM — breakfast time in the Organ Clock — and its energy naturally declines through the afternoon. When you skip meals or eat erratically, you're asking the Spleen to process food at times when its energy is already low, or leaving it idle when its energy peaks.

TCM principle: The Spleen thrives on routine. Three meals at roughly the same time each day — with the largest meal at midday when digestive energy peaks — gives the Spleen the consistent workload it needs to produce clean Qi instead of Dampness and Phlegm.

Practical timing guidelines from a TCM perspective:

  • Breakfast between 7-9 AM — Warm, cooked, and substantial enough to fuel your morning. This is Stomach time on the Organ Clock, when digestive energy is strongest.
  • Lunch as the largest meal (around 12-1 PM) — Heart time, when your body can metabolise a fuller meal. This is when most energy is available for digestion.
  • Dinner light and early (before 7 PM) — A small, easy-to-digest meal allows the Spleen to finish processing before the body shifts into rest and repair mode.
  • No snacking after 8 PM — The Spleen's energy declines through the evening. Eating late creates Dampness and disrupts sleep, both of which can trigger morning migraines.
  • Avoid going longer than 5 hours without eating — For Blood Deficiency patterns, even a small, nutritious snack (like a handful of almonds or a piece of fruit) between meals may help avoid the energy drop that can trigger a migraine.

What Research Says About Diet and Migraine

While TCM dietary principles are based on traditional clinical observation spanning centuries, some modern research supports the food-migraine connection:

  • Ginger for migraine (Phytotherapy Research, 2014): A randomized controlled trial found ginger powder as effective as sumatriptan in reducing migraine severity and duration, with fewer side effects. The active compounds (gingerols) are present in fresh ginger tea — a traditional TCM remedy for Phlegm-Damp and Cold patterns.
  • Dietary triggers systematic review (Nutrients, 2021): A comprehensive review identified alcohol, chocolate, aged cheese, and caffeine as among the most commonly reported dietary triggers in migraineurs — the same foods TCM classifies as "heating" or "Dampness-creating."
  • Meal skipping and migraine (Headache, 2016): A large epidemiological study found that skipping meals was associated with a significantly higher risk of migraine attacks — supporting the TCM emphasis on regular, consistent meal timing.
  • Chrysanthemum anti-inflammatory effects (laboratory studies): Multiple studies have shown chrysanthemum (ju hua) extracts have anti-inflammatory and vasodilatory properties, which may explain its traditional use for headache patterns involving heat and upward-moving energy.
Note on research: These studies are observational or small-scale. TCM dietary principles are traditional frameworks informed by centuries of clinical practice, not evidence-based medical guidelines. Always work with your healthcare provider for migraine management.

Practical Takeaways

🔑 Identify your pattern first: Notice whether your migraines feel throbbing and hot (Liver Yang), heavy and foggy (Phlegm-Damp), or dull and exhaustion-linked (Blood Deficiency). This determines which foods may help and which may trigger.

🍽️ Eat on a consistent schedule: Three meals at roughly the same time each day — with a warm breakfast and a light dinner — may be the single most impactful dietary change for migraine prevention.

🍵 Try a pattern-matched tea: Chrysanthemum tea for throbbing, heat-type migraines (Liver Yang). Ginger tea for heavy, foggy migraines (Phlegm-Damp). Warm bone broth or congee for exhaustion-linked migraines (Blood Deficiency).

📓 Keep a food-migraine journal: Note what you ate, when you ate, and the quality of your migraine (throbbing, heavy, dull). After 2 weeks, look for patterns — they often align with one of the three TCM patterns above.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Dietary changes may support your wellbeing, but they are not a substitute for medical care. Migraines can be disabling and require proper diagnosis and treatment. Seek immediate medical attention if you experience:

  • A sudden, severe headache — the worst of your life ("thunderclap headache")
  • Headache with fever, stiff neck, confusion, or seizure
  • Headache after a head injury
  • Headache with vision changes, weakness on one side of the body, or difficulty speaking
  • New headaches after age 50 or a significant change in your headache pattern

For general migraine management, work with both a neurologist and a qualified TCM practitioner for a coordinated, complementary approach.
