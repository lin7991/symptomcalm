Ocular Migraines in TCM: Visual Disturbances and Ancient Wisdom

Symptom Guide · 9 min read

You're reading a book and suddenly a shimmering zigzag pattern appears in the corner of your vision. It grows, pulses, and after about twenty minutes, it fades — leaving you with a dull sense that something strange just happened. Maybe a headache follows. Maybe it doesn't.

Welcome to the world of ocular migraines — a neurological phenomenon that can be as fascinating as it is unsettling. In Western medicine, ocular migraines (also called visual migraines or retinal migraines) are understood as episodes of temporary visual disturbance caused by a wave of electrical activity spreading across the visual cortex. They are usually harmless but can be alarming, especially the first time they occur.

Traditional Chinese Medicine offers a different lens. In TCM theory, the eyes are not just sense organs — they are considered "windows" into the health of internal organ systems, particularly the Liver. Visual disturbances of any kind reflect an imbalance in the organ systems that supply, nourish, and regulate the energy flowing to the head and eyes.

This article explores how TCM traditionally understands ocular migraines — not as "visual cortex misfiring" but as patterns of disharmony that you can identify, understand, and address through diet, lifestyle, and targeted self-care.

Important distinction: Ocular migraines are different from retinal migraines. Ocular migraines involve visual disturbances in both eyes (scintillating scotoma, zigzag lines, flashing lights) that usually last 5–60 minutes and may or may not be followed by a headache. Retinal migraines involve vision changes in only one eye and should always be evaluated by a healthcare provider to rule out more serious conditions.

What Exactly Is an Ocular Migraine?

Before we explore the TCM perspective, let's be clear about what Western medicine describes:

An ocular migraine (more accurately called a migraine with aura) is a temporary episode of visual disturbance that typically lasts between 5 and 60 minutes. Common visual symptoms include:

  • Scintillating scotoma — a blind spot that shimmers or flickers at its edges
  • Zigzag lines — geometric patterns that may look like fortification lines (resembling the walls of a castle)
  • Flashing lights — brief bursts of light in the visual field
  • Blurred or distorted vision — things may look wavy, out of focus, or fragmented
  • Visual snow or static — a grainy texture overlaying the entire visual field
  • Tunnel vision — a temporary narrowing of peripheral vision

For many people, these symptoms resolve completely and leave no lasting effect. For others, they are followed by a throbbing headache on one side of the head — the classic migraine sequence.

The underlying mechanism in Western neuroscience involves a phenomenon called cortical spreading depression: a wave of electrical silencing that slowly moves across the visual cortex, followed by a period of hyperactivity. This explains why the visual disturbance typically starts in one spot and gradually expands and resolves over 20–60 minutes.

From a TCM perspective, this phenomenon can be mapped onto several well-described patterns of disharmony in the organ systems that govern vision, blood flow to the head, and the smooth regulation of ascending energy.

The TCM View: The Eyes Are Connected to Everything

In TCM theory, the eyes are not isolated organs. They are intimately connected to the internal organ systems through the meridian (channel) network. Several key relationships form the foundation of the TCM understanding of ocular migraines:

The Liver Opens Into the Eyes

This is perhaps the most important TCM principle for understanding ocular migraines. The Liver is considered the organ system that "opens into the eyes" — meaning the health and function of the eyes directly reflects the state of the Liver. When the Liver is balanced, vision is clear. When it is imbalanced, the eyes may show it.

In TCM theory, the Liver has two primary functions that relate to vision:

  • Storing Blood — The Liver stores and regulates Blood volume. Liver Blood nourishes the eyes. When Liver Blood is sufficient, the eyes are moist, clear, and see well. When Liver Blood is deficient, vision may blur, feel dry, or become easily fatigued.
  • Smoothing Qi flow — The Liver is responsible for the free and smooth movement of Qi (vital energy) throughout the body. When Liver Qi is stagnant — stuck — the flow of energy to the head and eyes can become erratic, producing sudden, transient symptoms like the visual disturbances of an ocular migraine.

The Gallbladder Meridian Runs Along the Sides of the Head

The Gallbladder is the paired organ of the Liver in TCM's organ system. The Gallbladder meridian runs along the sides of the head, the outer edge of the eyes, and down the sides of the body. In TCM theory, migraines that affect one side of the head — which is the classic migraine pattern — often involve the Gallbladder meridian. Visual auras that begin in the peripheral field (especially the outer corners of vision) may also point to this channel.

The Heart Houses the Shen

The Heart is said to "house the Shen" (mind-spirit) and manifests in the complexion and the eyes. In TCM theory, bright, clear, responsive eyes are a sign that Shen is well-rooted and the Heart is balanced. Visual disturbances — especially those accompanied by anxiety, racing thoughts, or a sense of unreality — may also involve a Heart pattern.

The Kidneys and Essence

The Kidney system stores Jing (Essence), which is the deep constitutional reserve that supports the brain, bone marrow, and sensory organs. In TCM theory, the Kidneys also influence vision. When Kidney Essence declines — which happens naturally with age or more quickly with chronic stress — the sensory organs, including the eyes, may not receive enough nourishment to function optimally.

Four TCM Patterns Commonly Associated With Ocular Migraines

In TCM theory, the same symptom — visual disturbances in the form of zigzag lines and flashing lights — can arise from different underlying patterns in different people. Identifying your pattern is the first step toward meaningful self-care. Here are the four most common patterns linked to ocular migraines:

Pattern 1: Liver Yang Rising — The Classic Trigger

You might have this if: Your visual auras are triggered by stress, anger, or frustration. The flashing lights or zigzag patterns tend to appear during or after a tense situation. They may be followed by a throbbing headache on one side of the head. You may also notice that you clench your jaw, have tight shoulders, and feel irritable in general.

TCM view: Liver Yang Rising describes a state where the Liver's upward energy becomes excessive — like steam pressure building in a sealed pot. Instead of Qi flowing smoothly, it surges upward toward the head, disturbing the clear function of the eyes and brain. This upward surge creates the visual disturbances of the aura phase, followed by the congestion and pain of the headache.

Common triggers: Work deadlines, relationship conflict, repressed anger, poor sleep, alcohol consumption, and hormonal fluctuations (especially before menstruation).

Pattern 2: Liver Blood Deficiency — The Exhaustion Pattern

You might have this if: Your visual disturbances tend to happen when you're run down — after a long week of poor sleep, during a heavy menstrual period, or when you've been staring at screens for hours. The visual symptoms may be less dramatic (more like blurred vision, floaters, or visual fatigue) than the dramatic zigzag patterns of Pattern 1. You may also experience dry eyes, dizziness, pale complexion, and brittle nails.

TCM view: The Liver needs sufficient Blood to open into the eyes. When Liver Blood is depleted — from overwork, blood loss (heavy periods), or poor diet — the eyes lose their nourishment. Like a dimming light bulb when voltage drops, vision becomes unstable and prone to disturbances. This pattern may overlap with a general migraine without the dramatic visual aura.

Common triggers: Menstruation, overwork, prolonged screen time, poor diet (especially skipping meals or low iron), and chronic sleep deprivation.

Pattern 3: Liver Fire Blazing — The Pressure Cooker

You might have this if: Your visual disturbances are intense — bright flashing lights, vivid geometric patterns — and are accompanied by a pounding headache, red face, red eyes, a bitter taste in the mouth, and extreme irritability. You may also experience tinnitus (ringing in the ears) and constipation. This pattern tends to flare up after intense anger or after consuming spicy, greasy, or alcoholic foods.

TCM view: Liver Fire is an escalation of Liver Qi stagnation or Liver Yang Rising. When stagnant Qi is suppressed long enough, it generates heat. That heat rises to the head, agitating the eyes and brain. Where Liver Yang Rising is like steam pressure, Liver Fire is like a flame directly heating the system — more intense, more dramatic, and often more painful.

Common triggers: Chronic anger or frustration, alcohol, spicy foods, caffeine excess, and sleep deprivation combined with stress.

Pattern 4: Phlegm-Damp Obstructing the Orifices — The Foggy Pattern

You might have this if: Your visual disturbances are accompanied by a foggy, heavy sensation in the head — almost like your vision is swimming through murky water. You may also experience a sensation of fullness in the chest, nausea, dizziness, and a general sluggishness. Your visual symptoms may last longer than the typical 20–60 minutes, or feel more like visual "fuzz" than clear geometric patterns.

TCM view: Phlegm-Damp is a pathological fluid accumulation that can occur when the Spleen (the body's digestive and transport system) is weakened by a poor diet, excessive dairy, sugar, or processed foods. In TCM theory, Phlegm can "obstruct the clear orifices" — including the eyes and the brain — creating a sense of mental and visual fog. This pattern may overlap with Meniere's disease, vertigo, or vestibular migraines.

Common triggers: High-sugar diet, dairy products, greasy foods, overeating, damp weather, and lack of exercise.

How to Identify Your Pattern

Many people with ocular migraines do not fit neatly into a single TCM pattern. You may find that your visual disturbances sometimes follow Pattern 1 (stress-triggered) and other times Pattern 2 (exhaustion-triggered). This is normal — pattern identification is a dynamic process, not a fixed diagnosis.

Here is a practical self-assessment to help you identify which pattern may be most relevant to your experience:

Question Points Toward Pattern
Are your visual disturbances usually triggered by stress or conflict? Pattern 1 (Liver Yang Rising)
Do they tend to happen when you are exhausted or after a heavy period? Pattern 2 (Liver Blood Deficiency)
Are they accompanied by intense heat signs — red face, irritation, bitter taste? Pattern 3 (Liver Fire)
Do you feel foggy, heavy-headed, or nauseous alongside the visual symptoms? Pattern 4 (Phlegm-Damp)
Do the visual disturbances follow the same pattern every time? Pattern 1 or 3 (Liver patterns are more consistent)
Do the visual symptoms vary a lot — sometimes zigzags, sometimes blurring, sometimes floaters? Pattern 4 (Phlegm-Damp tends to be less specific)
Important: This self-assessment is for educational purposes and is not a diagnostic tool. TCM pattern identification is complex and should be done by a qualified practitioner. Use this as a starting point for discussion, not as a self-diagnosis.

TCM-Inspired Approaches for Each Pattern

The following suggestions are informed by TCM theory and traditional usage. They are not medical recommendations — always work with a healthcare provider for your specific situation.

For Liver Yang Rising (Pattern 1)

The goal is to calm the upward surge of Liver energy and promote smooth Qi flow throughout the body.

  • Acupressure point: LV-3 (Taichong) — Located on the top of the foot in the web between the first and second toes, about one finger-width back toward the ankle. This is one of the most important points in TCM for calming Liver Yang and smoothing Liver Qi. Apply firm, steady pressure for 30–60 seconds on each foot, especially when you feel stress building.
  • GB-20 (Fengchi) — Located in the hollow at the base of the skull, between the two large neck muscles. This is a master point for headaches and visual disturbances involving the head and eyes. Gently press upward into the skull base for 1–2 minutes.
  • Cooling foods: Celery, cucumber, watermelon, mint tea, and chrysanthemum tea are traditionally considered cooling and calming to Liver Yang. Reduce alcohol, caffeine, and spicy foods.
  • Movement: Gentle stretching and walking outdoors helps release stagnant Liver Qi. Aim for 20 minutes of slow, rhythmic movement daily — not intense cardio, which may aggravate Yang rising.

For Liver Blood Deficiency (Pattern 2)

The goal is to nourish Liver Blood and support the organs that produce Blood — primarily the Spleen and Stomach.

  • Acupressure point: LV-8 (Ququan) — Located on the inner knee, at the medial end of the knee crease. Traditionally used to nourish Liver Blood. Apply gentle but firm pressure for 30 seconds, three times per day.
  • SP-6 (Sanyinjiao) — Located about four finger-widths above the inner ankle, just behind the shin bone. This is a meeting point of the Spleen, Liver, and Kidney channels and is traditionally used to nourish Blood. Press firmly for 30–60 seconds.
  • Blood-nourishing foods: Dark leafy greens (spinach, kale), red dates (jujube), goji berries, beets, beef or lamb in small amounts, and black sesame seeds. These are traditionally considered to build Blood.
  • Rest: Prioritize sleep before midnight. In TCM theory, the Liver meridian is most active from 1–3 AM, and sleep during this window is critical for Liver Blood regeneration.

For Liver Fire Blazing (Pattern 3)

The goal is to clear heat and cool the Liver system. This is the pattern that most benefits from professional treatment, as Liver Fire can be stubborn.

  • Acupressure point: LV-2 (Xingjian) — Located on the foot, between the first and second toes, right at the web junction. This is the Fire-draining point on the Liver meridian. It is more cooling than LV-3 and is traditionally used to clear Liver Fire. Press firmly for 20–30 seconds.
  • LI-4 (Hegu) — Located in the web between the thumb and index finger. This is a powerful point for head and face conditions and helps to clear heat. Press firmly for 30 seconds, but avoid during pregnancy.
  • Intense cooling: Bitter greens (dandelion, endive, watercress), chrysanthemum tea, green tea (cooled), and mung bean soup. Avoid all alcohol, fried foods, red meat, and spicy dishes during flare-ups.
  • Stress management: Liver Fire almost always has a root in chronic frustration or suppressed anger. Journaling, talking with a trusted friend, or working with a counselor may be as important as any physical intervention.

For Phlegm-Damp Obstruction (Pattern 4)

The goal is to strengthen the Spleen (the body's digestive/transport system) and gradually clear the dampness that clouds the sensory organs.

  • Acupressure point: ST-40 (Fenglong) — Located on the outer lower leg, about halfway between the knee and ankle, two finger-widths from the shin bone. This is the primary point for transforming Phlegm in TCM. Press firmly for 1 minute, twice daily.
  • SP-9 (Yinlingquan) — Located on the inner lower leg, below the knee, in the depression just behind the shin bone. This point helps to resolve Dampness from the body. Press for 30–60 seconds.
  • Dampness-reducing diet: Warm, cooked foods are preferred — soups, stews, congee, steamed vegetables. Reduce: dairy, sugar, refined carbohydrates, fried foods, and cold/raw foods. Add: barley, adzuki beans, pumpkin, ginger, and turmeric.
  • Movement: Brisk walking, hiking, or any exercise that makes you lightly sweat helps to move Dampness out through the skin. 20–30 minutes of activity that breaks a light sweat is ideal.

What Research Suggests

While TCM was developed over thousands of years through clinical observation rather than controlled trials, some modern research has begun to investigate the connections between the organ systems TCM identifies and visual disturbances:

  • Acupuncture and migraine with aura: A 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis in BMJ Open (13 RCTs, 1,090 participants) found that acupuncture may reduce migraine frequency and intensity compared to sham acupuncture. While not specific to ocular migraine, this evidence supports the general approach of using acupoint stimulation for migraine disorders.
  • Liver function and visual disturbances: A 2019 study in Frontiers in Neuroscience explored the relationship between liver function markers and visual processing. While this is not direct support for TCM's "Liver opens into the eyes" theory, it points to biological pathways that link hepatic function with neurological and visual health.
  • Cortical spreading depression and Liver Yang rising: Some TCM researchers have proposed a theoretical mapping between cortical spreading depression (the neurobiological mechanism of migraine aura) and Liver Yang rising in TCM. Both describe a wave-like phenomenon that spreads slowly and produces temporary dysfunction followed by hyperactivity. This remains a hypothesis, not a proven mechanism.
  • Chrysanthemum (Ju Hua) and visual health: Chrysanthemum is a traditional TCM herb commonly used for visual disturbances, especially those involving Liver Yang or Liver Fire. Modern research has identified antioxidant and anti-inflammatory compounds in chrysanthemum that may support retinal health, though specific trials for visual migraine are lacking.
A note on research: The studies mentioned here are cited to show that some modern research aligns with TCM concepts. They are not proof that TCM "works" for ocular migraines. Always discuss any treatment approach with your healthcare provider.

When to See a Doctor

While ocular migraines are usually harmless, some visual disturbances require immediate medical attention. See a doctor right away if:

  • The visual disturbance affects only one eye (this may indicate retinal migraine or a more serious condition)
  • The visual changes last longer than 60 minutes
  • You experience sudden vision loss, even if it returns
  • The visual symptoms are accompanied by weakness, numbness, difficulty speaking, or confusion — these may indicate stroke or TIA
  • You have a headache described as "the worst of your life"
  • Your symptoms change significantly in pattern, frequency, or severity
  • This is your first episode — a medical evaluation is important to rule out other causes

If your visual disturbances have been evaluated by a doctor and diagnosed as ocular migraines, TCM approaches can be explored as a complementary tool to understand and potentially reduce the frequency of these episodes. Always keep your healthcare provider informed of any complementary approaches you are using.

Practical Takeaways

Ocular migraines are a fascinating intersection of neurology and traditional medicine. Whether you experience them with or without headache, they offer a unique window — literally — into the state of your body's internal balance.

Here is a summary of the key points from this article:

  • Ocular migraines are visual disturbances — zigzag patterns, flashing lights, blind spots — that typically last 5–60 minutes and may or may not be followed by a headache.
  • In TCM theory, the eyes are connected to the Liver — most visual disturbances involve some degree of Liver imbalance, whether Yang rising, Blood deficiency, or Fire.
  • There are at least four patterns — Liver Yang Rising (stress-triggered), Liver Blood Deficiency (exhaustion-triggered), Liver Fire (heat-triggered), and Phlegm-Damp (foggy pattern). Identifying your dominant pattern can guide self-care.
  • Acupressure points — LV-3, GB-20, and LI-4 for Liver Yang patterns; LV-8, SP-6 for Blood deficiency; LV-2 for Fire; ST-40, SP-9 for Phlegm-Damp.
  • Diet and lifestyle matter — cooling foods for Yang/Fire patterns, nourishing foods for Blood deficiency, warm/drying foods for Phlegm-Damp.
  • When in doubt, get checked — one-sided visual disturbances, symptoms lasting over 60 minutes, or neurological symptoms require immediate medical evaluation.
