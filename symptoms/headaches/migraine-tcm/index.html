Migraines and Tension Headaches: A TCM Pattern Guide

Explore · 9 min read

If you've ever had a pounding migraine or a tension headache that feels like a tight band around your skull, you know the difference in sensation is unmistakable. But what if these two types of head pain actually come from different root causes — and treating them the same way is why your current approach isn't working?

Traditional Chinese Medicine has understood this for centuries. In TCM theory, migraines and tension headaches are not just different names for the same problem. They belong to distinct pattern categories, each with its own underlying mechanism, body signals, and traditional approach.

This guide will help you understand the TCM view of headache patterns — so you can identify what's happening in your body and have a more informed conversation with your healthcare provider.

The Western View: What Pain Relief Misses

In modern medicine, migraines and tension-type headaches are treated as separate conditions. Migraines get triptans and anti-nausea medication. Tension headaches get ibuprofen, muscle relaxants, or sometimes antidepressants for prevention.

This can work — but it has a blind spot. Two people with identical migraine symptoms might respond completely differently to the same medication. Why? Because the underlying mechanism may be different even though the surface symptoms look the same.

Think of it this way: Imagine three houses with flickering lights. In one, a loose wire. In another, a faulty breaker. In a third, the grid is unstable. Replacing the lightbulb in all three is like taking the same medication for every headache — it might temporarily help, but it doesn't address what's actually wrong.

TCM takes the view that migraines and tension headaches, while both involving pain in the head, reflect different internal imbalances. The question TCM asks is not "what do you have?" but "what pattern is active in your body right now?"

A Different Lens: How TCM Sees Headache Patterns

In TCM theory, the head is called the "palace of the Yang" — it's where clear Yang energy rises to nourish the brain, senses, and consciousness. Pain in the head, from a TCM perspective, means something is blocking that upward flow: either too much Yang rushing upward out of control (as in migraines), or not enough Qi and Blood reaching the head through channels that are stuck (as in tension headaches).

This creates a useful framework for understanding headache types. Let's break down the most common patterns.

Pattern 1: Liver Yang Rising — The Migraine Connection

This is the most common TCM pattern associated with true migraines — especially those with visual disturbances, throbbing pain, and sensitivity to light and sound.

What it feels like:

  • Pounding, throbbing pain — often on one side of the head
  • Pain that feels like it's pulsing with your heartbeat
  • May be preceded by visual aura (flashing lights, zigzag patterns)
  • Sensitivity to light, sound, or smells
  • Nausea or vomiting during the attack

TCM view: Liver Yang Rising is like a pressure cooker with the lid on too tight. Stress, frustration, or lack of sleep causes Liver Qi to stagnate (see our guide on Liver Qi Stagnation). Over time, this stagnation "heats up" and the Yang energy — the active, upward-moving aspect of the Liver — surges violently to the head. The result is the pounding, pulsing pain characteristic of migraines.

What aggravates it: Stress, anger, lack of sleep, alcohol (especially red wine), bright lights, loud environments, and for some people, specific foods like aged cheese or chocolate — which TCM considers "wind-generating" foods that stir up Liver Yang.

Traditional approach: Calming the Liver, subduing Yang, and cooling the heat. Herbs like Tian Ma (Gastrodia) and Gou Teng (Uncaria) have been traditionally used for this pattern. Acupoints like LIV-3 (Taichong) and GB-20 (Fengchi) are commonly used to redirect downward the upward-flaring Liver energy.

Pattern 2: Qi Stagnation + Blood Tension — The Tension Headache Connection

Most tension headaches fall under this pattern. The sensation is different from migraines — it's a dull, squeezing, pressure-type pain rather than a throbbing one.

What it feels like:

  • Tight band sensation around the head — like wearing a hat that's too small
  • Pain that starts in the back of the head or neck and moves upward
  • Dull, aching quality rather than sharp or throbbing
  • Neck and shoulder tension that you can physically feel
  • Worsens as the day goes on

TCM view: This pattern is about obstruction, not rebellion. The Qi in the channels of the head and neck is stuck — like a traffic jam in the network of energy pathways. The muscles tighten because Qi and Blood aren't flowing freely. Unlike the violent surge of Liver Yang Rising, this is a slower, more stubborn blockage.

Pattern 3: Phlegm-Damp Obstruction — The Foggy Headache

This pattern is less talked about but surprisingly common — especially in people whose headaches come with brain fog, dizziness, and a heavy sensation in the head.

What it feels like:

  • Heavy, foggy sensation — like your head is wrapped in cotton wool
  • Dizziness or a floating feeling
  • Nausea that's not as intense as migraine nausea
  • A "fullness" in the chest or a phlegmy sensation in the throat
  • Worse in damp weather or after eating greasy food

TCM view: Phlegm in TCM is not just lung congestion — it's a metabolic concept. When the Spleen (the digestive/transformative organ in TCM) isn't functioning optimally, fluids don't get processed properly. They become "turbid" and rise to cloud the head, like mud swirling up into clear water. This Phlegm obstructs the orifices and blocks the clear Yang energy from reaching the brain.

What aggravates it: A diet high in greasy, fried, or dairy-heavy foods. Damp weather. Lack of exercise. Overeating.

Traditional approach: Transforming Phlegm and drying Dampness. Herbs like Ban Xia (Pinellia) and Chen Pi (aged tangerine peel) are traditionally used. Acupoints like ST-40 (Fenglong) — the main "Phlegm-resolving" point — and CV-12 (Zhongwan) are commonly selected.

Pattern 4: Blood Deficiency — The Exhaustion Headache

Not all headaches come from excess. Some come from deficiency — literally not enough Blood nourishing the head. These headaches are common in people with anaemia, heavy menstrual periods, or chronic fatigue.

What it feels like:

  • Dull, empty ache rather than sharp pain
  • Dizziness upon standing up quickly
  • Pale complexion and pale tongue
  • Fatigue, poor memory, difficulty concentrating
  • Headache is worse with exertion or mental work

TCM view: In TCM theory, Blood has the function of nourishing and anchoring the mind and senses. When Blood is deficient, there's not enough "fuel" to fill the vessels of the head. The head feels empty and the mind feels ungrounded. This pattern is different from Liver Yang Rising — instead of too much upward energy, there's not enough foundational substance.

What aggravates it: Blood loss (heavy periods, postpartum), poor nutrition, overwork, lack of sleep, excessive dieting.

Traditional approach: Nourishing Blood and building the foundation. Foods like dark leafy greens, beets, bone broth, and red dates (jujube) are traditionally recommended. Acupoints like ST-36 (Zusanli) and SP-6 (Sanyinjiao) are commonly used to support Blood production.

Reading the Map: Headache Location Matters in TCM

One of the most practical aspects of TCM headache analysis is location diagnosis. Where your head hurts gives direct clues about which organ system and channel is involved:

  • Temple area (side of head): Gall Bladder channel — often Liver Yang or Gall Bladder Fire. This is the classic migraine territory.
  • Forehead: Stomach channel — often Dampness or Stomach Heat. Common in Phlegm-pattern headaches or sinus-related headaches.
  • Back of head and neck: Bladder channel — often External Wind or Qi Stagnation. The classic tension headache distribution.
  • Top of the head (vertex): Liver channel — strongly associated with Liver Yang Rising. This location is a hallmark of migraines in TCM.
  • Whole head pressure: Often a combination pattern — Phlegm-Damp with Qi Stagnation. The head feels heavy all over.

Next time you have a headache, notice exactly where it hurts. That simple observation is one of the most powerful diagnostic tools in the TCM framework.

Practical Headache Approaches Based on Your Pattern

Here are some practical approaches traditionally recommended by TCM, organised by pattern. Remember: these are complementary approaches to discuss with your healthcare provider — not medical advice or a substitute for professional care.

For Migraine-Type (Liver Yang) Patterns

  • Acupressure point GB-20 (Fengchi): Located in the hollows at the base of the skull, between the two large neck muscles. Press firmly for 1-2 minutes when you feel a migraine coming on. Many people find this helps calm the upward surge of energy.
  • LIV-3 (Taichong): On the top of the foot, in the web between the big toe and second toe, about two finger-widths back. This is a primary point for redirecting Liver Yang downward.
  • Cool down: Peppermint tea or cooling foods like cucumber and watermelon are traditionally suggested. Avoid alcohol, caffeine, and spicy food when you're in a flare-up.
  • Sleep hygiene: Irregular sleep is one of the biggest triggers for Liver Yang patterns. Consistent bedtimes help keep the Liver's energy regulated.

For Tension-Type (Qi Stagnation) Patterns

  • Acupressure point GB-21 (Jianjing): Midway between the base of the neck and the tip of the shoulder, in the muscle belly. This is a master point for releasing shoulder and neck tension. Press and hold for 30 seconds while taking deep breaths.
  • LI-4 (Hegu): In the web between the thumb and index finger. This point is traditionally used to move Qi in the head and face — it's one of the most well-known acupressure points for headache relief.
  • Movement breaks: Qi stagnation accumulates in stillness. Getting up every 45 minutes to stretch, roll your shoulders, or take a short walk can prevent the stuck energy from building to headache levels.
  • Heat therapy: A warm compress on the neck and shoulders can help relax muscle tension and promote Qi flow through the affected channels.

For Phlegm-Damp Patterns

  • ST-40 (Fenglong): On the outside of the lower leg, about halfway between the knee and the ankle. This is the main TCM point for resolving Phlegm. Firm pressure here may help clear that heavy, foggy sensation.
  • Dietary shift: Reducing greasy, fried, and dairy-heavy foods is traditionally recommended. Light, simple foods like congee (rice porridge), steamed vegetables, and ginger tea may help the Spleen function better.
  • Dry warmth: A heating pad on the abdomen (CV-12 area) can support Spleen function in TCM theory.

For Blood Deficiency Patterns

  • ST-36 (Zusanli): Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width toward the outer shin. This point is traditionally used to build Qi and Blood. Rubbing it daily may help with overall energy levels.
  • SP-6 (Sanyinjiao): Four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone. This point nourishes Blood and supports the Spleen in TCM theory.
  • Iron-rich foods: Dark leafy greens, red dates, bone broth, and beets are traditionally used in TCM dietary therapy for Blood deficiency.
  • Rest: This pattern demands actual rest, not just distraction. The headache is a signal that the body needs to replenish, not just push through.
A note on acupressure: Acupressure is a gentle, non-invasive technique that you can do on yourself. It is not a replacement for professional medical care. If you have a serious health condition, are pregnant, or are unsure about technique, consult a qualified practitioner before applying acupressure.

What Research Says

Headache research is one of the better-studied areas in TCM, with several notable findings:

  • A 2020 meta-analysis published in BMJ Open found that acupuncture may be associated with reduced migraine frequency and severity compared to sham acupuncture or routine care alone.
  • A 2016 Cochrane review of acupuncture for tension-type headaches found that acupuncture could be an effective, non-pharmacological option for reducing headache frequency.
  • Some preliminary research has explored the effects of specific Chinese herbal formulas on migraine symptoms. A 2021 systematic review suggested that certain formulas traditionally used for Liver Yang patterns may offer supportive benefits, though larger, standardised trials are needed.
  • Small studies on acupressure for migraine relief have shown promising results for pain intensity reduction, particularly with GB-20 and LI-4 points.

This research is promising but not definitive. TCM is a complementary approach that works alongside conventional medical care — not a replacement for it. If you're managing chronic headaches, work with both a medical doctor and a qualified TCM practitioner who can communicate with each other about your care.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Identify your pattern. Is your headache pounding (Liver Yang) or squeezing (Qi Stagnation)? Is it worse with activity or with rest? Location and quality matter — use the comparison table above to find your likely pattern.
  2. Match your approach to your pattern. Points like GB-20 for migraines and GB-21 for tension headaches target different mechanisms. Using the wrong point might not help — but using the right one can make a noticeable difference.
  3. Track your triggers through the TCM lens. Weather changes, specific foods, emotional stress, and fatigue all correspond to specific TCM patterns. A headache journal with pattern-awareness can reveal connections you'd miss otherwise.
  4. Consider combining approaches. Acupuncture has a stronger evidence base for headache management than many other TCM applications. If you're exploring TCM for headaches, this may be the most evidence-supported starting point.
  5. Work with a qualified practitioner. A licensed TCM or acupuncture practitioner can identify your specific pattern combination — most people don't have just one pattern — and create an individualised plan that addresses your unique presentation.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Seek emergency medical attention if you experience: the worst headache of your life (sometimes called a "thunderclap headache"), headache after a head injury, headache with fever and stiff neck, headache accompanied by vision changes, confusion, weakness on one side of the body, or trouble speaking. New or changing headache patterns, especially in people over 50, should always be evaluated by a healthcare provider to rule out underlying causes. TCM is a complementary approach and does not replace emergency care or proper medical diagnosis.
