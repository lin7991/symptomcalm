Endometriosis from a TCM Perspective: Blood Stasis and Cold Patterns

Cluster Guide · 9 min read

You've been told your pelvic pain is "just bad cramps." You've had laparoscopies, hormone therapies, maybe multiple surgeries — and still the pain returns. The fertility clock is ticking, and every failed attempt feels like another door closing. If this sounds familiar, you're not imagining it, and you're not alone.

Endometriosis affects roughly 1 in 10 women of reproductive age worldwide — that's nearly 190 million women. Yet the average time to diagnosis is 7 to 10 years. Many are told their pain is normal, dismissed as "anxiety," or cycled through treatments that manage symptoms but don't address the underlying question: why is this happening, and what can be done about it?

In Western medicine, endometriosis is defined as tissue similar to the uterine lining (endometrium) growing outside the uterus — typically on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, and pelvic lining. These lesions respond to hormonal cycles, causing inflammation, scarring, and pain. Treatment usually focuses on hormonal suppression (birth control, GnRH agonists) or surgical removal of lesions. Both can be effective, but recurrence rates are high — studies suggest up to 40-50% within 5 years after surgery.

Traditional Chinese Medicine has studied patterns of pelvic pain and infertility for centuries. While TCM doesn't have a direct historical term for endometriosis, it offers a remarkably coherent framework for understanding what's happening — and it starts with a single concept: Blood Stasis.

The TCM View: Endometriosis as Blood Stasis (血瘀)

Picture a river. When it flows freely, the water is clear, the ecosystem thrives, and nothing backs up. Now picture a section of that river where debris has accumulated — fallen branches, sediment, rocks. The water slows. It pools. It stagnates. Over time, the stagnation creates pressure, inflammation, and damage to the surrounding banks.

In TCM theory, Blood Stasis (Xue Yu, 血瘀) works the same way in the pelvic cavity. Blood that should flow smoothly through the uterus and surrounding channels becomes stagnant. This stagnation creates a physical mass — the endometrial lesions themselves — and produces the hallmark symptoms of endometriosis: sharp, fixed, stabbing pain that worsens during menstruation.

The key insight in TCM is that Blood Stasis is rarely a standalone condition. It's usually the result of something else that disrupted the flow in the first place. For endometriosis, two contributing factors are especially common: Cold in the Uterus and Liver Qi Stagnation. And there's often an underlying constitutional weakness — Kidney Deficiency — that creates the terrain for these patterns to take hold.

A note on terminology: In TCM theory, "Blood Stasis" doesn't mean your blood is literally clotted in the way a bruise is. It's a functional concept — describing a state where blood circulation in a specific area (here, the pelvis) is sluggish, congested, or obstructed. The endometrial lesions are seen as both a cause and a consequence of this stagnation.

Key TCM Patterns Associated with Endometriosis

In TCM, the specific combination of your symptoms tells practitioners which pattern — or more likely, which combination of patterns — is involved. Here are the patterns most commonly associated with endometriosis:

1. Blood Stasis with Cold Retention (寒凝血瘀)

Signs: Severe, sharp, stabbing pelvic pain that feels worse with cold exposure and better with heat. Pain during menstruation that's intense — often described as "knife-like" or "twisting." Dark menstrual blood with large clots. Cold hands and feet. A strong preference for warm drinks and warm environments. The pain may radiate to the lower back and thighs.

TCM view: Cold congeals Blood — think of how cold temperatures thicken oil or slow a stream. When Cold invades the pelvic region (from exposure to cold environments, cold-weather clothing that leaves the abdomen exposed, cold foods and drinks consumed habitually, or constitutional Cold), it constricts the Blood vessels and slows circulation in the uterus and pelvic cavity. Blood that's already sluggish from endometriosis becomes even more stagnant, creating intense, stabbing pain.

Traditional approach: Warming the uterus and dispelling Cold while resolving Blood Stasis. Moxibustion (burning mugwort near acupoints on the lower abdomen) is a classic technique for this pattern — the deep, penetrating heat is believed to help break up Cold-related stagnation. Herbal formulas like Wen Jing Tang (Warm the Menses Decoction) are traditionally used, combining warming herbs with those that move Blood.

2. Liver Qi Stagnation with Blood Stasis (气滞血瘀)

Signs: Pelvic pain that fluctuates with stress levels. Premenstrual worsening of pain. Distending, pressing pain in the lower abdomen. Irritability, mood swings, and breast tenderness before the period. The pain may ease once menstrual flow begins. A tendency to sigh frequently or feel a "lump in the throat" when upset.

TCM view: The Liver is responsible for the free and smooth flow of Qi throughout the body — think of it as the body's traffic controller. When stress, repressed emotions, or frustration obstruct this flow (Liver Qi Stagnation), the Qi can't move Blood effectively. Since Qi moves Blood, stagnant Qi leads directly to stagnant Blood. For endometriosis, this pattern is especially relevant because stress is a known trigger for flare-ups — and many women report their pain is significantly worse during high-stress periods.

Traditional approach: Soothing the Liver, moving Qi, and activating Blood circulation. Acupuncture points like LV3 (Taichong), LI4 (Hegu), and SP6 (Sanyinjiao) are traditionally stimulated. Herbal approaches often include modified versions of Xiao Yao San (Free and Easy Wanderer) combined with Blood-moving herbs.

3. Kidney Deficiency with Blood Stasis (肾虚血瘀)

Signs: Chronic, lingering pelvic pain that may be dull rather than sharp. Lower back weakness or aching. Fatigue, especially around the time of menstruation. Frequent urination or weak stream. Dizziness, tinnitus (ringing in the ears), or poor memory. Fertility challenges. A history of multiple surgeries or long-standing disease.

TCM view: The Kidneys in TCM store your constitutional essence (Jing) — your inherited "battery charge" that fuels growth, reproduction, and aging. Long-standing endometriosis, multiple surgeries, hormonal treatments, and chronic pain all deplete Kidney energy over time. When Kidney Qi is deficient, the body lacks the power to move Blood effectively — like a river with a weak current. The result is that Blood Stasis becomes chronic and difficult to clear. This pattern is especially common in women who've had endometriosis for many years or who've undergone multiple surgical procedures.

Traditional approach: Tonifying the Kidneys while gently resolving Blood Stasis. This is a more nuanced approach — strong Blood-moving herbs can deplete Qi further, so the treatment focuses on strengthening the foundation while gradually addressing the stagnation. Dietary support (warming, nourishing foods) and lifestyle adjustments are considered important.

How Endometriosis Differs from "Regular" Menstrual Cramps in TCM

This distinction matters. Many women have Blood Stasis during their period — it's a common pattern for ordinary menstrual cramps. But endometriosis-related Blood Stasis has two distinguishing features in TCM theory:

  1. Chronicity and depth: The Stasis isn't just in the uterus — it's in the deeper channels (the "extraordinary vessels," or Qi Jing Ba Mai) that govern the pelvic cavity. This is why endometriosis pain can persist even when you're not on your period, and why it's harder to resolve than ordinary cramps.
  2. Structural change: In TCM terms, the endometrial implants represent a transformation of Stagnant Blood into a semi-solid "mass" or "accumulation" (症瘕, Zheng Jia). This is considered a deeper level of Stasis than simple congestion — it requires a more sustained, multi-faceted approach.

TCM Approaches That May Be Considered for Endometriosis Support

These are traditional approaches — not medical recommendations — that some women explore alongside conventional care:

Acupuncture

Acupuncture is one of the most commonly studied TCM modalities for endometriosis-related pain. Some research suggests it may help by modulating pain signaling pathways and improving pelvic circulation. Points commonly selected in TCM for endometriosis support include:

  • CV4 (Guanyuan) — On the lower abdomen, traditionally used to warm the uterus and build foundational Qi
  • CV6 (Qihai) — The "Sea of Qi," often stimulated to support pelvic energy
  • ST29 (Guilai) — Located near the uterus, traditionally used to regulate pelvic blood flow
  • SP6 (Sanyinjiao) — A key point for women's health, believed to harmonize the Liver, Spleen, and Kidney meridians
  • BL23 (Shenshu) — On the lower back, associated with Kidney support
Important: Acupuncture should only be performed by a licensed, qualified practitioner. Needle placement for endometriosis involves deeper points in the abdominal region and should not be attempted as self-treatment.

Herbal Support (Traditional Formulas)

In TCM, herbal formulas are typically customized to the individual's pattern diagnosis. Some well-known formulas that are traditionally modified for endometriosis-type patterns include:

  • Gui Zhi Fu Ling Wan (桂枝茯苓丸) — A classic formula for pelvic Blood Stasis with Cold, traditionally used for uterine masses and stagnant pelvic circulation
  • Shao Fu Zhu Yu Tang (少腹逐瘀汤) — "Dispel Stasis in the Lower Abdomen Decoction," used for Cold-related Blood Stasis in the pelvic region
  • Xue Fu Zhu Yu Tang (血府逐瘀汤) — "Drive Out Stasis from the Blood Mansion," for Qi Stagnation combined with Blood Stasis

Moxibustion

Moxibustion — the burning of dried mugwort (Ai Ye, 艾叶) near acupoints — is traditionally used to warm the pelvic region and promote Blood circulation. For endometriosis with Cold retention, moxibustion on the lower abdomen (CV4, CV6) is a commonly recommended supportive technique.

Dietary Considerations (TCM-Inspired)

Dietary approaches from a TCM perspective focus on reducing inflammatory load, warming the system, and supporting Blood circulation:

  • Warm and cook your food. Raw salads, cold drinks, and ice cream are minimized — TCM theory suggests cold foods constrict circulation and worsen Blood Stasis. Warm soups, stews, cooked grains, and room-temperature water are preferred.
  • Incorporate warming spices. Ginger (fresh, in tea or cooking), turmeric, cinnamon, black pepper, and cloves are traditionally used to promote circulation and dispel Cold.
  • Include Blood-nourishing foods. Cooked dark leafy greens (kale, spinach, Swiss chard), bone broth, black beans, beets, goji berries, and small amounts of high-quality animal protein (if you eat it) are traditionally associated with building Blood.
  • Reduce inflammatory foods. While not a traditional TCM concept, modern TCM practitioners often advise reducing processed foods, refined sugar, and excessive dairy, as these are believed to contribute to Dampness and Phlegm — which can combine with Blood Stasis.

What the Research Says

Modern research on TCM approaches for endometriosis is growing, though much of it is preliminary:

  • A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis suggested that acupuncture may reduce pain intensity and improve quality of life in women with endometriosis, though the authors noted variability in study design and the need for higher-quality trials.
  • Some laboratory studies have explored the effects of individual herbs commonly used in Blood Stasis formulas — such as Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis), Chuan Xiong (Ligusticum chuanxiong), and Tao Ren (Prunus persica) — on pelvic circulation and inflammatory markers. These studies are preclinical and should not be interpreted as clinical evidence of efficacy.
  • A 2017 Cochrane review of Chinese herbal medicine for endometriosis found that the evidence was insufficient to draw firm conclusions due to methodological limitations in the available studies, though some trials reported improvements in pain and pregnancy rates.
  • Neuroendocrine research has suggested that acupuncture may modulate the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis and influence pain signaling pathways, which could be relevant to endometriosis-related pain.

This research is promising but preliminary. TCM approaches are best used as complementary tools alongside — not in place of — proper gynecological care, including regular monitoring by a gynecologist or reproductive specialist.

Key message: Evidence for TCM in endometriosis is still emerging. If you choose to explore acupuncture or herbal medicine, do so with a licensed practitioner in collaboration with your medical team. Do not stop or reduce prescribed medications without consulting your doctor.

Practical Takeaways

If you're navigating endometriosis and curious about what TCM might offer, here are some steps to discuss with your healthcare provider:

  1. Find a qualified practitioner. Look for a licensed TCM practitioner with experience in women's health and endometriosis specifically. Ask about their experience with your condition — endometriosis requires more advanced pattern differentiation than general menstrual complaints.
  2. Bring your medical records. A good TCM practitioner will want to see your diagnostic laparoscopy results, ultrasound reports, and hormone panel. They should work with your gynecologist, not in isolation.
  3. Track your symptoms carefully. Pain scores, cycle timing, blood quality, emotional state, diet, and stress levels — the more detail you provide, the more precisely a TCM practitioner can identify your pattern.
  4. Consider combining approaches. Many women find the best results using Western diagnostics (laparoscopy, imaging) alongside TCM supportive care. This is not about choosing one over the other — it's about using both where each excels.
  5. Be patient with the process. Endometriosis is a chronic condition. In TCM, resolving deep Blood Stasis takes time — typically months of consistent treatment. Dramatic results in a few sessions are rare for this condition.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Endometriosis requires proper medical diagnosis and monitoring. Seek medical attention immediately if you experience any of the following:

  • Sudden, severe pelvic or abdominal pain
  • Pain accompanied by fever, nausea, or vomiting
  • Heavy bleeding (soaking through a pad or tampon every hour for 2+ hours)
  • Difficulty becoming pregnant after 12 months of trying (or 6 months if over 35)
  • Pain during or after intercourse that is new or worsening
  • Pain with bowel movements or urination during your period

Endometriosis lesions can cause adhesions, ovarian cysts (endometriomas), and fertility challenges that require medical or surgical management. TCM is a complementary approach — it does not replace proper gynecological care, surgical evaluation when needed, or fertility treatment such as IVF where indicated.
