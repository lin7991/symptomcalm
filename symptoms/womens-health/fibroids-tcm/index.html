Uterine Fibroids in TCM: Blood Stasis, Phlegm & Gentle Support

Symptom Guide · 8 min read

Your periods have become heavier. There's a dull ache in your lower abdomen that wasn't there before. Maybe you feel pressure in your pelvis, or notice your belly seems a bit fuller than it used to. Your gynecologist runs an ultrasound and says the word: fibroids.

The diagnosis comes with options — watchful waiting, hormonal medication, or surgery. But what often goes unsaid is why fibroids develop in the first place, and whether there's anything you can do to support your body through the process.

From a Traditional Chinese Medicine perspective, uterine fibroids are understood as a type of mass formation (often called zheng jia or ji ju) that arises when the body's circulation of Qi, Blood, and fluids becomes chronically impaired. The two central TCM patterns involved — Blood Stasis and Phlegm Accumulation — offer a framework for understanding how fibroids form, and what gentle daily approaches may help support the body's natural self-regulation.

Fibroids from a Western Lens: A Quick Overview

Uterine fibroids (also called leiomyomas) are benign growths that develop in or on the muscular wall of the uterus. They are remarkably common — an estimated 70–80% of women will develop fibroids by age 50, though not all experience symptoms. Their growth is driven primarily by estrogen and progesterone, which is why fibroids often shrink after menopause when hormone levels decline.

Common symptoms include:

  • Heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia)
  • Pelvic pressure or a feeling of fullness
  • Lower back pain or abdominal discomfort
  • Frequent urination or difficulty emptying the bladder (when fibroids press on the bladder)
  • Constipation or bloating (when fibroids press on the bowel)
  • Pain during intercourse

Conventional medical approaches range from monitoring (for asymptomatic fibroids) to medications that manage bleeding, hormonal therapies (GnRH agonists), and procedures like uterine artery embolization, myomectomy (surgical removal), or hysterectomy. While these options are effective for symptom management, many women also look for complementary ways to address the underlying factors that may contribute to fibroid formation.

The TCM View: When Qi, Blood, and Fluids Stall

In TCM theory, the uterus is part of the Uterus (Bao Gong) system, which depends on a steady flow of Qi and Blood, especially during the menstrual cycle. Think of the uterus as a fertile field that needs constant irrigation — Qi is the force that moves the water (Blood), and when the flow is smooth, the field stays healthy. When Qi becomes stuck, Blood follows suit, and over time, stagnation creates areas where excess fluids and tissue can accumulate.

Analogy: Imagine a river that normally flows clear and steady. If a log (Qi stagnation) lodges in one spot, sediment (Blood Stasis) begins to collect around it. Over time, debris and mud (Phlegm) build up on top of the sediment, forming a mound that changes the river's course. The log is the initial obstruction, the sediment is stagnant Blood, and the mud is the Phlegm that solidifies around it — together they create a mass that wasn't there before.

Three interconnected factors are traditionally understood to contribute to this process:

  1. Liver Qi Stagnation — Chronic stress, frustration, or emotional suppression can impair the Liver's ability to keep Qi moving smoothly. When Qi flow is disrupted in the Liver channel (which reaches the pelvic region and the reproductive organs), the entire pelvic circulation becomes sluggish.
  2. Spleen Qi Deficiency — The Spleen in TCM governs the transformation and transportation of fluids. When Spleen function is weakened (by poor diet, overwork, or worry), fluids don't get processed properly and instead accumulate as Phlegm — a thick, turbid substance that can combine with stagnant Blood to form masses.
  3. Blood Stasis — This is the direct precursor to mass formation. When Qi stagnation has persisted for months or years, Blood eventually becomes stagnant too. Unlike a simple bruise that resolves, chronic Blood Stasis in the pelvic region creates an environment where fibroids can take root and grow.

Pattern 1: Qi Stagnation with Blood Stasis (The Most Common Pattern)

This pattern describes the early-to-mid stage of fibroid development, where congestion has built up but hasn't yet solidified into dense tissue.

You might recognize this pattern if:

  • Your periods are heavy with dark or clotted blood
  • You experience cramping pain that feels "stabbing" or fixed in one spot
  • The pain worsens before your period and eases after bleeding starts
  • You tend to feel irritable, easily frustrated, or emotionally "stuck"
  • You have breast tenderness before your period (a classic Liver Qi stagnation sign)

What's happening in TCM terms: The Liver's job of smoothing Qi flow has been compromised, likely by chronic stress. Qi stagnation in the pelvic region slows Blood circulation in the uterus, creating a congested environment. Over months to years, this congestion becomes Blood Stasis — a condition where blood doesn't circulate properly, like a slow-moving stream where silt builds up. The fibroid begins as a region of stagnation that gradually organizes into a defined mass.

Pattern 2: Phlegm-Damp Accumulation with Blood Stasis

This pattern involves a significant fluid metabolism component. When the Spleen is weak, it cannot transform fluids efficiently, leading to Phlegm — a dense, turbid substance that combines with Blood Stasis to create firmer, more established masses.

You might recognize this pattern if:

  • Fibroids are multiple or large in size
  • You feel heavy or bloated, especially after eating
  • You tend to carry excess weight, particularly around the abdomen
  • You have a sensation of "fullness" or heaviness in the pelvis
  • Your discharge is thick or cloudy
  • You tend to feel sluggish, foggy-brained, or lethargic
  • You crave sweet or greasy foods

What's happening in TCM terms: The Spleen's inability to transform fluids creates Phlegm, which descends into the Lower Jiao (the pelvic region). Here it encounters Blood Stasis (from Liver Qi stagnation or Cold invasion), and the two combine into a denser, more stubborn mass. This pattern is often harder to shift because it involves both a circulation problem (Blood Stasis) and a metabolic inefficiency (Phlegm).

Pattern 3: Cold-Damp with Blood Stasis

In some cases, exposure to cold — either environmental (living in a cold climate) or constitutional (a tendency toward Cold in the Uterus) — contributes to fibroid formation by constricting circulation and congealing Blood.

You might recognize this pattern if:

  • Your periods are painful with a "cold" sensation in the lower abdomen
  • Applying warmth (heating pad, hot water bottle) significantly eases your cramping
  • Your flow is dark, clotted, and slow to start
  • You tend to feel cold generally, especially in your hands and feet
  • You have a pale complexion and a low-energy baseline

What's happening in TCM terms: In TCM theory, Cold contracts and congeals. When Cold invades the Uterus — whether from insufficient protection during menstruation, exposure to cold environments, or a constitutional Yang deficiency — it causes Blood to move sluggishly. Over time, congealed Blood forms masses.

Gentle Approaches Traditionally Used in TCM

It's important to note that TCM does not claim to "remove" or "shrink" fibroids through lifestyle approaches. Rather, TCM offers a framework for addressing the underlying patterns that may create an environment conducive to fibroid growth. The emphasis is on gentle, sustained support rather than aggressive intervention.

Movement and Qi Flow

Since Qi Stagnation is a core contributor to Blood Stasis, gentle movement that encourages circulation in the pelvic area is traditionally emphasized. This doesn't mean intense exercise — in fact, overexertion can deplete Qi further. What's more traditionally recommended is:

  • Walking — 20–30 minutes daily to support general Qi circulation
  • Gentle yoga — poses that open the hips and pelvic region, such as Butterfly (Baddha Konasana), Cat-Cow, and Child's Pose
  • Abdominal self-massage — clockwise circular massage around the navel, gradually expanding outward, to support the movement of Qi and Blood in the lower abdomen
  • Tai Chi or Qigong — slow, flowing movements that are traditionally used in TCM to move Qi without depleting it

Important: If you have large fibroids or heavy bleeding, consult your healthcare provider before starting any new exercise or self-massage routine. Certain yoga inversions and deep abdominal pressure may not be appropriate for your situation.

Dietary Considerations from a TCM Perspective

Food choices in TCM are traditionally used to either support circulation, strengthen the Spleen's ability to process fluids, or warm the Uterus — depending on the underlying pattern.

  • For all patterns: Minimize cold, raw, and iced foods and drinks. In TCM theory, cold foods impair Spleen function and can worsen Blood Stasis by constricting circulation.
  • For Qi Stagnation + Blood Stasis: Foods traditionally considered to move Qi and invigorate Blood include turmeric, ginger, scallions, chives, saffron, and small amounts of vinegar in cooking. Leafy greens like spinach and kale support Blood nourishment.
  • For Phlegm-Damp patterns: Foods that support Spleen function and reduce Phlegm include barley, adzuki beans, pumpkin, celery, mushrooms, and light broths. Minimize dairy, fried foods, sugar, and excessive fats — these are traditionally considered Phlegm-producing.
  • For Cold-Damp patterns: Warming spices like cinnamon, ginger, nutmeg, and black pepper are traditionally used. Cooked foods and soups are preferred over raw vegetables.

Herbs Traditionally Used in TCM Formulations

TCM herbal formulas for fibroids typically focus on moving Blood Stasis, transforming Phlegm, and softening hard masses. These formulas are prescribed by licensed TCM practitioners based on a detailed pattern diagnosis and should never be self-prescribed.

Some commonly used herbs in fibroid-related formulas include:

  • Gui Zhi (Ramulus Cinnamomi) — traditionally used to warm and promote Blood circulation in the pelvic region
  • Fu Ling (Poria) — traditionally used to drain Dampness and support Spleen function
  • Dan Shen (Salvia miltiorrhiza) — traditionally used to invigorate Blood circulation and address Blood Stasis
  • Tao Ren (Persica semen) and Hong Hua (Carthami flos) — a classic pair traditionally used to break up Blood Stasis
  • Bie Jia (Trionycis carapax) — traditionally used to soften hard masses

⚠️ Critical safety note: Many Blood-invigorating herbs are CONTRAINDICATED during pregnancy and heavy menstruation. Never take TCM herbs without a qualified practitioner who has done a full pattern diagnosis and understands your complete medical picture.

What Research Says

While research on TCM approaches to uterine fibroids is still developing, some studies offer interesting insights:

  • A 2021 systematic review in Medicine (20 studies, n=2,106) found that Chinese herbal medicine combined with conventional treatment may be associated with improved fibroid-related symptoms and reduced fibroid volume compared to conventional treatment alone. The authors noted that the quality of evidence was limited by small sample sizes and methodological variability.
  • A 2019 meta-analysis in BMC Complementary Medicine and Therapies (9 RCTs, n=818) reported that acupuncture may help reduce heavy menstrual bleeding and pain associated with fibroids, though the review called for larger, better-designed trials.
  • Research from a 2022 study in Journal of Ethnopharmacology examined the pharmacological effects of Gui Zhi Fu Ling Wan (a classic TCM formula for pelvic Blood Stasis) on uterine fibroid cells in vitro, finding that the formula may modulate estrogen receptor expression and inhibit cell proliferation. These are preliminary findings and do not constitute clinical recommendations.
  • The relationship between chronic stress (Liver Qi Stagnation) and fibroid risk has been explored in observational research. A 2017 prospective study in Epidemiology (n=3,230) found that women reporting high levels of perceived stress had a 27% higher risk of fibroid diagnosis over a 7-year follow-up period.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Know your pattern. Use the three pattern descriptions above to get a sense of which imbalance may be most relevant to your situation. Blood Stasis + Qi Stagnation is the most common, but Phlegm and Cold components change the approach significantly.
  2. Start with gentle circulation. The most accessible interventions are daily walking, gentle hip-opening stretches, and warm abdominal self-massage. These support Qi flow without depleting energy.
  3. Warm your center. In TCM theory, the Uterus thrives on warmth. Avoid cold drinks, especially during your period. A warm compress on the lower abdomen during downtime is a simple way to encourage local circulation.
  4. Support your Spleen. If you tend toward bloating, heaviness, or Phlegm, consider reducing dairy, sugar, and fried foods for 2–3 weeks and notice whether your digestive experience (and your overall sense of lightness) shifts.
  5. Don't self-prescribe herbs. Blood-moving herbs are powerful and can be dangerous if used incorrectly — especially if you're trying to conceive or have heavy bleeding. Work with a licensed TCM practitioner for customized herbal support.
  6. Track your cycle. Keeping a symptom diary — menstrual flow, pain levels, emotional state, and dietary patterns — can help both you and your healthcare providers see patterns that might otherwise go unnoticed.

When to See a Doctor

Uterine fibroids require regular monitoring by a gynecologist. Consult a healthcare provider if:

  • You experience extremely heavy bleeding (soaking through a pad or tampon every hour for several consecutive hours)
  • You have severe, debilitating pelvic pain
  • You experience sudden, sharp abdominal pain that may indicate fibroid degeneration or torsion
  • You have difficulty emptying your bladder or experience sudden changes in bowel habits
  • You are trying to conceive and fibroids may be affecting fertility
  • Your symptoms worsen or change significantly
  • You experience bleeding between periods or after menopause

Regular ultrasound monitoring is standard for fibroid management, and any complementary approaches should be discussed with your gynecologist to ensure they are safe alongside your current medical care.
