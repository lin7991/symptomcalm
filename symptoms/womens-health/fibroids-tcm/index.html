Ocular Migraines and Visual Aura: A TCM Perspective

Symptom Guide · 10 min read

⚠️ Medical Emergency Note: If you experience sudden vision loss, visual disturbances accompanied by slurred speech, weakness on one side of your body, or the worst headache of your life, seek emergency care immediately. These can be signs of a stroke or other serious neurological condition. This article is for people who have already been diagnosed with ocular migraines by a healthcare professional.

The zig-zag lines appear without warning. Or maybe it's a shimmering, crescent-shaped patch in your peripheral vision that slowly expands over 20 minutes, leaving a blind spot in its wake. Your vision clears, and then — sometimes — the headache arrives. Sometimes it doesn't.

Your doctor calls it an ocular migraine (or more accurately, a migraine with aura). The explanation you get is often brief: "They're generally harmless." Which leaves the real question unanswered — why does this keep happening? And is there anything you can do to reduce how often they come?

From a Traditional Chinese Medicine perspective, visual aura is not random neurological noise. It follows specific patterns that involve the Liver, Gallbladder, and the concept of Internal Wind — an ancient framework that maps surprisingly well onto modern neuroscience.

What Exactly Is an Ocular Migraine?

In Western medicine, an ocular migraine (migraine with aura) is understood as a type of migraine where the classic headache is preceded — or sometimes replaced — by visual disturbances. These can include:

  • Scintillating scotoma — a shimmering, zig-zag pattern that expands across your field of vision
  • Blind spots — areas where vision temporarily disappears
  • Flashes of light — brief, bright sparkles or geometric patterns
  • Blurred or distorted vision — objects may appear to warp or shimmer

The underlying mechanism is believed to be cortical spreading depression — a wave of electrical activity that slowly travels across the visual cortex (the part of your brain that processes sight), followed by a period of depressed activity. This wave typically moves at about 3 mm per minute, which explains why the visual disturbance slowly expands across your field of vision over 20–60 minutes before resolving.

The TCM View: Liver, Gallbladder, and the Concept of Internal Wind

In TCM theory, the visual disturbances of an ocular migraine are closely linked to three interconnected systems: the Liver, the Gallbladder, and a concept called Internal Wind.

The Liver in TCM has a special relationship with the eyes. One of the Liver's core functions is to store Blood and ensure it reaches the eyes to support clear vision. When Liver function is balanced, vision is sharp and steady. When Liver energy becomes disturbed — whether through chronic stress, poor sleep, or dietary factors — its upward-reaching energy can carry disturbance to the head and eyes.

The Gallbladder meridian runs up the side of the head, branches to the area around the eyes, and is the Liver's paired organ. In TCM, the Gallbladder governs decision-making and the ability to "see clearly" in a metaphorical sense — and its meridian pathway literally influences the physical eye area. When Gallbladder Fire or Damp-Heat arise, they often affect the visual system.

Analogy: Think of Internal Wind like a sudden gust of wind that stirs up a still pond. The pond is your visual system — normally clear and calm. When Internal Wind rises (typically from Liver Yang or Liver Fire), it "stirs" the pond, creating ripples: shimmering lights, zig-zag patterns, and visual disturbances that come and go unpredictably. Just as wind in nature is sudden, moving, and changeable, Internal Wind produces symptoms that are transient and shifting.

Internal Wind is a TCM concept that describes sudden, moving symptoms that appear and then disappear — tremors, spasms, dizziness, and visual aura. It's called "Wind" because its behavior mirrors natural wind: unpredictable, fast-moving, and affecting the upper body (head, eyes, face) first.

The most common source of Internal Wind in TCM is an imbalance in the Liver. Think of it this way:

  • Chronic stress creates Liver Qi Stagnation — like a highway where traffic has stopped moving
  • Prolonged stagnation generates heat — Liver Fire
  • Heat rises naturally (like steam), carrying that disturbance upward
  • When the heat reaches the head and eyes with enough force, it creates Internal Wind

Pattern 1: Liver Yang Rising with Internal Wind (Most Common)

This is the most frequent TCM pattern associated with ocular migraines. It describes a state where Liver energy has become excessive and rising, like pressure building in a sealed container. When it reaches a tipping point, it "releases" as Internal Wind.

You might recognize this pattern if:

  • Your visual aura tends to follow periods of high stress or pent-up frustration
  • You also experience tension in your neck, shoulders, and jaw (the Liver meridian runs through these areas)
  • You're prone to irritability or feeling "wound up"
  • The aura may be followed by a throbbing headache on one or both sides of the head
  • You notice your symptoms more on busy, high-pressure days

What's happening in TCM terms: Liver Qi becomes stagnant from stress → the stagnation generates heat → the heat rises, carrying disturbance to the head → the Gallbladder channel (which branches to the side of the head and eye area) gets "scorched" → Internal Wind appears as the visual aura.

Modern parallel: This pattern maps closely to what neuroscience describes as sympathetic nervous system overactivation. When the stress response is chronically engaged, blood flow patterns in the brain shift, and the threshold for cortical spreading depression — the wave underlying visual aura — may be lowered.

Pattern 2: Liver Blood Deficiency

While Pattern 1 involves excess and rising activity, Pattern 2 involves deficiency — not enough resources to nourish the visual system properly.

In TCM theory, Liver Blood is responsible for nourishing the eyes and supporting clear vision. The classic TCM saying goes: "When the Liver receives Blood, the eyes can see." When Liver Blood is insufficient, the visual system becomes like a computer running on low power — flickering, glitching, unstable.

You might recognize this pattern if:

  • Your visual disturbances tend to happen when you're exhausted, sleep-deprived, or after your period (for women)
  • You also experience dry eyes, blurred vision, or sensitivity to light
  • You feel dizzy or lightheaded when standing up quickly
  • Your complexion tends to be pale or sallow
  • The aura is more likely to be followed by a dull, generalized headache rather than a sharp throbbing pain

What's happening in TCM terms: Blood is insufficient to reach the eyes and moisten the visual system. Without adequate nourishment, the eyes become vulnerable to disturbances. The visual cortex, in this model, is like a garden that hasn't been watered — the plants (neurons) are more likely to misfire.

Modern parallel: There's growing research on the relationship between iron deficiency, anemia, and migraine frequency. A 2020 systematic review in Headache: The Journal of Head and Face Pain found that iron deficiency anemia was significantly associated with increased migraine prevalence — particularly in women. While iron metabolism is not the same as Liver Blood in TCM, the pattern of insufficient resources affecting neurological and visual health shows a meaningful parallel.

Pattern 3: Gallbladder Fire and Damp-Heat

The Gallbladder is the Liver's paired organ, and its meridian pathway directly influences the sides of the head and the eye area. When Gallbladder Fire or Damp-Heat arises — often from a combination of dietary factors and unresolved stress — it can manifest as visual disturbances and headaches.

You might recognize this pattern if:

  • The visual disturbances are accompanied by a bitter taste in your mouth
  • You feel nauseous during or after the aura
  • The headache (if it follows) is on the side of your head, near the temple
  • You tend to eat a diet high in greasy, spicy, or rich foods
  • You feel irritable, with a "short fuse"

What's happening in TCM terms: Heat in the Gallbladder channel rises to the head, and as it passes through the eye-area branches of the meridian, it disrupts normal visual function. The bitter taste and nausea are classic signs of Gallbladder heat descending into the digestive system.

Practical Approaches Based on Your Pattern

Since ocular migraines can stem from different underlying imbalances, the most effective approach in TCM is to match the strategy to your pattern. Here are pattern-specific approaches traditionally used in TCM:

For Liver Yang Rising with Wind (Pattern 1)

  • Acupressure point — LV-3 (Tai Chong): Located on the top of your foot, in the hollow between the big toe and second toe, about two finger-widths back. This is a primary point for calming Liver Yang and subduing Internal Wind. Press firmly with your thumb for 1–2 minutes on each foot, taking slow, deep breaths.
  • GB-20 (Feng Chi): Located in the hollows at the base of your skull, between the two large neck muscles. This is the "Wind Pool" point — traditionally used to release Wind from the head and eyes. Press gently upward for 1–2 minutes.
  • Daily practice: Find ways to discharge stress regularly before it builds to a tipping point. In TCM theory, moving stagnant Qi early may reduce the likelihood of it transforming into rising Yang and Internal Wind.

For Liver Blood Deficiency (Pattern 2)

  • Acupressure point — LV-8 (Qu Quan): Located on the inner knee, at the end of the knee crease when the knee is bent. This point is traditionally used to nourish Liver Blood.
  • ST-36 (Zu San Li): Located four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width toward the outside of the shin. This is the body's primary point for building Qi and Blood — it supports the digestive system to produce better quality blood.
  • Supportive foods: In TCM dietary theory, dark leafy greens (especially spinach), beets, goji berries, liver (in small amounts), and bone broth are traditionally considered blood-nourishing foods.
  • Sleep: Prioritize 7–9 hours of quality sleep — this is when Liver Blood is replenished most effectively.

For Gallbladder Fire / Damp-Heat (Pattern 3)

  • Acupressure point — GB-1 (Tong Zi Liao): Located about half an inch outward from the outer corner of the eye. This is a local point on the Gallbladder channel that directly influences the eye area. Gently press for 30 seconds.
  • GB-34 (Yang Ling Quan): Located on the outer side of the lower leg, in the hollow just below the knee. This is the Gallbladder's "influential point" and helps clear Gallbladder fire and promote the smooth flow of Qi.
  • Dietary considerations: In TCM dietary theory, reducing greasy, fried, and heavily spiced foods is traditionally recommended when Gallbladder heat is present. Bitter foods like dandelion greens, endive, and radicchio are traditionally used to clear Gallbladder heat.

What Research Says

While the TCM framework for understanding ocular migraines is based on a diagnostic system developed over centuries of clinical observation, some research offers interesting parallel insights:

  • A 2021 meta-analysis in BMJ Open (21 RCTs, n=2,183) found that acupuncture may reduce migraine frequency compared to sham acupuncture, with effects sustained at 24-week follow-up. While this study covered migraines generally rather than ocular migraines specifically, it suggests that modulating the channels involved in headache pathways may offer benefits.
  • A 2019 study in Neurological Sciences (n=72) found that patients with migraine and visual aura showed higher levels of inflammatory markers compared to migraine patients without aura — which aligns with the TCM concept that Internal Wind (visual aura) arises when an underlying imbalance (stagnation, heat, or deficiency) reaches a critical threshold.
  • The relationship between stress and migraine frequency is one of the most well-established findings in headache medicine. A 2022 prospective cohort study in Neurology (n=5,247) found that increased perceived stress was associated with a 28% higher risk of migraine attacks in the following 6 weeks — consistent with the TCM understanding that Liver Qi Stagnation from chronic stress is a primary driver of headache-related patterns.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Identify your pattern. Use the descriptions above to get a sense of whether your ocular migraines lean toward Liver Yang Rising (stress-triggered, tense), Liver Blood Deficiency (fatigue-triggered, pale), or Gallbladder Fire (diet-related, bitter taste). Most people will recognize themselves in one pattern most strongly.
  2. Start with acupressure. LV-3 (calming Liver Yang) and GB-20 (releasing Wind from the head) are two of the most accessible points for daily self-care. Practice them when you feel prodromal symptoms — the early warning signs that often precede an aura — to potentially reduce intensity.
  3. Track your triggers with a pattern lens. In addition to tracking potential triggers (certain foods, screen time, stress, sleep changes), note which TCM pattern you were leaning toward at the time. Over weeks, you may notice a clear relationship between your lifestyle and your dominant pattern.
  4. Support your Liver throughout the day. Movement — even a 10-minute walk — is one of the most effective ways to move stagnant Liver Qi before it rises. In TCM theory, the Liver loves smooth, flowing movement and dislikes stagnation.
  5. Consider dietary adjustments. If the Gallbladder Fire pattern resonates with you, experiment with reducing greasy and spicy foods for 2–3 weeks to see if your visual disturbances become less frequent.

When to See a Doctor

While ocular migraines are generally considered harmless, certain symptoms require immediate medical evaluation. Seek emergency care if:

  • You experience visual disturbances for the first time after age 50
  • The visual disturbance lasts longer than 60 minutes
  • You have sudden vision loss that persists
  • The aura is accompanied by slurred speech, weakness on one side of the body, or difficulty walking
  • Your headache pattern changes significantly — it becomes more frequent, more severe, or different in quality
  • You experience "thunderclap" headache — a severe headache that reaches maximum intensity within seconds

If you haven't been formally diagnosed with ocular migraines, make an appointment with a neurologist or an eye doctor (ophthalmologist) to rule out other causes of visual disturbances, including retinal issues, optic nerve problems, or vascular concerns.
