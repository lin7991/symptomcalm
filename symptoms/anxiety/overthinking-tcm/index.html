Overthinking and Rumination from a TCM Perspective: Spleen, Heart and the Racing Mind

Symptom Guide · 9 min read

The Thought Loop You Can't Escape

It starts the same way every time. A conversation from three days ago plays on repeat — you mentally rewrite your responses, analyze every word you said, imagine every way it could have gone better. Or maybe it's the worry that circles and circles: what if this happens, what if that happens, what if — always leading to a darker scenario, never to a resolution.

You try to stop. You tell yourself to think about something else. But the thought drags you back like a current you can't swim against.

By the end of the day, you're mentally exhausted — drained in a way that sleep alone doesn't fix. Not because you were busy, but because your own mind wore you out from the inside.

Western psychology calls this rumination — repetitive, passive dwelling on negative thoughts. It's a hallmark of anxiety and depression, and it's notoriously hard to stop through willpower alone. Antidepressants may take the edge off, and therapy can help reframe thought patterns, but many people still find themselves caught in the loop.

TCM offers a different map. Instead of asking "how do I stop this thought?", it asks: which organ system is allowing this pattern to take hold? Because in TCM theory, overthinking isn't just a psychological habit — it's a signal that specific internal systems are out of balance, and different kinds of racing thoughts point to different root causes.

TCM's Map of Overthinking: Three Organs, Three Kinds of Racing Mind

In TCM, every organ houses a specific aspect of your mental-emotional life. When that organ is out of balance, the way you think changes — not just the content of your thoughts, but the pattern they take.

Overthinking in TCM can come from three main sources — and here's the key insight: the quality of your overthinking reveals which organ is involved.

Organ Mental Function When Imbalanced Feels Like
Spleen Yi (Intellect) — focused thought, concentration, study Yi loses its anchor — thoughts drift, loop, and exhaust without resolution A mental treadmill: lots of effort, no forward movement
Heart Shen (Spirit) — clarity, presence, settled awareness Shen becomes unsettled — thoughts race, jump between topics, and refuse to settle A browser with 30 tabs open, all playing video at once
Liver Planning, decision-making, emotional flow Qi stagnates — the same thought or grievance loops without release A stuck record needle: same track, same frustration, no forward movement

Pattern 1: Spleen-Yi Deficiency — The Exhausting Loop

If this sounds like you: You overthink in circles. You replay conversations, analyze decisions, worry about the future — and never reach a conclusion. The more you think, the less clear everything becomes. Your mind feels heavy, not sharp. You also tend toward digestive issues: bloating after meals, loose stools, low appetite. Your energy dips after eating, and you feel physically and mentally drained by mid-afternoon.

In TCM, the Spleen governs the Yi (Intellect) — the aspect of mind responsible for focused, applied thinking: studying, planning, remembering, concentrating. The Spleen takes raw food and transforms it into Qi (energy) and Blood, which nourish the brain. When the Spleen is strong, your thinking is clear, grounded, and productive. When the Spleen is weak, the Yi loses its anchor.

Western analogy: Think of your Spleen as your body's power plant — it converts food (fuel) into Qi (usable energy) and distributes it to your brain, muscles, and organs. When the power plant runs at half capacity, your brain runs on backup battery: slow processing, poor concentration, and thoughts that loop because there isn't enough mental energy to complete them.

This is why Spleen-related overthinking is so exhausting — it's not just the mental effort of worrying; it's that your body doesn't have the energy reserves to process thoughts efficiently. Every thought cycle consumes more energy than it produces, creating a feedback loop: worry weakens the Spleen, a weak Spleen produces less Qi and Blood, less Qi and Blood for the brain means more sluggish thinking, which generates more worry.

Spleen Qi Deficiency (the most common subtype) feels like mental fog paired with physical fatigue. You wake up tired, your thinking is slow, and overthinking makes you feel heavier — not more alert. Spleen Qi Sinking adds a sensation of heaviness or bearing-down in the abdomen, along with a mind that feels weighed down and unable to lift itself into clear thought.

If you're a student, a knowledge worker, or someone who spends long hours analyzing and ruminating, the Spleen-Yi pattern is the most common source of chronic overthinking. The constant cognitive load depletes the Spleen, which then struggles to supply the brain — making you more prone to unproductive loops.

Pattern 2: Heart-Shen Disturbance — The Scattered Mind

If this sounds like you: Your thoughts race. They don't just loop — they jump. One moment you're worrying about work, the next about a conversation, then about your health, then about something that happened last year. It's not the same track on repeat; it's a channel surfer with ADD. You feel restless, agitated, and find it hard to sit still. Sleep doesn't come easily — your mind keeps chattering even when your body is exhausted.

The Heart in TCM houses the Shen (Spirit) — the aspect of mind that maintains presence, clarity, and settled awareness. When the Shen is at rest, your mind is calm, present, and able to respond to life without getting swept away by every thought or emotion. Think of it as the mental equivalent of sitting on solid ground while waves pass around you — you notice the waves, but you don't get pulled into each one.

When the Shen is disturbed, the opposite happens: every thought pulls you. The mind becomes reactive rather than responsive, scattered rather than settled.

Western analogy: Heart-Shen disturbance is like having a browser with 30 tabs open, all streaming video simultaneously. Your system is overwhelmed — not because one task is hard, but because too much input is demanding attention at once. The RAM is maxed out. Nothing loads smoothly. And the more tabs you try to manage, the slower everything becomes.

Heart-Shen overthinking tends to be hotter and faster than the Spleen pattern. It often coexists with:

  • Reduced sleep quality — difficulty falling asleep because the mind won't settle, or waking with a racing heart and looping thoughts at 2-3 AM
  • Restlessness — fidgeting, pacing, needing constant distraction (phone scrolling, binge-watching, snacking)
  • Heat signs — feeling hot in the chest, redder complexion than usual, thirst, tendency toward mouth ulcers or red-tipped tongue

Heart-Shen disturbance can be triggered or worsened by emotional shocks, chronic sleep deprivation, overstimulation from screens and social media, or the Spleen-Yi pattern progressing to affect the Heart. In TCM theory, the Spleen produces Blood, and the Heart stores Blood — when the Spleen is depleted enough, the Heart loses its nourishment, and the Shen becomes unsettled. This is known as the Heart-Spleen Deficiency pattern, where overthinking and insomnia feed each other in a tight vicious cycle.

Pattern 3: Liver Qi Stagnation — The Stuck Record

If this sounds like you: You replay the same grievance over and over. An argument that happened last week that you can't let go of. A situation at work that feels unfair. You imagine what you should have said, what you would say if you had the chance, what the other person's reaction would be. The thought has a clear emotional charge — usually frustration, irritation, or suppressed anger — and it doesn't fade even when you try to distract yourself.

Your body reflects this mental stuck-ness: tight shoulders, a clamped jaw, headaches that start in the temples or sides of the head. You might sigh frequently without realizing it, or feel a knot of tension in your throat or chest that won't release. You're irritable — not with everything, but with that one thing that your mind keeps circling back to.

The Liver in TCM governs the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body — both physical and emotional. When the Liver is balanced, emotions move like a healthy river: you experience frustration, process it, and let it go. When the Liver is stagnant (a condition called Liver Qi Stagnation), the emotional river hits a dam. The energy backs up. And your mind keeps returning to the blocked spot, trying to push through — but without release.

Western analogy: Liver stagnation overthinking is like a stuck record player — the needle keeps hitting the same groove and the same sound repeats. No matter how many times you listen to it, the song doesn't change because the needle isn't moving forward. The thought repeats precisely because the energy behind it hasn't been allowed to move through its natural arc of expression and release.

A key feature of this pattern: the overthinking is goal-oriented even if the goal is imaginary. You're not worrying abstractly — you're mentally rehearsing what you'd do, say, or change. The mind is trying to resolve an unresolved situation, but because the situation hasn't actually changed (or can't be changed), the thought loops indefinitely. This is why this type of overthinking feels different from the Spleen's vague worry-loop or the Heart's scattered racing — it has a sharper, more focused quality, with an edge of frustration or resentment.

Liver Qi Stagnation can progress to Liver Fire Blazing if left unaddressed — the stagnant energy builds up heat, transforming the stuck-record feeling into explosive irritability, pounding headaches, and red, angry thoughts that are hard to contain. This is the tipping point where overthinking becomes active agitation.

A Quick Self-Assessment: Which Pattern Sounds Like You?

Most people with chronic overthinking will recognize themselves in more than one pattern. That's normal — these organ systems are interconnected, and imbalance in one can trigger imbalance in another. But one pattern is usually primary — the one that started the cycle.

Take a moment to identify which description resonates most:

Signal Spleen-Yi Heart-Shen Liver Qi
Thought quality Heavy, looping, same track Racing, jumping, scattered Focused, grievance-based, intense
Physical clues Bloating, fatigue after eating, loose stools, brain fog Restlessness, poor sleep, palpitations, heat in chest Shoulder tightness, jaw clenching, temple headaches, sighing
Emotional tone Anxious worry, confusion, mental exhaustion Agitation, restlessness, overwhelm Irritation, frustration, resentment
Worst time Mid-afternoon, after eating Evening, bedtime, 2-3 AM waking Morning (Liver time), after conflict

If you're unsure which pattern is primary, pay attention to the physical sensations that accompany your overthinking — they're often the clearest clue. A tired, bloated digestive system points to the Spleen. A restless, buzzing quality points to the Heart. A tight jaw and clenching fists point to the Liver.

What Research Says About Overthinking and TCM's Organ Systems

Modern neuroscience doesn't map neatly onto TCM organ theory, but there are intriguing parallels worth noting.

The gut-brain axis and the Spleen-Yi connection. Research has firmly established that the gut microbiome communicates bidirectionally with the brain via the vagus nerve, influencing mood, cognition, and stress responses. Approximately 90-95% of the body's serotonin is produced in the gut. When digestive function is compromised — whether through poor diet, chronic stress, or gut dysbiosis — the signaling between gut and brain is disrupted. This aligns directly with the TCM observation that Spleen (digestive) weakness leads to mental fog and looping thoughts. A 2019 review in Nature Microbiology found that gut microbiome composition significantly affects cognitive function and emotional regulation.

The default mode network (DMN) and rumination. The DMN is a brain network that activates when you're not focused on an external task — it's the neural basis of daydreaming, self-referential thought, and mental time travel. People with depression and anxiety tend to have a hyperactive DMN that they can't easily disengage from — functionally, this is the neural correlate of rumination. Interestingly, meditation and mindfulness practices (which calm the Shen in TCM terms) have been shown to reduce DMN activity. A 2011 study in PNAS found that experienced meditators had significantly lower DMN activity during rest compared to non-meditators.

Liver Qi stagnation and the stress response. The TCM concept of Liver Qi stagnation — where emotional and physical energy builds up without release — maps well onto chronic sympathetic nervous system dominance. When the stress response is chronically activated, the body stays in "fight or flight" mode, and the cognitive correlate is a hypervigilant, grievance-focused, stuck-record thought pattern. A 2020 review in Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews found that chronic stress impairs the brain's ability to shift attention away from threat-related stimuli — the neural basis of the "stuck record" pattern.

The Heart-Shen and insomnia-rumination cycle. Clinical research has documented a strong bidirectional relationship between insomnia and rumination — each one worsens the other. A 2018 study in Sleep Medicine Reviews found that rumination was both a predictor of and a consequence of poor sleep, creating the exact vicious cycle that TCM describes as Heart-Spleen Deficiency. Acupuncture has been studied for its effects on both insomnia and rumination, with some evidence suggesting it may help regulate the autonomic nervous system and reduce DMN overactivity.

TCM herbs and cognition. Certain herbs traditionally used in TCM formulas for overthinking and racing thoughts have been studied for their effects on cognition and stress. Herbs like Polygala tenuifolia (Yuan Zhi, traditionally used to calm the Shen and improve mental clarity) and Ziziphus jujuba (Suan Zao Ren, used for Heart-Shen disturbance with insomnia) have shown potential cognitive and anxiolytic effects in preclinical studies. These findings are preliminary but suggest that the herbs traditionally used for "settling the mind" may have genuine biological activity relevant to overthinking.

Practical Takeaways: How to Calm Your Racing Mind

Each pattern of overthinking responds to different approaches. Here's what to prioritize based on your primary pattern:

For Spleen-Yi Deficiency (The Exhausting Loop)

  • Food first. Eat warm, easily digestible meals — soups, congee, steamed vegetables, bone broths. Avoid raw, cold, and iced foods, which require more energy to digest. The Spleen thrives on warmth and predictability.
  • Beware the lunch crash. If your overthinking peaks in the afternoon, examine your lunch. Heavy, greasy, or raw foods force the Spleen to work harder, diverting resources from the brain to the digestive system. Choose a warm, modest lunch and see if the afternoon fog lifts.
  • Grounding movement. Gentle walking, not high-intensity cardio. The Spleen benefits from steady, rhythmic movement that doesn't deplete energy. A 15-minute walk after meals supports digestion and helps clear mental fog.
  • Acupressure point: ST-36 (Zusanli) — four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width outward. Apply firm pressure for 1-2 minutes on each leg. This is the classic point for boosting Qi and clearing the mind.

For Heart-Shen Disturbance (The Scattered Mind)

  • Before-bed ritual. Create a hard shutdown routine at least 60 minutes before sleep — no screens, no work thoughts, no problem-solving. A warm foot soak, a few minutes of gentle breathing, or listening to calm music tells the Shen that it's safe to settle.
  • Cooling practices. If you feel heat in your chest or a buzzing restlessness, try cooling the body — a cool cloth on the forehead, peppermint tea (cooling in TCM), or breathing practices that emphasize long, slow exhalations.
  • Acupressure point: HT-7 (Shenmen) — on the wrist crease, on the pinky-finger side of the wrist. This is the primary point for calming the Shen. Gentle pressure for 1-2 minutes before bed can be surprisingly effective at settling racing thoughts.
  • Limit stimulation. Social media, news alerts, and rapid-content consumption are particularly agitating for Heart-Shen overthinking. Consider a "low-stimulation morning" (no phone for the first 30 minutes) to protect your Shen during its most vulnerable window.

For Liver Qi Stagnation (The Stuck Record)

  • Movement is medicine. Unlike the Spleen pattern (which needs gentle walking), Liver stagnation responds to active release — stretching, yoga, side-bending, any movement that opens the ribcage and sides of the body. The Liver channel runs through the inner thighs, ribcage, and sides of the neck — stretching these areas helps physically move stuck Qi.
  • Express, don't suppress. If you're replaying a grievance, suppressing it makes the stagnation worse. Find a safe outlet — journaling (write the angry draft, then throw it away), talking it through with a trusted friend, or even saying what you need to say into a voice memo. The goal isn't to act on the emotion, but to let the energy behind it move.
  • Sigh with intention. Frequent sighing (a sign of Liver Qi stagnation) is your body's attempt to release pressure. Instead of suppressing it, take a deep breath and exhale with a long audible sigh — this is the Liver's natural release mechanism. Repeat 3-5 times when the stuck-record feeling starts.
  • Acupressure point: LV-3 (Taichong) — on the top of the foot, in the web between the big toe and second toe. This is the primary point for moving Liver Qi and is remarkably effective at releasing the stuck-record thought pattern. Apply steady, moderate pressure for 1 minute on each foot.

One thing all three patterns share: the overthinking gets worse when you try to fight it. In TCM, the approach is never "force the thought away" — it's to support the underlying organ system so the mind settles naturally. Like calming a rough surface by addressing the turbulence underneath, not by trying to flatten each wave with your hand.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Overthinking and rumination can be symptoms of underlying mental health conditions including generalized anxiety disorder, depression, or OCD. If your racing thoughts feel unmanageable, interfere with daily functioning, are accompanied by suicidal thoughts, or persist despite lifestyle changes, please seek professional mental health support. TCM is a complementary approach — it does not replace therapy, medication, or crisis care. If you're in crisis, contact a crisis helpline or go to your nearest emergency room.
