Acupressure Points for Anxiety Relief: A Step-by-Step Self-Care Guide

Cluster Guide · 6 min read

You know that feeling. Your chest tightens. Your thoughts start racing. Your shoulders climb toward your ears. You try to breathe deeply, but the tension won't release. Maybe you're lying in bed at 2 AM, heart pounding. Maybe you're at your desk, unable to focus because something is gnawing at you.

Western medicine offers tools — therapy, medication, breathing exercises — and many of them help. But there's one simple, drug-free technique you can use anywhere, anytime, with nothing but your own hands: acupressure.

What if you could press a button to calm your nervous system? In TCM theory, your body already has those buttons — you just need to know where they are and how to push them.

What Is Acupressure?

Think of acupressure as acupuncture without the needles. Instead of inserting fine needles into specific points, you apply gentle but firm pressure using your fingers or thumb. Your body contains a network of energy pathways — meridians — that carry Qi (vital energy). When Qi flows freely, your mind stays calm and your body feels balanced. When stress blocks that flow, anxiety appears.

From a Western research perspective, acupressure may work through different mechanisms: some studies suggest it can activate the parasympathetic nervous system (the "rest and digest" branch), lower cortisol levels, and reduce activity in the amygdala — the brain's fear center. A 2018 meta-analysis published in JAMA Network Open suggested that acupressure may reduce anxiety symptoms more than no treatment, though the authors called for larger, more rigorous trials.

The beauty of acupressure is that it puts the control back in your hands — literally. You don't need to book an appointment, buy equipment, or remember to take a pill. The following five points are some of the most well-studied and traditionally used for anxiety relief in TCM.

The 5 Points

Each point targets a different aspect of anxiety. The one that works best for you depends on how your anxiety shows up.

1. PC6 — Neiguan (内关) — The Inner Gate

Best for: General anxiety, chest tightness, the feeling of being "stuck" with worry. Also excellent if your anxiety comes with nausea or digestive upset — a common pair in TCM.

Location: On the inner forearm, about two finger-widths below the wrist crease, between the two prominent tendons. Make a loose fist and you'll see the tendons pop up — PC6 sits right between them.

How to stimulate: Use your thumb pad to press firmly into the space between the tendons. Hold steady pressure for 2-3 minutes on each arm. You may feel a mild ache or a traveling sensation — that's a good sign. Breathe slowly while you press.

TCM rationale: PC6 is the most important point in TCM for regulating the flow of Qi through the chest. In TCM, when Qi gets "stuck" in the chest from stress or worry, it creates a sensation of tightness and unease — the physical feeling many people describe as anxiety. PC6 opens the chest, moves stagnation, and quiets the mind. It belongs to the Pericardium meridian, which in TCM is the "protector of the Heart" — and the Heart houses the Shen (mind/spirit).

Modern research note: PC6 is one of the most extensively studied acupoints in Western research. Some studies suggest it may regulate heart rate variability, reduce sympathetic (fight-or-flight) activity, and increase vagal tone — the nerve signal that tells your body to relax. It's also commonly studied for postoperative nausea and chemotherapy-induced vomiting.

2. HT7 — Shenmen (神门) — The Spirit Gate

Best for: Racing thoughts, heart palpitations, the kind of anxiety where your mind won't shut off — especially at night.

Location: On the inner wrist crease, in line with the pinky finger. Bend your wrist slightly and feel for the small hollow just below the pisiform bone (the round bone at the base of your palm on the pinky side).

How to stimulate: Press firmly into the hollow with your thumb pad. Hold for 2-3 minutes per wrist. A dull ache is normal. This point is often tender, so start gently and increase pressure gradually.

TCM rationale: The name says it all — Shenmen translates to "Spirit Gate." In TCM, the Heart houses the Shen, which is the aspect of our consciousness responsible for clear thinking and emotional stability. When the Heart is imbalanced — whether from excess heat, blood deficiency, or external shock — the Shen has no stable home, and the mind races. HT7 is traditionally considered the most direct point for calming the Shen, anchoring it so the mind can settle.

3. LV3 — Taichong (太冲) — The Great Surge

Best for: Irritability, frustration, the type of anxiety that builds up from stress and suppressed anger. If you find yourself snapping at people or feeling like you're about to explode, this is your point.

Location: On the top of the foot, in the depression between the first and second toes, about one finger-width back from where the toes join the foot.

How to stimulate: Press firmly into the V-shaped hollow between the two bones. Hold for 2-3 minutes per foot. This point can be surprisingly tender — especially if you're under a lot of stress. Start with moderate pressure and increase as you can tolerate it.

TCM rationale: LV3 is the main point for moving Liver Qi — and Liver Qi stagnation (肝气郁结) is the single most common TCM pattern associated with stress and anxiety. In TCM, the Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. When stress, frustration, or unexpressed emotions block this flow — like a river dammed by debris — the backup creates tension, irritability, and eventually "Liver Fire," which can flare upward and disturb the mind. LV3 acts like opening a floodgate, releasing the built-up pressure and restoring smooth flow.

4. CV17 — Danzhong (膻中) — The Sea of Tranquility

Best for: The physical sensation of a heavy chest, the urge to sigh, shallow breathing from anxiety, and emotional overwhelm.

Location: At the center of the chest, on the sternum, at the level of the fourth intercostal space — roughly in line with the nipples (for most people, about halfway between the collarbone notch and the bottom of the sternum).

How to stimulate: Use two or three fingers together to press gently into the center of your sternum. You can also use the pad of your thumb. Hold for 2-3 minutes while taking slow, deep breaths. This point may feel tender or "heavy" when your anxiety is high.

TCM rationale: CV17 is known as the "Sea of Tranquility" — and in TCM, it's the most important point on the chest. It's the alarm point (Mu point) of the Pericardium, which protects the Heart from emotional shocks. When you experience anxiety, grief, or emotional stress, the Qi in the chest can become stagnant and stuck, creating a physical sensation of pressure or a "lump in the throat." CV17 disperses that stagnation and opens the chest, allowing Qi to flow freely again. Many people instinctively rub their chest when stressed — CV17 explains why that motion feels so natural.

5. Yintang (EX-HN3) — The Hall of Impression

Best for: General anxiety, mental restlessness, difficulty focusing, and the feeling of being "wired." It's also a favorite for calming down before a stressful event — a presentation, a difficult conversation, or a medical appointment.

Location: In the center of the forehead, directly between the eyebrows, in the depression where the bridge of the nose meets the forehead. It's easy to find — it's the spot where you'd naturally rub your forehead when you're stressed.

How to stimulate: Use your index or middle finger to press firmly into the small hollow between your eyebrows. Hold steady pressure for 2-3 minutes. Many people find this point instantly calming. You can also use a light circular massage motion. Close your eyes while you do it.

TCM rationale: Yintang is an "extra point" — it doesn't belong to a specific meridian, but it's one of the most commonly used points in clinical practice for calming the mind. In TCM theory, the forehead is the area where "Shen disturbance" often manifests — think of the furrowed brow of someone under stress. Yintang is traditionally believed to settle the Shen, clear the mind, and bring scattered mental energy back into focus. It's also the location of the "third eye" in yogic traditions, used across cultures as a point of centered awareness.

Modern research note: Some studies suggest that stimulating Yintang may influence the prefrontal cortex and limbic system — brain regions involved in emotional regulation and anxiety processing. A 2017 pilot study found that acupressure at Yintang reduced cortisol levels and self-reported stress in participants undergoing a stressful task.

How to Build Your Anti-Anxiety Acupressure Routine

You don't need to do all five points every time. The most effective approach is to customize based on what you're feeling:

  • For general anxiety / anytime relief: Start with PC6 (Inner Gate) and Yintang — they're the most accessible and broadly calming. Do both PC6 points (both arms) for 2 minutes each, then Yintang for 2 minutes.
  • For racing thoughts / mind won't stop: Focus on HT7 (Spirit Gate) and Yintang. These two points directly target the Shen and are excellent before bed or before a stressful meeting.
  • For irritability / frustration / anger-based anxiety: Use LV3 (Great Surge) and CV17 (Sea of Tranquility). LV3 releases the built-up pressure from your Liver, and CV17 opens the chest so the released energy has somewhere to go.
  • For chest tightness / shallow breathing / feeling "stuck": CV17 and PC6 are your go-to pair. Both target the chest and upper burner, where anxiety physically manifests as tension.
  • For acute anxiety or panic moments: Yintang + PC6 + slow deep breathing. Do all three simultaneously — press Yintang with one hand and PC6 with the other, and breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 6.
Pro tip: The most effective pressure is "painfully pleasant" — deep enough to feel a distinct sensation (dull ache, warmth, or a traveling feeling), but not so hard that it causes sharp pain or bruising. Hold steady rather than rubbing or tapping. And always breathe slowly while you press — the acupressure and the breathing work together to signal your nervous system that it's safe to relax.

When to Use Caution

Acupressure is generally very safe and has no known drug interactions. However, there are a few situations where you should be careful:

  • Pregnancy: Some acupoints (particularly LV3, ST36, and SP6 on the ankle) are traditionally considered contraindicated during pregnancy, as they are thought to stimulate uterine contractions. If you are or may be pregnant, consult a licensed acupuncturist or your healthcare provider before trying acupressure.
  • Varicose veins or skin conditions: Avoid pressing directly on varicose veins, cuts, bruises, rashes, or areas of irritated skin.
  • Recent injury or surgery: Avoid acupressure near surgical incisions, fractures, or acute injuries.
  • Bleeding disorders or blood thinners: While acupressure is much gentler than acupuncture, people on blood-thinning medication or with bleeding disorders should use lighter pressure and avoid any vigorous manipulation of the points.

What About the Research?

While acupressure has been used in East Asian medicine for over 2,000 years, modern research has begun investigating its effects on anxiety. The evidence is still emerging, but the picture so far is encouraging:

  • A 2018 meta-analysis published in JAMA Network Open reviewed 15 randomized controlled trials and found that acupressure was associated with reduced anxiety scores compared to sham acupressure or no treatment — though the authors noted that many studies had methodological limitations and called for larger, more rigorous trials.
  • A 2021 systematic review in Frontiers in Psychiatry looked at acupoint stimulation (including acupressure) for anxiety disorders and found evidence that it may modulate the HPA axis (the body's central stress response system) and influence neurotransmitter activity, including serotonin and GABA pathways.
  • Neuroimaging studies using fMRI have shown that stimulating certain acupoints (including PC6 and LV3) can modulate brain activity in the amygdala, insula, and prefrontal cortex — regions central to emotional processing and anxiety regulation.

This research is promising but preliminary. Acupressure is best understood as a complementary self-care tool — it can be a wonderful addition to your wellness routine, but it is not a replacement for professional medical care for diagnosed anxiety disorders.

Danger signals: If you experience panic attacks that interfere with daily life, suicidal thoughts, or anxiety that feels overwhelming or unmanageable, please seek immediate medical attention. Self-care techniques like acupressure can be helpful, but they should not delay diagnosis or treatment of underlying mental health conditions. If you're in crisis, contact a mental health professional or call the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (in the US).

Putting It Together

Anxiety can make you feel powerless — like your own body is working against you. Acupressure offers a quiet, accessible way to reclaim some of that control. The next time you feel the familiar tightness in your chest or the spiral of racing thoughts, try pressing Yintang between your eyebrows, closing your eyes, and taking five slow breaths. Then move to PC6 on your inner wrist. Hold for two minutes on each arm. You might be surprised at what happens when you give your nervous system the signal it's been waiting for: you're safe. You can rest now.
