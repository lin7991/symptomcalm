GERD and Acid Reflux: A TCM Guide to Digestive Comfort

Symptom Guide · 9 min read

The Western View: GERD as a Mechanical Problem

If you've been diagnosed with GERD (gastroesophageal reflux disease), you've likely heard the standard explanation: the valve at the top of your stomach — the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) — isn't closing properly. Stomach contents flow backward into your esophagus, causing the burning sensation you know as heartburn.

This mechanical model has guided treatment for decades: reduce acid production (PPIs, H2 blockers), strengthen the valve (surgery), or neutralize acid that escapes (antacids). These approaches work for many people, but they leave a nagging question unanswered: why did the valve start malfunctioning in the first place?

For people with chronic GERD — not the occasional heartburn after a spicy meal, but the persistent reflux that interferes with sleep, eating, and daily life — this question matters. And it's where TCM offers a different perspective.

The TCM View: GERD as a Direction Problem

In TCM, digestion is governed by a basic directional principle: the Stomach sends food downward. After initial breakdown, the Stomach's job is to pass contents down to the Small Intestine for further processing. This downward movement is called Stomach Qi Descending (胃气下降, wèi qì xià jiàng).

When you have GERD, this directional flow has reversed — or as TCM puts it, Stomach Qi is moving upward instead of down. This pattern is called Stomach Rebellious Qi (胃气上逆, wèi qì shàng nì).

Think of it this way: Imagine an escalator designed to move downward. If the mechanism reverses — even partially — people (or in this case, stomach contents) start moving up instead of down. The burning you feel isn't excess acid attacking a healthy esophagus; it's the wrong direction of flow bringing contents where they don't belong.

This is a key distinction from the Western view. TCM doesn't see GERD as a problem of too much acid — many people with GERD have normal or even low stomach acid levels. Instead, TCM asks: what is causing Qi to flow in the wrong direction?

For a deeper explanation of Stomach Rebellious Qi and its role in general acid reflux, see our guide on Acid Reflux in TCM. This article focuses specifically on chronic GERD — the mechanisms, patterns, and long-term management strategies for people dealing with persistent reflux.

Why GERD Becomes Chronic: The TCM Perspective

Occasional heartburn is different from chronic GERD. In TCM terms, chronic GERD suggests a sustained pattern of Stomach Rebellious Qi — not a temporary disruption that resolves on its own.

Three factors commonly drive the chronicity:

1. The Liver-Stomach Axis (肝胃不和, gān wèi bù hé)
The Liver governs smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. When Liver Qi becomes stagnant — often from stress, frustration, or suppressed emotions — it "attacks" the Stomach, disrupting its downward movement. This is the most common driver of chronic GERD in TCM.

2. Spleen Qi Deficiency (脾气虚, pí qì xū)
The Spleen is responsible for transforming food into usable energy (Qi and Blood). When Spleen Qi is weak, digestion slows, food sits longer in the Stomach, and the resulting stagnation pushes Qi upward. This explains why many GERD sufferers also experience bloating, fatigue, and loose stools.

3. Stomach Heat or Damp-Heat (胃热 / 湿热, wèi rè / shī rè)
Chronic Stomach Rebellious Qi generates friction, and friction generates heat. This Stomach Heat can combine with Dampness to form Damp-Heat — a pattern associated with bitter taste in the mouth, sour regurgitation, nausea, and a coated tongue.

These factors often coexist in chronic GERD. A stress-prone individual with a weak Spleen and long-standing Liver Qi stagnation may develop both Heat (from Qi stagnation) and Deficiency (from the Spleen not producing enough Qi).

How to Tell Which Pattern Fits Your GERD

Not all GERD is the same in TCM. The following patterns describe the most common presentations experienced by people with chronic reflux. Read through them and see which one resonates with your experience.

1. Liver Qi Stagnation Attacking the Stomach (肝气犯胃)

You might have this if: Your reflux flares up during stressful periods. You notice a connection between tension, irritability, and burning. You tend to hold stress in your shoulders and jaw. The burning may be accompanied by a feeling of a lump in your throat.

TCM view: Stress stagnates Liver Qi, which then "invades" the Stomach and disrupts its downward movement. This is the most common pattern in stress-related GERD.

Common triggers: Work deadlines, relationship conflicts, suppressed emotions, irregular eating habits during stress

2. Spleen Qi Deficiency with Stomach Rebellion (脾气虚兼胃气上逆)

You might have this if: Your reflux is worse after meals, especially heavy or greasy foods. You feel bloated, tired after eating, and may have loose stools. Your reflux tends to be more regurgitation than sharp burning.

TCM view: A weak Spleen can't transform food efficiently. Undigested material sits in the Stomach, creating upward pressure. The Qi simply has nowhere to go but up.

Common triggers: Overeating, rich/fried foods, dairy, irregular meal timing, overwork

3. Stomach Heat or Damp-Heat (胃热 / 湿热)

You might have this if: Your reflux is accompanied by strong burning, a bitter or sour taste in your mouth, nausea, or a sensation of heat rising to your chest and throat. You may crave cold drinks and feel worse with spicy or greasy foods.

TCM view: Stomach Heat is often a complication of chronic Qi stagnation — the friction of stuck energy generates heat. When combined with Dampness (from Spleen deficiency), it creates a Damp-Heat pattern that feels particularly uncomfortable.

Common triggers: Spicy foods, alcohol, coffee, overeating, prolonged stress, late-night eating

Pattern-Matched Approaches for Chronic GERD

For Liver Qi Stagnation: Movement and Release

The priority here is to move stagnant Liver Qi. Since the Liver governs smooth flow, any practice that releases tension in the body's lateral lines and promotes deep breathing can help.

Dietary focus:

  • Include aromatic, Qi-moving foods: mint, basil, fennel, rosemary, chamomile tea
  • Reduce greasy, heavy foods that further obstruct Qi flow
  • Eat in a relaxed setting — not at your desk or while scrolling

Lifestyle practices:

  • Gentle movement: walking, side stretches, qigong (especially opening and closing movements)
  • Diaphragmatic breathing — 5 minutes before meals to relax the Liver and prepare the Stomach
  • Address stress triggers: this pattern is the body's signal that the nervous system needs recalibration

For Spleen Qi Deficiency: Support the Foundation

The priority is to strengthen the Spleen so it can properly transform food. This means lighter, warmer, more frequent meals and avoiding overwork.

Dietary focus:

  • Prioritize cooked, warm foods: congee, soups, stews, steamed vegetables
  • Include Spleen-friendly foods: sweet potato, pumpkin, oats, ginger, dates
  • Avoid: raw salads, cold drinks, excessive dairy, sugar, large heavy meals

Lifestyle practices:

  • Eat smaller, more frequent meals (5-6 small meals vs. 3 large ones)
  • Wait 3 hours after eating before lying down
  • Rest after meals — brief (5-10 min) sitting or gentle walking, not lying flat
  • Warmth on the abdomen (a warm compress or gentle abdominal massage in clockwise circles)

For Stomach Heat: Cool and Harmonize

The priority is to cool the Stomach and clear accumulated Heat. This often requires both dietary adjustments and addressing the underlying Qi stagnation or Spleen deficiency that generated the Heat in the first place.

Dietary focus:

  • Cooling, moistening foods: cucumber, watermelon, celery, pear, mung bean, tofu
  • Include bitter greens (arugula, endive, dandelion greens) which traditionally help drain Heat
  • Avoid: spicy foods, alcohol, coffee, fried foods, excessive red meat

Lifestyle practices:

  • Avoid eating late at night — Stomach Heat builds when digestion is attempted during sleep hours
  • Stay hydrated with room-temperature water, not iced drinks
  • Chamomile or peppermint tea can help (peppermint may relax the LES in some individuals — monitor your response)

Acupressure Points for GERD Relief

The following acupressure points are traditionally used in TCM to help regulate Stomach Qi and calm digestive upset. Gentle pressure applied for 1-2 minutes per point, bilaterally where applicable, may offer symptom relief.

ST36 (Zusanli) — "Leg Three Miles"

Location: Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width toward the outside of the shin bone.

How: Apply firm, steady pressure with your thumb. You may feel a dull ache or warmth. Hold 1-2 minutes, repeat on the other leg.

Traditional use: ST36 is one of the most important points for digestive health. It is traditionally used to strengthen the Stomach and Spleen and regulate Qi flow in the digestive system.

CV12 (Zhongwan) — "Middle Cavity"

Location: On the midline of the abdomen, halfway between the navel and the base of the breastbone (sternum).

How: Use two or three fingers to apply gentle, circular pressure. Breathe deeply as you press. Hold 1-2 minutes.

Traditional use: CV12 is the Front-Mu point of the Stomach — a point believed to directly influence Stomach function. It is traditionally used for regurgitation, bloating, and Stomach pain.

PC6 (Neiguan) — "Inner Gate"

Location: Three finger-widths below the wrist crease on the inner arm, between the two tendons.

How: Press firmly with your thumb, aiming slightly toward the center of the arm. Hold 1 minute, repeat on the other wrist.

Traditional use: PC6 is the go-to point for nausea and upward-moving Qi. It is traditionally used for morning sickness, motion sickness, and any condition where Qi is rising instead of descending.

What the Research Says About TCM and GERD

  • Acupuncture for GERD (Medicine, 2020): A systematic review and meta-analysis of 12 RCTs found that acupuncture combined with conventional treatment improved GERD symptoms and quality of life compared to conventional treatment alone.
  • TCM herbal formulas for GERD (EBCAM, 2020): A review of 60 clinical trials suggested that certain TCM herbal formulas (including Ban Xia Xie Xin Tang and Si Ni San) may offer symptom relief for GERD by modulating esophageal motility and reducing inflammation.
  • Acupressure at PC6 (Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine, 2017): A small trial found that acupressure at PC6 reduced reflux episodes and improved gastric emptying in patients with functional dyspepsia, suggesting a role for acupressure in GERD management.
A note on the research: These studies are promising but preliminary. Most trials are small, use varying treatment protocols, and may have cultural or methodological differences from Western clinical standards. TCM approaches should be seen as complementary to — not a replacement for — standard medical care for GERD.

Practical Takeaways: Where to Start

Know your pattern: Read through the three patterns above and identify which one fits your experience. GERD that flares with stress needs a different approach than GERD that's worse after heavy meals.

Start with one meal: Pick one meal per day (ideally breakfast) and make it warm, simple, and easy to digest — congee, oatmeal, or a light soup. Notice how your reflux responds.

Try acupressure before meals: Apply gentle pressure to ST36 and CV12 for 1-2 minutes each, 10-15 minutes before eating. This may help prepare the digestive system for incoming food.

Address the stress connection: If Liver Qi stagnation is your primary pattern, 5 minutes of diaphragmatic breathing before meals can make a measurable difference. The breath is the most direct tool for calming the Liver.

When to See a Doctor

While TCM offers a useful framework for understanding and managing chronic GERD, certain symptoms require medical attention:

  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or pain with swallowing
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Vomiting blood or black/tarry stools
  • Persistent nausea or vomiting
  • Chest pain — always rule out cardiac causes first
  • GERD symptoms that persist despite lifestyle changes and standard medical treatment
