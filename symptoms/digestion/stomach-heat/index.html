Stomach Heat: The TCM Pattern Behind Acid Reflux and Heartburn

Symptom Guide · 7 min read

If you've ever felt a burning sensation rising from your stomach into your chest — especially after a spicy meal, late at night, or when you're stressed — you know acid reflux. You've probably reached for antacids, avoided citrus and tomato sauce, and maybe even tried sleeping with an extra pillow.

And maybe it helps — temporarily. But the burning comes back. Because from a TCM perspective, acid reflux and heartburn are not caused by "too much acid." They're caused by Stomach Heat (胃热, wèi rè) — a pattern where the digestive system is not just irritated, but literally running too hot.

Think of your stomach like a cooking pot on a stove. In healthy digestion, the burner is on medium — warm enough to cook food thoroughly, but not so hot that it boils over. In Stomach Heat, the burner is cranked to high. The contents bubble up. Steam (reflux) escapes upward. The pot scorches. That burning sensation you feel? It's the heat radiating from a system that's been turned up too high for too long.

This guide explains what Stomach Heat is, how it shows up in your body, what causes it, and what TCM has traditionally offered — all framed as an educational exploration, not a prescription.

For a broader overview of TCM's digestive framework, start with our Digestive Health guide.

What Is Stomach Heat in TCM?

In TCM theory, every organ has a functional "temperature" — not literal temperature, but a quality of activity. The Stomach should be slightly warm so it can "rot and ripen" food (TCM's description of the stomach's digestive function). This warmth is essential for breaking down food and extracting Qi.

Stomach Heat is when that warmth becomes excessive. Instead of gently cooking food, the Stomach is scorching it. The heat rises upward (heat naturally ascends in TCM theory) — and that rising heat creates the burning sensation in the chest, the sour taste in the throat, and the inflammation in the esophageal lining.

Key distinction: Stomach Heat is not the same as having too much stomach acid. You can have normal or even low stomach acid and still have Stomach Heat. The "heat" refers to the quality of the digestive activity — inflamed, irritable, overactive — not the pH of your gastric juices. This is why antacids may offer temporary relief without addressing the root.

Are You Experiencing Stomach Heat?

Stomach Heat has a distinct set of signs that go beyond "I have heartburn." See how many resonate:

Primary Signs

  • Burning sensation in the chest or upper abdomen — the classic heartburn sensation, often worse after eating spicy, greasy, or fried foods.
  • Sour or bitter taste in the mouth — especially upon waking or after meals. This is the "rising" nature of Stomach Heat bringing digestive contents upward.
  • Frequent burping or belching — the Stomach is so agitated that gas and heat escape upward forcefully.
  • Thirst with a desire for cold drinks — your body naturally craves cold to counteract the internal heat. This is a telltale sign of a heat pattern.
  • Bad breath (halitosis) — in TCM, bad breath that doesn't resolve with brushing is a classic sign of Stomach Heat. The heat "ferments" the contents, producing foul-smelling gases that rise.

Supporting Signs

  • Red or swollen gums, canker sores, or mouth ulcers — the Stomach channel runs through the gums. Stomach Heat can manifest as inflammation in the mouth.
  • Gingivitis or bleeding gums — heat can push blood upward, causing gum inflammation.
  • Hunger soon after eating, or constant hunger — Stomach Heat speeds up digestion to the point where the Stomach "burns through" food too quickly, leaving you hungry again soon after a full meal.
  • Nausea, especially when lying down — the heat rises when you recline, making reflux more likely at night.
  • Constipation with dry, hard stools — heat consumes fluids, dehydrating the intestines and making elimination difficult.

Pattern Indicators

  • Your tongue has a red body with a yellow coating — a red tongue body signals heat; a yellow coating (especially a thick, dry yellow coating) is the classic tongue sign of Stomach Heat.
  • Your symptoms are aggravated by spicy, greasy, or fried foods — these are "hot" and "greasy" foods that directly feed the heat pattern.
  • Alcohol and coffee make your symptoms worse — both are considered hot-natured in TCM food energetics.
  • You feel better after cold drinks or cool foods — temporary relief from cold is a hallmark of a heat pattern.
Does this sound familiar? If you experience multiple of these signs — especially the burning sensation, sour taste, constant thirst for cold drinks, and bad breath — Stomach Heat may be the TCM pattern underlying your reflux. What Causes Stomach Heat?

In TCM theory, Stomach Heat doesn't appear overnight. It's usually the accumulated result of dietary and lifestyle habits over weeks or months.

Dietary Causes (Most Common)

  • Excessive spicy, hot, or warming foods — chili, curry, hot peppers, garlic, onion, and ginger in large quantities. In small amounts these are beneficial; in excess they create heat.
  • Fried and greasy foods — deep-fried foods, fast food, and heavy oils contribute to both heat and "dampness" (undigested fluids that mix with heat to become Damp-Heat).
  • Alcohol, especially in excess — alcohol is considered very hot-natured in TCM. Chronic drinking is one of the most common causes of Stomach Heat.
  • Excessive coffee and caffeinated drinks — coffee is warming and drying. In moderation it's fine; in excess it can contribute to Stomach Heat.
  • Overeating rich, heavy foods — too much meat, cheese, and heavy protein taxes the digestive system, creating heat as a byproduct of incomplete digestion.
  • Eating late at night — the Stomach's energy naturally declines in the evening. Eating late forces it to work when it's weakest, and the resulting stagnation generates heat.

Emotional Causes

  • Chronic frustration, irritability, or suppressed anger — in TCM, these emotions affect the Liver, which can "transmit" its heat to the Stomach via the Liver-Stomach relationship. This is why people who are chronically stressed or frustrated often develop reflux.
  • Excessive worry and rumination — the emotion of the Spleen (worry) can also generate heat when it's chronic, contributing to Stomach Heat indirectly.

Other Causes

  • Acute illness with fever — a high fever can leave residual heat in the Stomach.
  • Overuse of warming herbs or supplements — some herbs and supplements are warming by nature; taking them in excess without balancing with cooling elements can create heat.
  • Smoking — tobacco is considered hot and drying in TCM, and the heat enters the Lung and Stomach channels.

How Stomach Heat Differs From Other Digestive Patterns

Stomach Heat is one of several TCM patterns that can cause digestive discomfort. Here's how it compares to the other patterns covered in our digestion guide:

Stomach Heat (This Pattern)

Key feeling: "Burning in my chest, sour taste, I crave cold drinks, I'm hungry again soon after eating."

Think: The stove burner is too high. The pot is boiling over.

Worse with: Spicy foods, alcohol, coffee, fried foods, eating late

Better with: Cool (not ice-cold) foods, small bland meals, stress reduction

Spleen Qi Deficiency

Key feeling: "I bloat after meals, I'm tired after eating, my stools are loose."

Think: The generator is underpowered. Not enough energy to transform food.

Worse with: Raw/cold foods, overwork, irregular meals

Damp-Heat in the Spleen

Key feeling: "Heavy, sluggish, sticky stool with a foul smell, feeling like there's a swamp in my gut."

Think: A swamp — too much moisture AND heat without drainage.

Worse with: Greasy, spicy, rich foods; alcohol; humid weather

Food Stagnation

Key feeling: "I ate hours ago and I can still taste it. I feel uncomfortably full and bloated."

Think: A traffic jam in the digestive tract. Food isn't moving through.

Worse with: Large meals, eating late at night, overeating

Liver Invading the Spleen

Key feeling: "My digestion goes haywire when I'm stressed — alternating reflux and loose stools."

Think: Stress (Liver) is overwhelming the digestive system (Spleen/Stomach).

Worse with: Stress, frustration, eating while upset

These patterns can coexist. For example, long-term Stomach Heat can damage the Stomach's Yin (cooling fluids), leading to a mixed pattern of heat with underlying deficiency. A qualified TCM practitioner can identify your unique pattern combination.

What Can You Do About Stomach Heat?

If Stomach Heat resonates with your experience, here are approaches traditionally used in TCM. Remember: these are educational descriptions, not medical recommendations.

⚠️ Important: If you're experiencing severe, persistent heartburn, difficulty swallowing, or chest pain that radiates to your arm or jaw, see a doctor immediately — these can be signs of a heart attack or serious esophageal condition. TCM approaches are complementary and should not replace medical evaluation.

Dietary Approaches (The Most Powerful Lever)

For Stomach Heat, the dietary principle is clear: cool the fire. Eat foods that are neutral to cool in nature, and avoid foods that add fuel to the heat.

Foods to Reduce or Avoid

  • Spicy foods — chili, hot pepper, curry, wasabi, horseradish. These directly add "heat" to the Stomach.
  • Alcohol — especially beer and spirits. Even small amounts can trigger symptoms in a Stomach Heat pattern.
  • Coffee and strong caffeinated tea — both are warming. If you can't eliminate them, reduce and don't drink on an empty stomach.
  • Fried and greasy foods — French fries, fried chicken, onion rings, heavy oils — these are hard to digest and generate heat.
  • Red meat in large quantities — warming and heavy. Small amounts are fine; large portions contribute to heat.
  • Garlic, onion, scallions, leeks (in large amounts) — all are warming. Small amounts in cooking are usually fine; raw or excessive amounts can aggravate Stomach Heat.
  • Tobacco — directly introduces heat into the Lung and Stomach channels.

Eating Habits

  • Eat smaller, more frequent meals. Large meals distend the stomach, pushing heat and acid upward. Five to six small meals per day put less pressure on the esophageal sphincter.
  • Don't eat within 3 hours of bedtime. Reflux is worst when you lie down on a full stomach. Give the Stomach time to empty before you recline.
  • Eat slowly and calmly. Rushing through meals creates "wind" in the digestive system, which can force heat upward. Chew thoroughly and eat in a relaxed state.
  • Don't drink large amounts of liquid with meals. Too much liquid dilutes digestive juices and distends the stomach. Small sips of room-temperature water are fine; avoid ice water with meals.
  • Sit upright for at least 30 minutes after eating. Gravity helps keep stomach contents where they belong.

Lifestyle Practices

  • Manage stress and frustration. Stomach Heat often has an emotional component. Finding ways to process frustration — through exercise, journaling, therapy, or meditation — can directly reduce the heat in your system.
  • Gentle movement after meals. A short walk after eating helps digestion without creating more upward pressure. Avoid bending over or intense exercise right after meals.
  • Avoid tight clothing around the waist. Belts, waistbands, and shapewear that compress the abdomen push stomach contents upward.
  • Sleep with your head slightly elevated. This is practical advice that aligns with both Western and TCM approaches — gravity helps keep stomach contents down.
  • Cooling breathing exercises. Slow, deep breathing (especially exhaling longer than inhaling) activates the parasympathetic nervous system and has a cooling effect on the system.

Acupuncture (Traditional Applications)

Acupuncture points commonly used in TCM for Stomach Heat include:

  • Stomach 44 (内庭, Nèi Tíng) — on the top of the foot, between the 2nd and 3rd toes. A specific point for clearing Stomach Heat.
  • Stomach 36 (足三里, Zú Sān Lǐ) — below the knee on the outer shin. The master point for digestive health, used to regulate Stomach function and clear heat when combined with other points.
  • Ren 12 (中脘, Zhōng Wǎn) — on the midline of the abdomen, halfway between the navel and the base of the breastbone. Directly regulates Stomach function.
  • Large Intestine 11 (曲池, Qū Chí) — at the outer end of the elbow crease. A point traditionally used to clear heat from the system.
  • Liver 3 (太冲, Tài Chōng) — on the top of the foot, between the 1st and 2nd toes. Used when Stomach Heat has an emotional (Liver) component.

These points are traditionally stimulated during acupuncture sessions — always by a licensed practitioner, never self-administered with needles.

Herbal Traditions

The most well-known TCM herbal formula for Stomach Heat is Tiao Wei Cheng Qi Tang (调胃承气汤, "Regulate the Stomach and Order the Qi Decoction") — a gentle purging formula that clears heat from the Stomach and intestines. It is typically used only for short periods under practitioner supervision.

A gentler formula often used for chronic Stomach Heat with mild symptoms is Yu Nu Jian (玉女煎, "Jade Woman Decoction") — which clears Stomach Heat while nourishing Stomach Yin, suitable for cases where heat has started to consume the body's cooling fluids.

For mild, occasional Stomach Heat, a simple tea of chrysanthemum flowers (菊花, jú huā) or honeysuckle (金银花, jīn yín huā) is traditionally used as a gentle cooling beverage.

⚠️ Herbal formulas should only be taken under the guidance of a qualified practitioner. Some herbs can interact with medications, and the wrong formula for your specific pattern can make things worse — for example, using a cooling formula when the real issue is Spleen Qi Deficiency can weaken your digestion further.

What Modern Research Says

The TCM concept of Stomach Heat has interesting overlaps with modern gastroenterology:

  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD): The classic symptoms of Stomach Heat — burning chest pain, sour taste, belching — correspond closely to GERD. Interestingly, many GERD patients have normal or even low stomach acid on pH testing, which aligns with the TCM view that "heat" is not the same as excess acid. A 2019 review in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine found that TCM pattern differentiation (including Stomach Heat) could identify GERD subtypes that didn't neatly fit standard Western classification.
  • Inflammatory mechanisms: Modern research has confirmed that GERD involves not just acid exposure but also inflammatory mediators (cytokines) in the esophageal tissue. The TCM concept of "heat" as inflammation has been explored in several studies — a 2020 paper in Chinese Medicine found that patients diagnosed with Stomach Heat had elevated inflammatory markers compared to other TCM digestive patterns.
  • The gut-brain axis: Stomach Heat that's triggered by stress and frustration parallels the well-documented connection between psychological stress and reflux symptoms. Stress increases gastric acid secretion and reduces lower esophageal sphincter pressure — physiological effects that TCM would describe as "Liver heat disturbing the Stomach."
  • Dietary triggers: Modern gastroenterology has identified specific dietary triggers for reflux — spicy foods, coffee, alcohol, and fatty meals — that overlap almost perfectly with the foods TCM categorizes as "heating." This convergence suggests that the TCM food energetics system has practical value, even if its theoretical framework differs from Western science.

These parallels don't "prove" TCM concepts, but they suggest that a framework developed 2,000 years ago has meaningful predictive value about diet, stress, and digestive health that modern science is still validating.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Name it. If Stomach Heat describes your experience — burning, sour taste, thirst for cold, bad breath — you now have a framework. It's not "mysterious heartburn." It's a recognizable pattern with known causes and traditional approaches.
  2. Cool the fire at the source: your diet. The single most impactful change: eliminate or significantly reduce spicy foods, alcohol, and coffee for 2-4 weeks. Replace with cooling foods — cucumbers, leafy greens, mung beans, small bland meals. Notice how your symptoms change.
  3. Watch your meal timing. Eat your last meal at least 3 hours before bed. If you eat late, the heat has nowhere to go but up when you lie down.
  4. Address the emotional heat. If stress, frustration, or irritability are part of your life, that emotional heat is contributing to the physical heat. Find an outlet that works for you — exercise, meditation, therapy, journaling.
  5. Work with a professional. If you're exploring TCM, look for an NCCAOM-certified practitioner in the US or a similarly qualified practitioner in your region. Bring your symptom history and any medical diagnoses (GERD, gastritis, hiatal hernia) so they can integrate TCM and Western knowledge.
  6. Be consistent, not aggressive. Stomach Heat that's been building for months won't disappear in a weekend. Gentle, consistent dietary changes over 3-6 weeks are more effective than aggressive cold-water fasting or extreme diet changes.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ These symptoms require immediate medical attention and should not be managed with TCM alone:

  • Chest pain that radiates to the arm, jaw, or back (possible heart attack)
  • Difficulty or pain with swallowing (dysphagia)
  • Vomiting blood or material that looks like coffee grounds
  • Black, tarry, or bloody stools
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Persistent hoarseness or chronic cough that doesn't resolve
  • Frequent heartburn that doesn't respond to lifestyle changes (may require endoscopy to rule out Barrett's esophagus or other conditions)

TCM offers a valuable complementary framework for understanding and managing digestive patterns — but it works best alongside, not instead of, professional medical care. Start with a medical evaluation, then explore TCM approaches with practitioner guidance.
