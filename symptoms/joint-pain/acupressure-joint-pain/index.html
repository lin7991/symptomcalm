Acupressure Points for Joint Pain Relief: A Self-Care Guide

Self-Care Guide · 6 min read

Your knees ache when you stand up from a chair. Your knuckles feel stiff first thing in the morning. That dull, grinding sensation in your hip has been hanging around for months.

You've probably tried ice, heat, stretching, and maybe a topical gel that smells medicinal and works for about 45 minutes. Joint pain has a way of making you feel helpless — like your own body is working against you, and all you can do is wait it out.

But what if you had a tool literally at your fingertips? Something you could use anytime — while sitting at your desk, watching TV, waiting for the kettle to boil — that might actually help your joints feel better?

That's the promise of acupressure: a self-care technique drawn from Traditional Chinese Medicine that uses your own hands to stimulate specific points on your body. It's acupuncture without needles, and it's one of the most accessible tools TCM offers for managing joint discomfort on your own terms.

Acupressure in a nutshell: Your body has a network of energy channels (meridians). When Qi (vital energy) flows freely, tissues are nourished and comfortable. When Qi gets stuck — around a stiff joint, for example — you feel pain and tightness. Acupressure points are like valves: press them correctly, and you can help release the stuck energy, encouraging fresh circulation to the area.

Why Acupressure for Joint Pain?

Joint pain — whether from osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or everyday wear and tear — has a common thread in TCM: obstruction. The TCM framework calls it Bi Syndrome (痹证), a pattern where Wind, Cold, Damp, or Heat blocks the flow of Qi and Blood in the channels that supply the joints.

When Qi and Blood can't reach the joint tissues, they become starved of nourishment. The result: stiffness, pain, swelling, and reduced mobility — the same symptoms you feel day to day.

Acupressure works by applying firm, sustained pressure to points along the affected meridians. Research suggests that mechanical stimulation of these points may influence pain signaling pathways and encourage local blood flow. A 2020 systematic review in the BMJ Open found that acupressure may reduce pain intensity and improve physical function in people with chronic musculoskeletal pain, though the authors noted the need for larger, better-designed studies.

In practical terms, acupressure offers three advantages for joint pain:

  • Self-directed: You don't need a practitioner visit for every flare-up. Once you know the points, you can use them daily.
  • Low-risk: When done correctly, acupressure has very few side effects — mainly some mild soreness or bruising at the point.
  • Complementary: It works alongside whatever else you're doing — medication, physical therapy, stretching — without interference.

The Key Points: Your Acupressure Toolkit for Joint Pain

Below are the most commonly recommended acupressure points for joint pain, organised by location. Each point has been used in TCM for centuries, and many have been studied in modern clinical settings.

How to press: Use your thumb or index finger. Press firmly enough to feel a distinct sensation — dull ache, warmth, or a slight "electrical" feeling — but never sharp pain. Hold for 30–60 seconds while breathing slowly. Repeat 2–3 times per point, 1–2 times daily.

LI4 (Hegu) — The General Pain Point

Location: In the fleshy web between your thumb and index finger. Squeeze the thumb and index finger together; the point is at the highest spot of the muscle bulge. On the back of the hand.

Best for: General pain relief, especially for the upper body — hands, wrists, elbows, shoulders. In TCM, LI4 is considered a master point for moving Qi and stopping pain anywhere in the body.

Why it works: LI4 is the source point of the Large Intestine meridian, which runs from the index finger up the arm to the face. By stimulating LI4, you're effectively "unlocking" the entire meridian, encouraging Qi flow through the arm and shoulder.

Contraindication: Do not stimulate LI4 during pregnancy — it is traditionally avoided in pregnancy as it may stimulate uterine contractions.

ST36 (Zusanli) — The Energy Powerhouse

Location: Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width outside the shin bone (tibia). In the muscle belly of the tibialis anterior.

Best for: Knee pain, leg weakness, overall vitality. ST36 is one of the most important points in TCM for building Qi and Blood — it's traditionally used for fatigue, digestive weakness, and general recovery, making it ideal when joint pain has left you feeling depleted.

Why it works: ST36 is the "sea of nourishment" point on the Stomach meridian. In TCM theory, the Stomach is responsible for extracting Qi from food. By strengthening this point, you support your body's ability to repair and nourish joint tissues. Pressure here often produces a warm, spreading sensation down the leg — a sign that Qi is moving.

GB34 (Yanglingquan) — The Tendon and Joint Master

Location: On the outer side of the lower leg, in the depression just below the head of the fibula (the bony bump on the outside of the knee).

Best for: Knee pain, hip pain, tendon stiffness, and general joint mobility. GB34 is traditionally considered the "meeting point of the tendons" — making it the go-to point for anything involving connective tissue around joints.

Why it works: GB34 belongs to the Gallbladder meridian, which traverses the side of the body and passes through the hip and knee. In sports medicine research, GB34 stimulation has been associated with improved knee range of motion and reduced stiffness in osteoarthritis patients.

LI11 (Quchi) — The Inflammation Point

Location: Bend your elbow. LI11 is at the end of the elbow crease, on the outer side — in the depression between the end of the crease and the bony prominence of the elbow.

Best for: Elbow pain, tennis elbow, and patterns with heat or inflammation (red, hot, swollen joints). In TCM, LI11 is traditionally used to clear Heat — making it useful for inflammatory flare-ups.

Why it works: LI11 is the "uniting point" of the Large Intestine meridian — a powerful point for moving Qi through the elbow and upper arm. It's one of the points most commonly studied in acupuncture research for inflammatory conditions.

SP6 (Sanyinjiao) — The Circulation Regulator

Location: Four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, right along the edge of the shin bone. On the inside of the lower leg.

Best for: Lower body circulation, hip pain, and general Qi and Blood stagnation. SP6 is where three Yin meridians (Spleen, Liver, Kidney) meet — making it a powerful point for harmonising the body's deep energy systems.

Why it works: In TCM, the Spleen governs the muscles and limbs, the Liver governs the tendons, and the Kidneys govern the bones. SP6 connects all three — making it especially valuable when joint pain has multiple contributing factors (stiff muscles + tight tendons + weak bones).

Contraindication: Like LI4, SP6 is traditionally avoided during pregnancy.

KI3 (Taixi) — The Kidney Point for Joint Support

Location: In the depression between the inner ankle bone (medial malleolus) and the Achilles tendon.

Best for: Chronic joint pain, especially in the knees and ankles. Also useful if you experience fatigue alongside joint pain — which is extremely common. In TCM, the Kidneys are said to "govern the bones," so KI3 is traditionally used when joint problems are rooted in constitutional weakness rather than acute injury.

Why it works: KI3 is the source point of the Kidney meridian. In TCM theory, Kidney Essence (Jing) provides the raw material for bone health and joint integrity. Stimulating KI3 is like topping up the reservoir — supporting your body's deep reserves rather than just treating the local symptom.

A Simple Daily Acupressure Routine for Joint Pain

You don't need to do all six points every day. Start with this 5-minute morning routine, then add points based on where you feel the most discomfort:

  1. Sit comfortably and take three slow, deep breaths to settle your nervous system.
  2. Start with LI4 (Hegu): 30 seconds on each hand. This "opens the gates" for the rest of the session.
  3. Target your problem joints: If your knees are the issue, do ST36 and GB34 on both legs (30 seconds each). If your elbows are stiff, do LI11. If the pain is general and nagging, add KI3.
  4. Finish with SP6: 30 seconds on each leg. This helps move the Qi you've stimulated through the lower body.
  5. Move the joint gently for 30 seconds after pressing — rotate the knee, roll the ankle, make fists and release. This "wakes up" the circulation you've encouraged.

Consistency matters more than intensity. Five minutes daily will likely produce better results than 30 minutes once a week.

A note on expectations: Acupressure is a self-care tool, not a cure. You may notice a subtle shift after the first session — warmth in the joints, slightly easier movement — and more noticeable changes over several weeks of consistent practice. For some people, the effects are immediate and satisfying. For others, it's a gradual loosening. Both are normal.

When to Be Careful with Acupressure

Acupressure is generally safe, but there are important precautions:

  • Pregnancy: Avoid LI4 (Hegu) and SP6 (Sanyinjiao) during pregnancy. These points are traditionally contraindicated as they may stimulate the uterus.
  • Varicose veins: Do not press directly on varicose veins. Work around them.
  • Open wounds, burns, rashes, or bruises: Avoid pressing on damaged skin.
  • Recently injured joints: If the joint is acutely swollen, hot, or injured within the last 48 hours (sprain, strain, fracture), rest and ice are more appropriate. Wait until the acute phase has passed before using acupressure.
  • Blood clotting disorders: If you take blood thinners or have a clotting disorder, use lighter pressure to avoid bruising.
  • Cancer with bone metastases: Avoid pressing directly on bones that may be weakened by metastasis.

What the Research Says

Scientific interest in acupressure for joint pain has grown substantially. Here's a snapshot of what studies have found:

  • A 2020 meta-analysis of 18 RCTs in the BMJ Open found that acupressure may reduce pain intensity and improve physical function in people with chronic musculoskeletal pain, including knee osteoarthritis.
  • A 2017 study in Pain Medicine compared acupressure to physical therapy for chronic low back pain and found that acupressure produced similar improvements in pain and function after six weeks.
  • A 2019 clinical trial in Clinical Rheumatology reported that self-administered acupressure (done by patients at home after training) reduced knee pain and stiffness in people with osteoarthritis over 12 weeks.

Much of this research is promising but preliminary. Sample sizes are often small, and it's difficult to design a convincing "placebo control" for acupressure since the patient is actively involved. Still, the combination of centuries of clinical tradition and a growing body of modern evidence makes acupressure a low-risk option worth exploring.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Acupressure is a complementary self-care tool, but joint pain can sometimes signal a serious underlying condition. See a healthcare provider if:

  • Your joint is red, hot, and severely swollen — this could indicate infection, gout, or an inflammatory flare that needs medical treatment.
  • You have fever accompanying joint pain.
  • The pain started suddenly after an injury and you cannot bear weight on the joint.
  • You have unexplained joint pain accompanied by fatigue, rash, or weight loss.
  • The pain is severe and does not improve with rest or basic self-care.

Joint replacement, fractures, and infected joints require immediate medical attention — acupressure is not a substitute for these. If you have a diagnosed autoimmune condition (rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, psoriatic arthritis), continue your prescribed treatment and discuss complementary approaches with your rheumatologist before making changes.
