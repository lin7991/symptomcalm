5 Acupressure Points for Better Sleep

Cluster Guide · 6 min read

You've tried the sleep hygiene checklist: dark room, cool temperature, no screens an hour before bed. Maybe you've even tried melatonin. And yet — here you are, staring at the ceiling at 2 AM, mind running a marathon you didn't sign up for.

If counting sheep has failed you, there's a simple, drug-free technique worth exploring: acupressure. It's been used in Traditional Chinese Medicine for centuries, and unlike pills or supplements, it costs nothing, has no side effects, and you can do it yourself — right in bed.

What Is Acupressure?

Think of acupressure as acupuncture without the needles. Instead of inserting fine needles into specific points on the body, you apply gentle but firm pressure using your fingers or thumb. In TCM theory, these points lie along the body's energy pathways — meridians — that carry Qi (vital energy). When Qi flows smoothly, the body is balanced and sleep comes naturally. When Qi gets stuck, sleep suffers.

Western researchers have proposed a more mechanical explanation: stimulating these points may activate the parasympathetic nervous system (the "rest and digest" branch), lower cortisol, and trigger the release of endorphins. Some studies suggest that acupressure applied before bed can improve sleep quality, reduce the time it takes to fall asleep, and decrease nighttime awakenings.

Whatever the mechanism, the experience is the same: a few minutes of focused pressure on the right spots, and the nervous system begins to settle.

The 5 Points

Each of these points is chosen for a different reason in TCM. The one that works best for you depends on what kind of insomnia you're experiencing.

1. HT7 — Shenmen (神门) — The Spirit Gate

Location: On the inner wrist crease, in line with the pinky finger. If you bend your wrist slightly, you'll feel a small hollow just below the pisiform bone (the small round bone at the base of your palm on the pinky side).

How to stimulate: Use your thumb pad to press firmly into the hollow. Hold steady pressure for 2-3 minutes. You should feel a mild aching sensation — that's normal. Breathe deeply while you press.

TCM rationale: HT7 is the most famous sleep point in TCM. The Heart houses the Shen (mind/spirit), and this point is believed to calm the Shen and anchor it so the mind can settle at night. It's considered the go-to point for anyone whose insomnia involves a racing mind, anxiety, or heart palpitations.

2. PC6 — Neiguan (内关) — The Inner Gate

Location: Two finger-widths above the inner wrist crease, between the two prominent tendons. If you make a loose fist, you'll see two tendons pop up on your inner forearm — PC6 sits between them.

How to stimulate: Press with your thumb between the tendons, holding steady pressure for 2-3 minutes per arm. It can also be done with a circular massaging motion. This point is famous for its dual effect: calming the mind while also settling the stomach.

TCM rationale: PC6 is a key point for opening the chest and regulating Qi. In TCM, when Qi is stagnant in the chest, it creates a sensation of tightness and unease that can keep you awake. This point is traditionally used for insomnia accompanied by chest tightness, anxiety, or digestive discomfort before bed (think of the classic TCM saying: "if the stomach is not at peace, sleep is disturbed").

Modern research note: PC6 is one of the most studied acupoints in Western research. Some studies suggest it may influence the autonomic nervous system, reducing sympathetic (fight-or-flight) activity and promoting parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) tone. It's also commonly studied for nausea and anxiety.

3. LV3 — Taichong (太冲) — The Great Surge

Location: On the top of the foot, in the depression between the first and second toes, about one finger-width back from where the toes join the foot.

How to stimulate: Use your thumb to press firmly into the V-shaped hollow between the two bones. Hold for 2-3 minutes per foot. This point can be quite tender — especially if you're stressed — so start with moderate pressure and increase gradually.

TCM rationale: LV3 is the most important point for moving Liver Qi. In TCM, the Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. When stress, frustration, or suppressed anger causes Liver Qi to stagnate, it can "turn to fire" — and that fire disturbs the Shen, causing sleep problems. LV3 is particularly relevant if you wake up between 1-3 AM (the Liver hour in the TCM organ clock) or if you feel irritable and tense during the day.

4. ST36 — Zusanli (足三里) — The Leg Three Miles

Location: On the outer side of the lower leg, about four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width away from the shinbone toward the outside of the leg. You'll feel a muscle bulge when you flex your ankle.

How to stimulate: Press deeply with your thumb or use the knuckle of your index finger for a firmer point. Hold for 2-3 minutes per leg. A mild ache or sensation traveling down the leg is a good sign.

TCM rationale: ST36 is a major point for building Qi and Blood. In TCM, when the Spleen and Stomach are weak, the body doesn't produce enough Qi and Blood to nourish the Heart, which in turn fails to house the Shen properly. This leads to a type of insomnia where you fall asleep but wake up easily and have trouble getting back to sleep. ST36 is traditionally considered a nourishing, grounding point — it strengthens the foundation so the mind has a stable base to rest on.

5. KI3 — Taixi (太溪) — The Great Stream

Location: On the inner ankle, in the depression midway between the tip of the medial malleolus (the ankle bone on the inside of your leg) and the Achilles tendon.

How to stimulate: Press firmly into the hollow with your thumb. Hold for 2-3 minutes per ankle. This point can feel quite deep — it's nestled between the bone and the tendon.

TCM rationale: KI3 is the source point for the Kidney meridian. In TCM, the Kidneys store Jing (essence), the foundation of all Yin and Yang energy in the body. When Kidney Yin is depleted (common in chronic stress, aging, or overwork), the Heart Fire can flare up unchecked — leading to the classic pattern of "Heart-Kidney imbalance." This causes trouble falling asleep, night sweats, dry mouth at night, and a restless feeling. KI3 is traditionally used to nourish Kidney Yin and allow it to cool the Heart, restoring the natural sleep rhythm.

How to Build a Bedtime Acupressure Routine

You don't need to do all five points every night. The most effective approach is to experiment:

  • Night 1-3: Start with HT7 (Spirit Gate) and PC6 (Inner Gate) — the two points most directly connected to calming the mind. Do both sides for 2 minutes each.
  • If you wake at 1-3 AM: Add LV3 (Great Surge) before bed, and try pressing it gently if you wake during those hours.
  • If you have night sweats or dry mouth: Add KI3 (Great Stream) to address the underlying Yin deficiency.
  • If you have weak digestion and light sleep: Add ST36 (Leg Three Miles) to strengthen your foundation.
Pro tip: Consistency matters more than pressure. A gentle but consistent 2-minute hold on the right point every night will likely produce better results than a one-time aggressive press. The goal is to signal your nervous system: "It's safe to rest now."

When to Use Caution

Acupressure is generally very safe, but there are a few situations where you should be careful:

  • Pregnancy: Some acupoints (especially LV3, ST36, and SP6 on the ankle) are traditionally considered contraindicated during pregnancy. If you are or may be pregnant, consult a licensed acupuncturist before trying acupressure.
  • Varicose veins or skin conditions: Avoid pressing directly on varicose veins, cuts, bruises, or irritated skin.
  • After a heavy meal: Wait at least 30 minutes after eating before applying acupressure to the leg points.

What About the Research?

While acupressure is rooted in a 2,000-year-old medical tradition, modern researchers have begun studying its effects on sleep. A 2013 systematic review published in the Journal of Sleep Research found that acupressure appeared to improve sleep quality compared to no treatment, though the authors noted that many studies had small sample sizes and more rigorous research is needed.

Some studies suggest that acupressure may work through several mechanisms: promoting the release of melatonin, lowering cortisol levels, and activating the parasympathetic nervous system. However, the evidence is still emerging, and acupressure is best seen as a complementary self-care tool — not a replacement for medical treatment of chronic insomnia.

Danger signals: If you're experiencing persistent insomnia lasting more than 4 weeks, or if your sleep problems are accompanied by chest pain, shortness of breath, severe anxiety, depression, or sudden weight changes — please see a doctor. Self-care techniques like acupressure can be helpful, but they should not delay diagnosis of underlying medical conditions.

Putting It Together

Acupressure offers a simple, side-effect-free way to support your sleep — and it puts the control back in your hands (literally). The next time you find yourself lying awake, try pressing HT7 on your inner wrist for two minutes. Take five deep breaths while you do it. You might be surprised at what happens.
