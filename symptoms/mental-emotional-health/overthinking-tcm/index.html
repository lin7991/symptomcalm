Overthinking and Rumination: A TCM Approach to Calming the Busy Mind

Symptom Guide · 9 min read

You replay a conversation from three days ago, trying to find the exact moment you said the wrong thing. You spiral through every possible outcome of a decision you haven't even made yet. Your mind is a loop — and you can't seem to find the "stop" button, no matter how exhausted you are.

This is rumination: repetitive, circular thinking that doesn't lead to solutions. It's different from productive problem-solving. Productive thinking moves forward. Rumination goes in circles. And for millions of people, it's not just a bad habit — it's a daily drain that leaves them mentally and physically depleted.

Western psychology has a name for it and effective tools (CBT, mindfulness, thought-stopping techniques). But there's a layer of the story that Western models barely touch: the physical basis of overthinking. Why does it feel so hard to stop? Why does it drain your energy? And why do some people get stuck in rumination while others can let things go?

In TCM theory, overthinking is not just a mental habit. It's a signal that certain organ systems are out of balance — your Heart, Spleen, and Liver all play specific roles in whether your mind settles or spins. And the type of overthinking you experience tells you which organ is involved.

What Overthinking Actually Does to Your Body

Before we get into the TCM patterns, it's worth pausing on what rumination does at a biological level — because the effects aren't just "in your head."

When you ruminate, your body stays in a low-grade stress response. Your cortisol levels remain elevated. Your heart rate stays slightly higher than it should be. Your digestion slows. Your sleep becomes lighter and more fragmented. Over weeks and months, this takes a measurable toll: increased inflammation, impaired immune function, higher risk of anxiety and depression, and what feels like a permanent case of mental exhaustion.

From a TCM perspective, this makes perfect sense — but the causation runs in the other direction too. The same imbalances that produce overthinking are also what keeps your body from recovering from that overthinking. It's not just a mental problem. It's a whole-system problem.

The Three Organs of Overthinking: Heart, Spleen, and Liver

TCM divides overthinking into three distinct patterns based on which organ system is most involved. Most people experience a blend, but one is usually dominant. Here's how to tell them apart:

Pattern Primary Organ What It Feels Like Worst Time
Shen Disturbance Heart Racing thoughts, mental chaos, can't quiet the mind Night, when trying to sleep
Yi Unanchored Spleen Exhausting worry loops, analysis paralysis, replaying past events After meals, when tired
Liver Stagnation Liver Stuck on the same thought, frustration-driven loops, tension in the body Under stress or pressure

Think of your mind like a computer. The Heart is the CPU — when it's overheating, everything runs fast and chaotic. The Spleen is the operating system — when it's glitching, background processes eat up all your resources. The Liver is the task scheduler — when it's jammed, programs get stuck in an infinite loop.

Your organ-diagnostic hint: If you notice which of these feels most like your experience — chaotic racing, exhausted looping, or stuck repetition — you'll know which TCM approach is most relevant.

Pattern 1: Shen Disturbance (Heart) — The Racing Mind

In TCM theory, the Heart houses the Shen (神) — your consciousness, awareness, and your capacity for calm. When the Shen is well-settled, your mind is clear and your thoughts move freely without getting stuck. When the Shen becomes disturbed — by heat, by Blood deficiency, by emotional shock — your mind loses its anchor.

You might have this pattern if:

  • Your thoughts race rather than loop — they move fast, jumping from topic to topic
  • You can't fall asleep because your mind is "too busy" the moment you lie down
  • Your overthinking feels chaotic and scattered, not focused on one thing
  • You may also experience heart palpitations, a feeling of restlessness, or a sense of being on edge
  • Your face may be flushed, or you feel easily startled

Traditional approach: Calming the Shen (安神) is the primary goal. In TCM theory, this means nourishing Heart Blood (which anchors the Shen) and clearing any Heat that agitates the mind. Acupressure points like Heart 7 (Shenmen) — located on the wrist crease, in line with your pinky finger — are traditionally used to calm the mind. Herbs like Suan Zao Ren (Sour Jujube Seed) and Bai Zi Ren (Biota Seed) are classic Shen-calming herbs, traditionally used in formulas for insomnia and agitation.

Quick practice for Shen disturbance

Palm-over-heart grounding: Sit quietly, place your right palm over the center of your chest (the location of the Heart in TCM), and breathe slowly into that area. Count 4 seconds in, 6 seconds out. The extended exhale activates the vagus nerve — the parasympathetic "brake" on your nervous system. Do this for 2 minutes, especially before sleep.

Pattern 2: Yi Unanchored (Spleen) — The Exhausting Loop

The Spleen houses the Yi (意) — often translated as "Intellect" or "Thinking." In its healthy state, the Yi gives you focused concentration, the ability to study and learn, and the capacity to engage with thoughts and then let them go. It's connected to what TCM calls the Spleen's job of transformation and transportation — just as the Spleen transforms food into energy, the Yi transforms information into understanding.

When the Spleen is weak — from poor diet, irregular eating, chronic worry, or overwork — the Yi becomes "unanchored." It loses its ground. Instead of processing thoughts efficiently, it gets stuck in a loop — replaying, analyzing, worrying, and rarely reaching resolution.

You might have this pattern if:

  • Your overthinking is exhausting — the kind that leaves you physically tired afterward
  • You replay past conversations or events, analyzing what you "should have" done differently
  • You struggle with analysis paralysis — overthinking a small decision for hours or days
  • You have digestive symptoms alongside your mental loops: bloating, loose stools, poor appetite, fatigue after eating
  • Your thinking feels heavy and slow, as if wading through mental mud

Traditional approach: Strengthening the Spleen is the priority. When the Spleen has enough Qi and Blood, the Yi naturally settles — like a kite that's tethered to a strong anchor. Dietary changes are foundational: warm, cooked foods (soups, stews, congee), regular meal times, and avoiding cold/raw foods that strain the Spleen. For a deeper dive into the Spleen-worry connection, including specific foods and acupressure points, see our dedicated article on The Spleen-Worry Connection.

Quick practice for Yi unanchored

Thought-bubble release: When you catch yourself in a worry loop, visualize each thought as a bubble. Acknowledge it, label it ("replaying the argument," "worrying about tomorrow"), and let it float away. In TCM terms, this engages the Yi's naming/classifying function — giving it a defined task rather than letting it spin aimlessly. Pair this with placing your hand on your lower belly (the Dantian, the body's Qi reservoir) to physically re-anchor attention.

Pattern 3: Liver Qi Stagnation — The Stuck Thought

The Liver's job in TCM is to ensure the smooth flow of Qi throughout your body — physically, emotionally, and mentally. Think of it as your body's traffic controller: it keeps energy moving in the right direction, at the right speed, without jams.

When the Liver's flow is blocked — by stress, suppressed emotions, frustration, or poor lifestyle habits — Qi gets stuck. And just as physical tension accumulates in the shoulders and neck when you're stressed, mental energy gets stuck in repetitive thought patterns. You can't "move past" a thought because the energy behind it has nowhere to go.

You might have this pattern if:

  • You get stuck on one thought or grievance and can't let it go
  • Your overthinking is driven by frustration, irritability, or a sense of being "trapped" in a situation
  • You feel physically tight — jaw clenching, shoulder tension, neck stiffness — alongside your mental loops
  • You tend to sigh frequently (the body's attempt to move stuck Qi)
  • Your mood changes with your cycle (for women) or gets worse under pressure

Traditional approach: The goal is to move Liver Qi — to get the energy unstuck so your mind can release its grip. Movement is the most direct approach: stretching, walking, Qi Gong, or any gentle activity that involves the sides of the body (the Liver channel runs along the inner legs and the sides of the torso). Acupressure point Liver 3 (Taichong) — on the top of the foot, in the hollow between the big toe and second toe — is traditionally used to smooth Liver Qi and release mental tension.

Quick practice for Liver stagnation

Side-stretch release: Stand with feet hip-width apart. Reach your right arm overhead and lean to the left, opening the right side of your body. Breathe into the stretch for 30 seconds. Repeat on the other side. In TCM theory, this opens the Liver channel along the ribs and flanks. Follow with 5 deep sighs — inhale fully through the nose, exhale with an audible "haaa" sound through the mouth. This is traditionally believed to release stuck Qi.

The Downward Spiral: How Overthinking Becomes a Physical Problem

Here's the part that makes overthinking so insidious: it's self-reinforcing at every level.

When you ruminate, your stress response stays active. Sustained stress depletes Kidney Yin (your body's deep reserves), which produces more internal Heat, which further agitates the Shen (Heart), which makes your mind race even more. The same chronic stress tightens the Liver's smooth flow, creating more stagnation and more stuck thoughts. And the stress hormones themselves impair digestion — the Spleen receives less Qi to do its work, weakening the anchor of the Yi. Each pattern feeds the others.

Think of it like a feedback loop in an audio system. The initial thought (the input) gets amplified as it passes through each organ system — Heart heat amplifies it, Spleen weakness can't filter it, Liver stagnation won't release it. The result is a screeching mental feedback loop that gets louder the longer it runs. The solution isn't to tell the system to "stop" — it's to address the amplification at each stage.

A Western Science Bridge: The Default Mode Network and Your TCM Diagnosis

One of the most interesting findings in modern neuroscience is the default mode network (DMN) — the set of brain regions that becomes active when you're not focused on an external task. The DMN is responsible for self-referential thinking: reflecting on your past, imagining your future, understanding your place in the social world.

The DMN is not inherently bad — it's essential for identity, planning, and learning from experience. But in people who struggle with rumination, the DMN is hyperactive. It doesn't disengage properly when they need to focus on something else. It loops. It gets stuck. It keeps running even when the person is trying to sleep.

Recent research suggests that several factors can contribute to DMN hyperactivity:

  • Chronic stress alters DMN connectivity, making it harder to disengage from self-referential thought
  • Poor sleep prevents the brain's glymphatic system from clearing metabolic waste, which some studies suggest may contribute to cognitive rigidity and looping thoughts
  • Digestive inflammation — some research links gut inflammation to altered DMN activity through the vagus nerve and immune signaling
TCM meets neuroscience: The DMN's self-referential looping maps remarkably well onto the TCM concept of Yi (thinking/intellect) that's lost its anchor. When TCM says the Spleen grounds the Yi, modern neuroscience says the DMN needs a balanced relationship with the task-positive network to function well. When TCM says Liver stagnation creates stuck mental energy, neuroscience says DMN hyperconnectivity makes it hard to shift out of self-referential loops. The descriptions differ, but the patterns they describe — a system that can't disengage from its own output — are strikingly similar.

A 2020 review in Nature Reviews Neuroscience highlighted that mindfulness meditation — which TCM-derived practices like Qi Gong and focused breathing have included for millennia — can reduce DMN hyperactivity and improve cognitive flexibility. Some studies suggest that through regular practice, the brain literally learns to disengage the rumination circuit more efficiently.

Practical Techniques to Break the Loop Right Now

Based on the pattern that fits you best, here are targeted techniques you can use the next time you're caught in a rumination spiral:

For Racing Thoughts (Heart/Shen Pattern)

  • Cold water dunk: Splash cold water on your face or wrists. The mammalian dive reflex — an evolutionary response to cold water on the face — immediately slows heart rate and activates the parasympathetic nervous system. In TCM terms, it clears temporary Heat from the Heart.
  • Counted breathing: Inhale for 4, hold for 7, exhale for 8 — this exact ratio is traditionally used in yogic and TCM breathing practices to settle the Shen and bring the mind out of chaos.
  • Heart-centered grounding: Place one hand on your chest and one on your belly. Breathe slowly for 2 minutes. This directly addresses the Heart's role in Shen disturbance.

For Exhausting Worry Loops (Spleen/Yi Pattern)

  • Write it down and close the book: Externalizing the thought loop reduces the Spleen's mental burden. In TCM theory, this is like giving the Yi a specific task rather than letting it spin freely. Set a timer for 10 minutes, write everything down, then close the notebook. The act of closing it signals the mind that processing is done.
  • Warm drink: A cup of warm ginger tea or plain hot water. The Spleen responds to warmth, and the simple act of holding a warm cup and sipping slowly grounds attention in the present moment.
  • Eat something simple and warm: If you've been ruminating for a while, your Spleen is likely depleted. A small bowl of warm rice porridge, oatmeal, or broth-based soup can physically support the Spleen and help settle the Yi.

For Stuck/Repetitive Thoughts (Liver Pattern)

  • Physical movement: Walk, stretch, or sway. The Liver channel runs along the sides of the body and inner legs — any movement that opens the sides, twists the torso, or stretches the inner thighs can help move stuck Liver Qi.
  • Side-stretches with sighing: As described above — 30-second side stretch, followed by 5 audible sighs. The sigh is a Liver Qi release mechanism in TCM theory.
  • Change your environment: Liver stagnation thrives on the same patterns repeating. Simply going outside, looking at the horizon, or changing rooms can shift the Qi enough to break the thought loop.

What Research Says

Modern research has begun exploring the mechanisms behind TCM approaches to overthinking and mental chatter:

  • A 2021 systematic review of acupuncture for generalized anxiety disorder found that certain acupuncture protocols — particularly those using points like Heart 7 (Shenmen), Pericardium 6 (Neiguan), and Yintang (the third eye point) — were associated with reduced anxiety scores in several trials, though the authors noted methodological limitations and the need for larger studies.
  • The effects of acupuncture on the default mode network have been studied using fMRI. Some research suggests that needling at specific points (including Liver 3 and Stomach 36) may modulate DMN activity, potentially impacting the neural circuits involved in rumination. However, these findings come from small studies and should be considered preliminary.
  • Dietary interventions targeting the gut-brain axis — which aligns with TCM's focus on the Spleen's role in mental clarity — have shown some potential in reducing rumination and anxious thinking in early research, though well-controlled human trials are still limited.
  • Gui Pi Tang (Restore the Spleen Decoction), the classic TCM formula for Spleen deficiency with mental restlessness, has been studied in preclinical models for its potential effects on neurotransmitter regulation and cognitive function. Human research on this specific formula for overthinking remains limited.

This research is promising but preliminary. TCM is a complementary approach — it works alongside, not instead of, conventional mental health care.

Practical Takeaways

If overthinking and rumination are part of your daily experience, here are steps to discuss with your healthcare provider:

  1. Identify your pattern. Re-read the three patterns above — Shen Disturbance (racing mind), Yi Unanchored (exhausting loops), Liver Stagnation (stuck thoughts). Which one feels most familiar? That's your starting point.
  2. Try the matched practice first. Don't try everything at once. Pick the "quick practice" section that matches your pattern and do it for one week. Notice what changes in your mental experience.
  3. Support your digestion. If you have the Spleen pattern of overthinking (exhausting loops + digestive symptoms), dietary changes are the most efficient leverage point. Warm, cooked meals. Regular eating times. Less cold food and drink. These alone can meaningfully shift mental clarity over 2-4 weeks.
  4. Move stuck energy. If you identify with the Liver pattern, prioritize movement over mental techniques. Walk, stretch, twist, sigh. The thoughts won't release until the body releases.
  5. Don't stop current treatments. If you're in therapy, on medication, or using other approaches for mental health, keep doing what works. TCM is a complementary perspective — it adds insight and tools, not replacements.
  6. Read the pillar guide. For a broader overview of emotional health through the TCM lens, see our Mental-Emotional Health Guide, which covers five patterns of depression, the grief-lungs connection, fear and the Kidneys, and the Shen as the emotional command center.
Important: These are traditional observations based on TCM theory, not medical claims. Individual results vary. Understanding your overthinking pattern is one perspective among many — not a replacement for professional mental health care, therapy, or medical treatment.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ If your overthinking is accompanied by suicidal thoughts, self-harm, severe depression, panic attacks, or if rumination is significantly impairing your ability to work, maintain relationships, or function day-to-day, please seek immediate professional help. Call emergency services (911 in the US) or the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline. TCM is a complementary approach — it does not replace crisis care, therapy, or medical treatment for mental health conditions. If you experience physical symptoms alongside your overthinking — such as chest pain, shortness of breath, or persistent headaches — consult a medical doctor to rule out underlying conditions.
