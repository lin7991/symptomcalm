Depression and Low Mood: Understanding Through a TCM Lens

Explore · 9 min read

You wake up and the weight is already there. Not sadness exactly — something heavier. You feel stuck, like there's a pressure inside your chest that won't release. You know you should get up, move, do something — but the energy simply isn't there. And underneath it all, there's a low hum of irritability. A short fuse. A sense that if one more thing goes wrong, you might snap.

If this sounds familiar, you're not alone. Depression affects roughly 280 million people globally, according to the World Health Organization. But here's what standard descriptions often miss: not all depression feels the same. Some people feel numb and empty. Some feel rage and sadness at the same time. Some feel physically heavy, as though their body has become a cage.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), these differences matter. They point to different root causes — and one of the most common is a pattern called Liver Qi Stagnation. It's not a diagnosis you'll get from a Western doctor. But for millions of people, it may be the missing piece of the puzzle.

For a broader view of how TCM approaches mood disorders, see our main guide: Mental-Emotional Health: A TCM Perspective on Depression, Grief, and Mood.

What Is Liver Qi Stagnation? The Body's Jammed Highway

In TCM theory, the Liver's primary job is to ensure the smooth, free flow of Qi (energy) throughout your entire body. Think of it as the traffic controller of your internal highway system. Qi is the traffic — it needs to move constantly, smoothly, and in the right direction. The Liver manages that flow.

When stress, frustration, suppressed emotions, or life pressures interfere with this process, the traffic jams. Qi builds up behind the blockage. This is what TCM calls Liver Qi Stagnation (肝气郁结, gān qì yù jié).

Imagine a river with a dam. Upstream, the water swells and presses against the barrier — that's the tension, irritability, and frustration. Downstream, the riverbed goes dry — that's the fatigue, emptiness, and loss of motivation. The dam is the stagnation. The solution is not to force more water through — it's to release the dam so flow can resume naturally.

This analogy captures the paradox of Liver Qi Stagnation depression: you can feel both too much (irritable, agitated, tense) and too little (exhausted, numb, disconnected) at the same time. The same blockage creates both experiences.

Signs Your Depression May Involve Liver Qi Stagnation

How do you know if your low mood is connected to Liver Qi Stagnation? While only a qualified TCM practitioner can make a proper pattern diagnosis, certain signs are characteristic of this pattern:

  • You sigh frequently. Sighing is the body's natural attempt to release trapped Qi. A heavy sigh — especially one you don't notice yourself doing — is a classic sign of Liver Qi stagnation.
  • Your mood swings. One moment you feel flat and hopeless, the next you're irritable and ready to snap at anyone. This fluctuation is the hallmark of trapped Qi looking for release.
  • It gets worse under stress. When pressure builds — at work, in relationships, with finances — your mood plummets. When things are calm, you feel almost normal.
  • Your symptoms worsen before your period. If you menstruate, you may notice your low mood intensifies significantly in the week before your period. The Liver channel runs through the reproductive organs, and hormonal shifts during the menstrual cycle can amplify stagnation.
  • You carry tension in your shoulders, jaw, or neck. The Liver channel traverses the inner thighs, hip flexors, ribcage, sides of the neck, jaw, and temples. When Qi is stuck in the Liver channel, these areas often feel tight or painful.
  • You feel a lump in your throat. The sensation of something stuck in your throat that you can't swallow or cough up — TCM calls it "Plum Pit Qi" (梅核气, méi hé qì) — is a direct manifestation of Liver Qi stagnation affecting the throat area where the Liver channel passes.
  • Your depression alternates with digestive issues. The Liver's movement of Qi directly affects the Spleen and Stomach's ability to digest food. When Liver Qi is stagnant, you may experience bloating, reflux, changes in bowel habits, or nausea alongside your low mood.
Important distinction: If your depression feels more like pure exhaustion — hollow, cold, bone-deep fatigue with little irritability — Liver Qi stagnation may not be the primary pattern. You may be experiencing Kidney Yin deficiency, Heart-Spleen deficiency, or another TCM pattern. Always consult a qualified practitioner for a proper assessment.

The Liver-Heart Connection: Why Depression Feels Like Both Numbness and Irritability

In TCM theory, emotional health is not governed by a single organ. The Liver manages the flow of Qi, but the Heart houses the Shen (神) — the spirit or consciousness that gives you your sense of self, awareness, and capacity for joy.

When Liver Qi stagnates, the "traffic jam" sends a shockwave through the entire system — and the Heart-Shen is often the first casualty. Here's how it works:

  • The Liver stores the Ethereal Soul (Hun, 魂) — the part of your spirit connected to vision, purpose, inspiration, and the ability to plan for the future. When Liver Qi is blocked, the Hun becomes "trapped." This manifests as loss of direction, hopelessness, and a sense that life has no meaning.
  • The Heart-Shen becomes disturbed — with Liver Qi stagnation creating pressure that reaches the Heart, the Shen loses its anchor. You may experience restlessness, emotional fragility, a sense of being "unhinged," and poor sleep with vivid or disturbing dreams.
  • The Spleen can't keep up — the Liver's job includes supporting the Spleen's digestive function. When Liver Qi is stuck, the Spleen struggles to convert food into Qi and Blood. Less Qi and Blood available → less nourishment for the Heart and Shen → worse depression. This is why digestive issues and depression often go hand in hand.
A West-meets-East lens: Modern research on the gut-brain axis (the bidirectional communication between your digestive system and your brain) parallels the TCM understanding of the Liver-Spleen-Heart connection. The vagus nerve, gut microbiome, and enteric nervous system collectively form a communication network that strongly influences mood — and TCM identified this interconnection centuries ago through the framework of organ relationships and Qi flow.

What Makes Liver Qi Stagnation Different From Other TCM Depression Patterns?

The pillar article on mental-emotional health outlines five TCM patterns of depression. Here's how Liver Qi stagnation stands apart:

Pattern Core Feeling Key Difference
Liver Qi Stagnation Stuck, irritable, sighing, fluctuating Energy is present but blocked — the issue is flow, not depletion
Heart-Spleen Deficiency Exhausted, worried, foggy, pale Not enough Qi and Blood being produced — the issue is deficiency, not blockage
Kidney Yin Deficiency Hollow, burnt out, numb, night sweats Deep reserves depleted — the issue is exhaustion of essence, not temporary block
Lung Qi Deficiency (Grief) Deep sadness, weak voice, prone to crying Linked to a specific loss — the issue is grief that hasn't been released
Phlegm Obstructing the Mind Foggy, dull, slow, disconnected Mental "cloud" from poor diet and fluid metabolism — the issue is turbidity, not flow

This pattern differentiation matters because the approach that helps one pattern may not help another. Moving more (exercise) helps Liver Qi stagnation because it moves Qi. But moving more may exhaust someone with Kidney Yin deficiency, who needs rest more than movement. This is why individualized diagnosis is central to TCM.

What Research Says About Liver Qi Stagnation and Depression

Modern research has begun exploring the connection between the physiological processes TCM calls "Liver Qi stagnation" and depression:

  • A 2021 study published in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology examined Xiao Yao San ("Free and Easy Wanderer"), the classic TCM formula for Liver Qi stagnation. The review suggested that the formula may influence the HPA axis (the body's stress-response system) and modulate neurotransmitter activity relevant to mood.
  • Some research has explored the concept of "stagnation" from a neurobiological perspective, suggesting that chronic stress leads to dysregulation of the autonomic nervous system — maintaining a state of sympathetic dominance (fight-or-flight) even at rest. In TCM terms, this is the "stagnation" of Qi — the system's flow is locked in a stressed pattern.
  • Acupuncture studies have shown that needling Liver channel points (particularly Liver 3, Taichong and Liver 14, Qimen) may modulate activity in brain regions associated with emotional regulation, including the prefrontal cortex and limbic system.
  • A systematic review of TCM pattern differentiation in depression found that Liver Qi stagnation was the most commonly diagnosed pattern among study participants, appearing in 30-50% of cases depending on the study population.

This research is preliminary but suggests that the TCM framework of Liver Qi stagnation may correspond to identifiable physiological patterns. More high-quality research is needed to fully understand these connections. As always, TCM is best used as a complementary approach alongside, not instead of, conventional mental health care.

Practical Ways to Support Smooth Qi Flow

If you and your healthcare provider agree that supporting Liver Qi flow may be relevant to your situation, here are some traditionally used approaches:

Movement That Unwinds

In TCM theory, movement is one of the most direct ways to move stuck Qi. But not all exercise is equal for Liver Qi stagnation. The key is flow-based, rhythmic movement that engages the lateral body (where the Liver channel runs):

  • Side stretches — standing side bends, seated lateral flexions, and cat-cow with emphasis on the side body
  • Qi Gong flowing movements like "Cloud Hands" or "Wild Goose" — these are designed specifically to circulate Qi through the channels
  • Dance — free-form, not choreographed — that allows the body to move the way it wants to
  • Walking in nature — the rhythmic, repetitive motion combined with fresh air naturally supports Qi movement

Acupressure Points for Liver Qi Stagnation

These points are traditionally used to support smooth Liver Qi flow. Apply gentle, sustained pressure with your thumb for 1-2 minutes on each side:

  • Liver 3 (Taichong) — On the top of the foot, in the depression between the big toe and second toe, about an inch back from the web. This is the most important point for moving Liver Qi. Press firmly — it may feel tender if your Qi is stagnant.
  • Liver 14 (Qimen) — Below the nipple, in the sixth intercostal space (between the sixth and seventh ribs). This point directly helps release the "heavy chest" feeling of depression.
  • GB 34 (Yanglingquan) — On the outer side of the lower leg, in the depression just below the knee joint. This point influences the smooth flow of Qi in the muscles and sinews (which the Liver governs).
  • PC 6 (Neiguan) — On the inner forearm, about three finger-widths below the wrist crease, between the two tendons. This point is traditionally used to open the chest, calm the Shen, and relieve emotional distress.

Dietary Support

Foods that support smooth Qi flow in TCM theory tend to have aromatic, pungent, or dispersing qualities:

  • Mint and peppermint — a classic Qi-moving herb that also has a cooling effect. Peppermint tea is one of the simplest daily supports for Liver Qi stagnation.
  • Vinegar and sour foods — in small amounts, sour flavors are said to "gather" and regulate Qi. A splash of apple cider vinegar in warm water, or a squeeze of lemon, can be helpful.
  • Daikon radish, cabbage, and onions — these vegetables have a downward-moving quality in TCM theory, helping to relieve the "upward pressure" of stagnant Liver Qi (headaches, chest tightness, irritability).
  • Lightly cooked rather than raw foods — raw foods require more Spleen energy to digest, which may further deplete the system when Qi is already stagnant.
  • Reduce or avoid — excessive alcohol, greasy/fried foods, and overly spicy foods, as these may aggravate stagnation or create additional metabolic burden.

Breathing to Release Stagnation

The breath is the most direct bridge between your conscious mind and your Qi. Liver Qi stagnation often shows up as shallow, restricted breathing — particularly in the side-rib area. Try this simple practice:

Lateral breathing: Sit comfortably. Place your hands on your lower ribs on both sides of your body. As you inhale, consciously direct your breath into the side ribs, feeling your hands expand outward — especially on the left side (where the Liver is anatomically located and where the Liver channel passes). Exhale slowly through the mouth with a gentle sigh. Repeat 10-15 breaths, focusing on releasing tension on each exhale.

Pro tip: The sighing exhale is particularly important for Liver Qi stagnation. In TCM theory, the Liver "opens into the eyes" — and the sound associated with the Liver in Qi Gong practice is "Shu" (shhhh-ooooo), made on a long, slow exhalation. Try it: a long breath out with the sound "Sh-u-u-u-u," feeling your ribs soften and your shoulders drop.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ If you experience suicidal thoughts, thoughts of self-harm, severe depression that interferes with daily functioning, or any mental health crisis, please seek immediate professional help. Call emergency services (911 in the US) or the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline. TCM is a complementary approach — it does not replace crisis care or professional mental health treatment.

If you're currently taking antidepressants or seeing a therapist, do not stop or change your treatment without consulting your healthcare provider. TCM is most effective as a complementary approach used alongside — not instead of — conventional care.

Practical Takeaways

If Liver Qi stagnation feels relevant to your experience with low mood, here's where to start:

  1. Notice your sigh. Pay attention to whether you sigh frequently throughout the day. If yes, it's a strong signal that your Qi is not flowing smoothly — and that your body is trying to move it. Don't suppress the sigh; instead, turn it into conscious lateral breathing practice.
  2. Move laterally. Add side stretches, Qi Gong, or any side-body movement to your daily routine. Even two minutes of gentle side bending before bed can help release the day's accumulated tension.
  3. Drink peppermint tea. Simple, safe, and traditionally used to support Liver Qi flow. One cup in the afternoon (when stress tends to build) can make a noticeable difference.
  4. Press Liver 3. Find the point on the top of your foot between the big toe and second toe. Apply firm pressure for 1-2 minutes on each foot while breathing deeply and sighing on the exhale.
  5. Explore patterns with a professional. If you're working with a TCM practitioner, ask them specifically about Liver Qi stagnation and whether it's part of your pattern. If you're not, consider finding a licensed acupuncturist or herbalist who can perform a proper diagnosis.
