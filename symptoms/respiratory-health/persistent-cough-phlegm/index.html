Persistent Cough and Phlegm: TCM Patterns for Respiratory Clearing

Symptom Guide · 8 min read

That cough came with the cold three weeks ago. The fever and sore throat are gone. Everyone else at home is back to normal. But you're still clearing your throat every few minutes — that stubborn, productive cough with phlegm that just won't fully go away.

You're not alone. A cough that persists beyond the typical 2-3 week window affects a significant number of people after a respiratory infection. And when the main feature is phlegm — the color, consistency, and sheer persistence of it — standard over-the-counter remedies often feel like they're missing the point. Expectorants help you cough stuff out, but they don't explain why your body is producing so much in the first place.

Here's what makes phlegm different: A dry cough is like a smoke alarm — a warning signal. A cough with phlegm is more like a leaky pipe — your body is actively producing fluid that it's then trying to expel. In TCM theory, these two scenarios point to very different underlying patterns, and treating them the same way rarely works.

Why Phlegm Is a Signal, Not Just a Symptom

In Western medicine, phlegm (or sputum) is mostly viewed through the lens of infection. If it's green or yellow, it suggests bacterial involvement and may warrant antibiotics. If it's clear, it could be viral or allergic. This is useful — but it's only part of the story.

In TCM theory, phlegm (痰, Tán) carries far more diagnostic information. Think of it this way: your respiratory tract is like a drainage system. When it's healthy, fluids move through it smoothly and you hardly notice them. When something goes wrong — whether it's cold, heat, dampness, or a lack of energy behind the flow — those fluids change. They get thicker, stickier, more abundant, or harder to move. The quality of the phlegm tells you what kind of imbalance is at work.

The home-heating analogy: Imagine your home's radiator system. If the water is clear and flowing, everything works. If sediment builds up (dampness), the water turns cloudy and the system gets sluggish. If the boiler is too hot (heat), the water evaporates into thick sludge. If the pump is weak (Qi deficiency), the water barely circulates. Each problem needs a different fix — just like each phlegm pattern needs a different approach.

In TCM theory, the production of phlegm involves several organ systems working together — or failing to:

  • The Spleen (脾, Pí) transforms food into Qi and fluids. When the Spleen is weakened, it produces "Dampness" — a cloudy, turbid fluid that rises to the Lungs and becomes phlegm. This is why digestive health and phlegm are so closely linked in TCM theory.
  • The Lungs (肺, Fèi) are described as the "tender organ" — most vulnerable to external pathogens. They regulate the descending and dispersing of Qi and fluids. When Lung function is impaired, fluids accumulate instead of being properly distributed.
  • The Kidneys (肾, Shèn) govern water metabolism and help the Lungs "grasp" the breath. Kidney deficiency can contribute to chronic fluid retention and a sensation of congestion that never fully clears.

4 Phlegm Patterns: What Your Phlegm Is Telling You

In TCM theory, cough with phlegm isn't one condition — it's several distinct patterns. The color, texture, and behavior of your phlegm are clues that point to a specific underlying imbalance. Most people will recognize themselves in one of the following:

1. Cold-Damp Phlegm (寒痰) — Copious Thin White or Clear Phlegm

You might have this if: Your phlegm is thin, white, or watery — sometimes so profuse that you feel like you're constantly swallowing or spitting it out. You feel chilly, especially in your upper back and chest. Your nose runs clear fluid. You crave warm drinks and feel worse in cold weather or air-conditioned spaces. Your cough may be loose and rattling.

TCM view: This is often the result of a cold that wasn't fully resolved — what TCM would call an "external Wind-Cold invasion" that settled in the Lungs. The cold congeals fluids like ice forming in a pipe: the fluid becomes stagnant but stays thin because there's no heat to thicken it. The body produces more fluid in an attempt to flush out the cold, creating a cycle of excess thin phlegm.

Traditional support: Warming and drying approaches are traditionally used. Fresh ginger tea (simmer a few slices in hot water for 5 minutes) is a classic. Warming foods like scallions, garlic, cinnamon, and black pepper are traditionally recommended. Avoiding cold drinks, dairy, and raw foods can help reduce the fluid burden on the system.

2. Phlegm-Heat (热痰) — Yellow, Green, or Thick Sticky Phlegm

You might have this if: Your phlegm is yellow, green, or dark — thick and sticky, sometimes difficult to cough up. You may feel a sensation of heat in your chest, a dry or scratchy throat, and increased thirst (especially for cold drinks). Your cough is likely harsh and forceful. You might notice bad breath or a bitter taste in your mouth.

TCM view: Phlegm-Heat develops when an external pathogen (like a flu virus) generates heat that "cooks" the body's fluids into thick, sticky phlegm — like simmering a sauce until it reduces and thickens. The heat can also arise internally from a diet rich in spicy, greasy, or warming foods, or from chronic stress that generates "Liver Fire."

Traditional support: Cooling and clearing approaches are traditionally used. Foods like pear, white radish (daikon), cucumber, watermelon, and mung bean soup are considered cooling. Herbs like loquat leaf (Pi Pa Ye, 枇杷叶) and fritillaria bulb (Chuan Bei Mu, 川贝母) are traditionally used in TCM formulas for heat-type coughs. Reducing spicy, fried, and greasy foods during this phase is commonly advised.

3. Phlegm-Dampness from Spleen Deficiency (痰湿) — White Sticky Phlegm That's Hard to Cough Up

You might have this if: Your phlegm is white but thick and sticky — not easy to expel. You may feel a sensation of heaviness in your chest and a general feeling of sluggishness throughout your body. Your digestion might feel slow — bloating after meals, loose stools, fatigue, and a tendency to gain weight easily. Your tongue might feel "pasty" or swollen.

TCM view: This pattern originates in the digestive system. The Spleen — responsible in TCM theory for "transporting and transforming" food into energy — becomes weakened by a diet high in dairy, fried foods, sugar, refined carbohydrates, and cold beverages. Instead of producing clean energy, it produces turbid Dampness that rises to the Lungs and manifests as phlegm. This is why people with this pattern often notice their phlegm gets worse after meals or after eating certain foods.

Traditional support: Dietary change is central here. Reducing or eliminating dairy, sugar, refined carbs, fried foods, and cold drinks for a period can make a noticeable difference. Increasing light, warming, drying foods — barley, adzuki beans, pumpkin, leafy greens, ginger, and tangerine peel (Chen Pi, 陈皮) — is traditionally recommended. Tangerine peel, in particular, is prized in TCM cooking for its traditional use in "drying Dampness and transforming Phlegm."

4. Lung-Kidney Qi Deficiency with Fluid Retention (肺肾气虚) — Profuse Foamy Phlegm with Breathlessness

You might have this if: Your phlegm is thin, frothy, or bubbly — almost like soap foam. You feel short of breath, especially with minimal exertion. Your voice may sound weak or breathy. You tire easily and may feel a cold sensation in your lower back or knees. This pattern is more common in older adults or people with a history of chronic respiratory conditions.

TCM view: In TCM theory, the Kidneys "grasp" the Qi as you inhale — they help the breath reach downward into the lower abdomen. When Kidney Qi is deficient, the breath feels shallow and incomplete, as if you can't take a full, satisfying inhale. The Lungs, lacking support from below, can't properly disperse fluids, leading to accumulation of frothy phlegm. It's like a pump running at low pressure — the fluid moves sluggishly and foams up.

Traditional support: Building Qi and strengthening the Kidney and Lung systems is the focus. Gentle breathing practices (not vigorous exercise, which can deplete energy further) are traditionally recommended. Foods considered supportive include bone broth, oats, sweet potato, walnuts, and astragalus root (Huang Qi, 黄芪) in soups. Adequate rest is more important than any individual food or herb.

Acupressure Points for Phlegm Relief

Acupressure is a gentle self-care technique that involves applying light, steady pressure to specific points on the body. The following points are traditionally used in TCM for coughs with phlegm. Press gently with your fingertip for 1-2 minutes on each side while breathing deeply:

  • ST-40 (Fenglong, 丰隆) — On the outer lower leg, midway between the knee and the ankle, about two finger-widths outside the shinbone. This is TCM's primary point for "transforming Phlegm" — traditionally used for any pattern involving phlegm accumulation. Worth learning this one if you deal with recurrent phlegmy coughs.
  • LU-1 (Zhongfu, 中府) — In the upper chest, in the hollow below the outer end of the collarbone, about one thumb-width below the clavicle. Traditionally used to support Lung Qi and help the Lungs descend and disperse fluids properly.
  • LU-7 (Lieque, 列缺) — On the inner forearm, about 1.5 thumb-widths above the wrist crease, on the thumb side. A classic point traditionally used for coughs and for releasing the "defensive Qi" when external pathogens are involved.
  • CV-22 (Tiantu, 天突) — In the hollow at the base of the throat, just above the breastbone. Apply very gentle pressure only — this point is sensitive. Traditionally used for throat irritation, cough, and the sensation of phlegm stuck in the throat.
A note on self-care: Acupressure is a supportive practice, not a replacement for medical treatment. If a point feels tender or uncomfortable, ease off. Never apply pressure to broken skin, bruises, or areas of inflammation. If your cough is severe or accompanied by fever, shortness of breath, or blood in phlegm, seek medical attention promptly.

Dietary Support: Matching Food to Your Pattern

In TCM theory, food is considered one of the most powerful ways to shift phlegm patterns — because the Spleen (which produces phlegm) is directly fed by what you eat. But the right foods depend on your specific pattern:

What Research Suggests

Modern research has begun exploring some of the traditional approaches used in TCM for coughs with phlegm. While the evidence base is still developing, some findings are worth noting:

  • A 2021 systematic review in Journal of Ethnopharmacology examined multiple TCM formulas for acute and chronic cough and found that several herbal combinations — including those containing Platycodon grandiflorus (Jie Geng, 桔梗) and Fritillaria (Chuan Bei Mu) — were associated with improved symptom scores in some studies. The authors emphasized the need for larger, better-controlled trials.
  • A 2019 clinical trial published in Medicine investigated acupressure as an adjunctive approach for chronic cough in patients with upper airway cough syndrome. Some participants reported improvements in cough frequency and perceived phlegm difficulty.
  • Individual herbs like tangerine peel (Chen Pi) and loquat leaf (Pi Pa Ye) — both commonly used in TCM for phlegm patterns — have demonstrated expectorant and anti-inflammatory properties in laboratory and animal studies. Human clinical research remains limited.

These findings are preliminary. They suggest potential areas for further research rather than providing definitive treatment recommendations. As with any complementary approach, discuss any herbal products or significant dietary changes with your healthcare provider, especially if you have existing medical conditions or take prescription medications.

When to See a Doctor

While this article explores a TCM perspective on persistent cough with phlegm, some symptoms require prompt medical evaluation. Please consult a healthcare provider if you experience:

  • Coughing up blood or blood-tinged phlegm (hemoptysis)
  • Shortness of breath, wheezing, or difficulty breathing at rest
  • High fever (over 101°F / 38.3°C) lasting more than 3 days
  • Unexplained weight loss, night sweats, or persistent fatigue
  • Chest pain that is sharp or worsens with deep breathing
  • Phlegm that suddenly changes from white/clear to dark yellow, green, or brown
  • A cough that persists for more than 8 weeks without improvement despite self-care measures
  • History of COPD, asthma, heart failure, or other chronic conditions — always consult before trying new approaches
A note on safety: TCM is a complementary perspective — it works alongside modern medicine, not in place of it. If you're working with a TCM practitioner, keep your regular doctor informed about any herbs or therapies you're using. Some traditional herbs may interact with prescription medications, including blood thinners, heart medications, and immunosuppressants. Always tell all your healthcare providers about the full range of therapies you're exploring.

Practical Takeaways

  • Listen to your phlegm. Color, texture, and quantity are diagnostic clues in TCM theory. Clear/watery = cold pattern. Yellow/thick = heat pattern. White/sticky = dampness. Frothy/short of breath = Qi deficiency. Identifying your pattern is the first step.
  • Match your diet to your pattern. Warming foods (ginger, garlic) for cold phlegm. Cooling foods (pear, radish) for heat phlegm. Drying foods (barley, tangerine peel) for damp phlegm. Building foods (bone broth, oats) for deficiency.
  • Try ST-40 (Fenglong) acupressure. This is TCM's primary point for transforming phlegm. Located midway between knee and ankle, two fingers outside the shinbone. Gently press for 1-2 minutes each side.
  • Reduce dairy and sugar — these are commonly associated with increased phlegm production in both TCM theory and many people's personal experience. Try eliminating them for 1-2 weeks to see if your phlegm decreases.
  • See a doctor for red-flag symptoms. Blood in phlegm, high fever, chest pain, shortness of breath, or any cough lasting more than 8 weeks requires medical evaluation regardless of what pattern you think you have.
