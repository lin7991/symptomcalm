Chronic Cough and TCM: A Different Approach to Respiratory Health

Symptom Guide · 9 min read

You got over the cold weeks ago. Everyone else is fine. But you're still coughing — that dry, ticklish thing that flares up at night, or the productive cough that just won't clear your chest. You've tried over-the-counter suppressants. Maybe even a round of antibiotics. Nothing seems to fully resolve it.

That nagging cough that lingers beyond the usual 3-4 weeks — medically defined as a persistent or chronic cough when it lasts more than 8 weeks — affects roughly 10-20% of adults at some point in their lives. And the frustration is real: you look fine on paper, your chest X-ray comes back normal, but your body is telling you something is still off.

You're not alone. Chronic cough is one of the most common reasons for outpatient doctor visits worldwide. And while Western medicine has sophisticated diagnostic tools for identifying post-nasal drip, GERD, and asthma (the "three Ps"), some coughs remain unexplained — classified as "idiopathic" or unexplained chronic cough. This is where a complementary TCM perspective may offer a different way of looking at things.

For a broader overview of how TCM views respiratory health, see our Respiratory Health pillar guide.

The Western View: Why Some Coughs Stick Around

From a Western medical perspective, chronic cough is typically investigated through three common triggers: post-nasal drip (upper airway cough syndrome), gastroesophageal reflux (GERD), and asthma or airway hypersensitivity. When these are treated — with antihistamines, acid blockers, or inhaled steroids — the cough often resolves.

But when it doesn't, the diagnostic pathway gets blurry. You might be told your airways are "hyperreactive" or that you have "cough hypersensitivity syndrome" — essentially that your cough reflex is stuck in an overactive loop. Treatments shift to neuromodulators like gabapentin or low-dose morphine. These can help dampen the nerve signals, but they don't explain why the reflex started looping in the first place.

This is where TCM offers a complementary perspective: instead of just turning down the volume on the cough reflex, it asks what underlying imbalance is keeping the system locked in that pattern.

A Different Lens: How TCM Understands Cough

In TCM theory, the cough is not the problem — it's a signal. Think of it like your car's check-engine light. You could tape over the dashboard light (cough suppressant), but the underlying issue in the engine will continue and eventually get worse.

The Lungs are the organ most directly involved, but they don't act alone. In TCM theory, several organ systems interact to keep the respiratory system balanced:

  • The Lungs (肺, Fèi) govern Qi and breathing. They're described as the "tender organ" — most vulnerable to external pathogens like wind, cold, and heat.
  • The Spleen (脾, Pí) produces Qi and fluids from food. When the Spleen is weakened, it can create "Phlegm" (痰, Tán) — a pathological byproduct that clogs the Lungs and triggers coughing.
  • The Kidneys (肾, Shèn) "grasp" the breath. When Kidney Qi is deficient, you may feel like you can't take a deep, satisfying breath — often described as a "short, weak cough."
  • The Liver (肝, Gān) controls the smooth flow of Qi. When Liver Qi is stuck (often from stress), it can "invade" the Lungs, triggering coughs that flare with emotional tension.
The Qi analogy: Think of Qi as the operating system running your body's organs. When programs in the Spleen or Liver module encounter errors (stress, poor diet, exhaustion), they send error signals to the Lung module — and the cough is the error message. TCM looks at which module is sending the errors, not just at the error message itself.

5 TCM Patterns of Chronic Cough

In TCM theory, chronic cough isn't one condition — it's several possible patterns. Identifying which pattern resonates with your experience is the first step toward understanding your body's message. Most people will recognize themselves in one (or a mix) of the following:

1. Lung Yin Deficiency (肺阴虚) — The Dry, Tickling Cough

You might have this if: Your cough is dry and hacking — little to no phlegm. It's worse in the evening or at night, and you may notice a scratchy or sore throat. Your voice might sound hoarse by the end of the day. You sometimes feel heat in your chest or palms, especially at night.

TCM view: Lung Yin is like the lubrication fluid in your respiratory system. After a prolonged illness, chronic stress, or simply with age, this Yin fluid can become depleted — like the oil in a car engine running low. Without moisture, the airways become dry and irritable, triggering coughs from friction alone.

Traditional support: Foods and herbs that are traditionally used to nourish Lung Yin — pear, white fungus (snow fungus), lily bulb, and honey. These are considered "moistening" and cooling, helping to soothe irritated respiratory tissues.

2. Phlegm-Dampness (痰湿蕴肺) — The Productive, Rattling Cough

You might have this if: Your cough brings up phlegm — often white or slightly yellow, thick, and sticky. You feel heaviness in your chest, a sense of fullness, and maybe a "gurgling" sensation when you breathe. Your digestion might be sluggish — bloating, loose stools, fatigue after eating.

TCM view: The Spleen is responsible for transforming food into Qi and Blood. When the Spleen is weakened by a diet high in dairy, fried foods, sugar, or cold drinks, it produces "Dampness" — a turbid fluid that settles in the Lungs and turns into Phlegm. The body coughs to expel it.

Traditional support: Dietary adjustments are central here — reducing damp-producing foods (dairy, sugar, refined carbs, cold beverages) and increasing light, warming foods (ginger, barley, pumpkin, leafy greens). Tangerine peel (陈⽪, Chén Pí) is a well-known ingredient in TCM cooking for its traditional use in transforming Phlegm.

3. Liver Fire Invading the Lungs (肝火犯肺) — The Stress-Triggered Cough

You might have this if: Your cough flares up when you're stressed, angry, or frustrated. It's a harsh, barking cough with little or no phlegm, and may be accompanied by a bitter taste in the mouth, red eyes, headaches, or irritability. You notice it most during or after stressful conversations, work deadlines, or arguments.

TCM view: In TCM theory, the Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. When stress, resentment, or pent-up frustration block this flow, the Qi builds up pressure — like steam in a pipe. If the pressure gets high enough, it "attacks" the Lungs (the next organ in the energy cycle), forcing them into a reactive cough.

Traditional support: Stress management and Liver-soothing practices — gentle movement like walking or qigong, reducing caffeine and alcohol, and herbs traditionally used to smooth Liver Qi like mint tea or chamomile. Addressing the root emotional trigger is key here.

4. Lung-Spleen Qi Deficiency (肺脾气虚) — The Weak, Lingering Cough

You might have this if: You feel like you've never fully recovered from the last cold. Your cough is weak, low in volume, and — unusually — you actually feel better after coughing up a small amount of thin, clear phlegm. You tire easily, have a poor appetite, and your voice sounds faint or breathy.

TCM view: The Lungs and Spleen work as a team: the Spleen produces Qi from food, and the Lungs use that Qi for breathing and immunity. When both are deficient, the body lacks the energy to fully expel pathogens from the respiratory tract. The cough is weak because there's simply not enough Qi behind it — like a half-empty spray bottle.

Traditional support: Building Qi through gentle activities (not intense exercise that would further exhaust you) and foods traditionally used to tonify Qi — oats, sweet potato, chicken soup, dates, and astragalus root (also known as Huang Qi, 黄⺿) in cooking. Rest and adequate sleep are more important than any single remedy.

5. Cold-Phlegm in the Lungs (寒痰阻肺) — Cough with Clear White Phlegm

You might have this if: Your cough brings up thin, white, or watery phlegm. You feel chilly, especially in your back and chest area. Your nose runs clear fluid. You crave warmth and feel better after drinking something hot. Cold weather and air conditioning make your cough significantly worse.

TCM view: This pattern is often the result of an unresolved "Wind-Cold" invasion — what TCM would call a cold that wasn't fully cleared. The cold pathogen gets stuck in the Lungs, congealing fluids into thin phlegm. It's like ice forming in your car's radiator: the fluid thickens and circulation slows.

Traditional support: Warming foods and herbs — fresh ginger tea, cinnamon, garlic, scallions, and black pepper. Avoiding cold drinks (including iced water), dairy, and raw foods. The principle is to warm the Lungs and "evaporate" the cold phlegm, much like turning up the heat to melt ice.

Simple Daily Practices for Supporting Respiratory Comfort

In addition to identifying your pattern, some general practices may help support respiratory health from a TCM perspective. These are not intended to treat chronic cough, but to support your body's natural resilience:

Diet: What to Eat (and What to Avoid)

From a TCM perspective, the Lungs are associated with the Metal element, and foods that support Lung health are typically light, white-colored, and moistening:

  • Eat more: Pears (steamed or poached), white radish, daikon, white mushrooms, cauliflower, almonds, honey, oatmeal, millet, and bone broth.
  • Reduce or avoid: Dairy (especially cold milk and cheese), fried and greasy foods, excessive sugar, ice-cold drinks, and raw foods during winter months — these are traditionally believed to contribute to Phlegm production and Spleen weakness.
  • Simple remedy many people find comforting: Poached pear with rock sugar and a few slices of fresh ginger — a traditional preparation in Chinese households for dry, irritating coughs. Simmer peeled pear chunks in water with a small piece of ginger and a tablespoon of honey or rock sugar for 15-20 minutes.

Self-Acupressure for Occasional Cough Relief

Acupressure is a gentle technique that involves applying light pressure to specific points on the body. The following points are traditionally used in TCM for respiratory support. Press gently with your fingertip for 1-2 minutes on each side, breathing deeply:

  • LU-1 (Zhongfu, 中府) — Located in the upper chest, in the hollow below the outer end of the collarbone, about 1 thumb-width below the clavicle. Traditionally used to support Lung Qi and ease breathing discomfort.
  • LU-7 (Lieque, 列缺) — On the inner forearm, about 1.5 thumb-widths above the wrist crease, on the thumb side. This is a classic point traditionally used for coughs and headaches related to Wind-Cold patterns.
  • CV-22 (Tiantu, 天突) — In the hollow at the base of the throat, just above the breastbone. Apply very gentle pressure — this point is sensitive. Traditionally used for throat irritation and cough.
  • ST-40 (Fenglong, 丰隆) — On the outer lower leg, midway between the knee and the ankle, two finger-widths outside the shinbone. This is TCM's primary point for transforming Phlegm.
Important: Acupressure is a self-care practice, not a medical treatment. If you find a point tender or uncomfortable, ease off. Never apply pressure to broken skin, bruises, or areas of inflammation. If your cough is severe or accompanied by fever or shortness of breath, seek medical attention.

Steam and Humidification

The Lungs are traditionally described in TCM as preferring moisture but not excess dampness. Dry air — common in heated homes during winter or air-conditioned spaces in summer — can aggravate Lung Yin deficiency patterns. A humidifier in your bedroom or a bowl of hot water with a towel over your head (carefully!) for a few minutes of steam inhalation may help soothe irritated airways.

What Research Suggests

Modern research has begun to explore some of the traditional approaches used in TCM for respiratory health. Some preliminary studies suggest that certain TCM herbal formulas may offer benefits for chronic cough management, though more high-quality research is needed:

  • A 2020 systematic review published in Frontiers in Pharmacology examined several TCM formulas for post-infectious cough and found that some formulas were associated with improved symptom scores compared to placebo. The authors noted that larger, better-controlled trials are needed.
  • A 2018 study in Medicine looked at acupressure as an adjunctive approach for patients with chronic cough related to upper airway cough syndrome and reported that some participants experienced improvement in cough frequency and severity.
  • Individual herbs traditionally used in TCM respiratory formulas — such as Platycodon grandiflorus (Jie Geng, 桔梗), Fritillaria (Chuan Bei Mu, 川贝母), and Schisandra (Wu Wei Zi, 五味子) — have been studied in laboratory settings for their potential effects on airway smooth muscle and inflammatory pathways. However, human studies remain limited.

As with any complementary approach, it's worth discussing these options with your healthcare provider, especially if you're already taking medication for other conditions.

When to See a Doctor

While this article explores a TCM perspective on chronic cough, some symptoms require prompt medical evaluation. Please consult a healthcare provider if you experience:

  • Coughing up blood or blood-tinged phlegm (hemoptysis)
  • Shortness of breath or wheezing at rest
  • Unexplained weight loss, fever, or night sweats
  • Chest pain that's sharp or persistent
  • A cough that began after starting a new medication (ACE inhibitors are a common cause)
  • A feeling that something is stuck in your throat accompanied by difficulty swallowing
  • Any cough that persists for more than 8 weeks without improvement
A note on safety: TCM is a complementary perspective — it works alongside modern medicine, not in place of it. If you're seeing a TCM practitioner, keep your regular doctor informed about any traditional herbs or therapies you're using. Some herbs may interact with prescription medications. Always tell all your healthcare providers about the full range of therapies you're exploring.

Practical Takeaways

  • Identify your pattern. Is your cough dry and nighttime (Lung Yin deficiency)? Productive with sticky phlegm (Phlegm-Dampness)? Triggered by stress (Liver Fire)? The right approach depends on the right pattern.
  • Adjust your diet. Reduce dairy, sugar, and cold foods if your cough is phlegmy. Increase moistening, warm foods like pear, oatmeal, ginger tea, and bone broth.
  • Try gentle self-acupressure. LU-1 (upper chest), LU-7 (wrist), and ST-40 (lower leg) are traditional points for respiratory support. 1-2 minutes of gentle pressure each side, with deep breathing.
  • Manage stress. If your cough flares with emotional tension, stress reduction may be as important as any dietary change. Gentle movement, breathing exercises, and adequate rest support the whole system.
  • See a doctor for red-flag symptoms. Blood in phlegm, unexplained weight loss, fever, chest pain, or any cough lasting more than 8 weeks requires medical evaluation.
