Asthma and TCM: Patterns, Triggers, and Breathing Support

Symptom Guide · 7 min read

If you have asthma, you know the feeling: that tightness in your chest, the audible wheeze when you exhale, the sense that you can't quite get enough air. Western medicine treats asthma primarily as an inflammatory airway condition — and that's accurate. But Traditional Chinese Medicine offers a different map of the same terrain, one that can help you understand why your breathing gets stuck and what you can do between flare-ups to support your respiratory health.

Think of your lungs like a pair of bellows. In TCM, the Lungs govern Qi and control breathing — they take in the fresh, clear Qi of the air and push it down through your body. When they're working well, the bellows open and close smoothly. When something blocks that motion — phlegm, weakness, cold, or heat — the bellows stiffen. That's asthma.

This article explores how TCM understands asthma, the most common patterns, and the traditional approaches that have been used for respiratory support over centuries. For a broader look at TCM approaches to respiratory health, see Respiratory Health: A TCM Perspective.

How TCM Views the Lungs and Breathing

In TCM theory, the Lungs (肺, Fèi) have several critical roles beyond respiration as Western medicine understands it:

The Lungs Govern Qi and Control Breathing

The Lungs are the first organ to encounter the outside world — with every breath, they take in the "air Qi" (空気, kōng qì) and combine it with the Qi derived from food (Gu Qi, 谷气) to form Zong Qi (宗气, or Gathering Qi). This is the Qi that powers your voice, your circulation, and your physical stamina. When Lung function weakens, breathing becomes shallow, the voice grows thin, and you tire easily.

The Lungs Control the Skin and Defensive Qi

This is one of the most unique concepts in TCM: the Lungs are said to "control the skin and body hair" and govern Wei Qi (卫气, Defensive Qi) — the body's immune barrier. Think of Wei Qi as your personal force field, a layer of protective energy that circulates just beneath the skin, regulating the pores and repelling external pathogens like cold, wind, and allergens.

Western parallel: The Wei Qi concept maps well to the mucosal immune system and the skin barrier. The respiratory tract is the body's largest interface with the external environment — and TCM has recognized this frontline defense role of the Lungs for thousands of years.

The Kidneys "Grasp" the Qi

This is critical for understanding asthma. In TCM, breathing is a two-step process: the Lungs inhale the Qi, but the Kidneys grasp it and hold it down. If the Kidneys are weak, the Qi isn't held — it floats back up. You feel like you can take a breath in, but you can't get it to go down deep enough. This is the hallmark of Kidney-not-grasping-Qi (肾不纳气, shèn bù nà qì), a core pattern in chronic asthma.

The Most Common TCM Asthma Patterns

In TCM, asthma isn't a single condition — it's a symptom with several underlying patterns. Identifying your pattern helps explain what triggers your flare-ups and what kind of support may be most appropriate.

Lung Qi Deficiency (肺气虚)

Key features: Weak cough, shortness of breath on mild exertion, low voice, frequent colds, spontaneous sweating, pale complexion

Feels like: Your lungs just don't have the power. You can take air in, but you can't push it out with force. Your cough is weak and ineffective.

Common triggers: Weather changes, cold exposure, physical exhaustion

Kidney Qi Deficiency / Kidney-Not-Grasping-Qi (肾不纳气)

Key features: Shortness of breath that worsens on exertion or when lying flat, difficulty inhaling fully, lower back weakness, frequent urination, cold extremities, asthma that has been present for years

Feels like: You can take air in, but it "bounces" right back out. The breath feels shallow no matter how hard you try. Exhaling takes conscious effort.

Common triggers: Overwork, aging, sleep deprivation, the cold season

Phlegm-Dampness Obstructing the Lungs (痰湿阻肺)

Key features: Wheezing with copious phlegm (white or clear), feeling of heaviness in the chest, digestive bloating, a greasy mouth coating, loose stools

Feels like: Something is clogging your airways. You can hear and feel the phlegm rattling when you breathe. Coughing brings up mucus, which gives temporary relief.

Common triggers: Dairy, greasy foods, cold drinks, damp weather, sugar

Cold-Phlegm (寒痰阻肺)

Key features: Wheezing with thin white phlegm, feeling cold, aversion to cold, no thirst, pale tongue with white coating

Feels like: A tight, cold band around your chest. You feel worse in cold weather and better in warmth. Your lips may look pale or purplish.

Common triggers: Cold air, ice-cold drinks, swimming in cold water, air conditioning

Heat-Phlegm (热痰阻肺)

Key features: Wheezing with thick, yellow or green phlegm, thirst, irritability, feeling hot, red face, rapid breathing, yellow tongue coating

Feels like: Your lungs feel hot and tight. Breathing is labored and uncomfortable. You feel restless and warm.

Common triggers: Hot weather, spicy foods, alcohol, respiratory infections

Critical note: Many people have mixed patterns — for example, a chronic Lung-Kidney deficiency with acute flare-ups driven by Heat-Phlegm or Cold-Phlegm. This is called Ben Biao (本标, root-branch) in TCM: the root is the underlying weakness, the branch is the acute trigger. Effective support addresses both layers.

Asthma Triggers from a TCM Perspective

TCM has a distinct framework for understanding what triggers an asthma attack. These concepts often align with Western triggers but offer additional insight.

External Pathogens (外邪, Wài Xié)

The most common asthma triggers in TCM are Wind-Cold and Wind-Heat — essentially, catching a cold or flu. The Wind pathogen carries Cold or Heat into the body through the skin and nose (both governed by the Lungs). This is why asthma attacks often follow a common cold or abrupt weather change.

This aligns with Western understanding: viral respiratory infections are among the most common asthma triggers.

Dietary Factors

In TCM theory, diet directly influences phlegm production. Foods that "generate Dampness and Phlegm" include:

  • Dairy products — milk, cheese, yogurt (especially cold dairy)
  • Cold and raw foods — cold drinks, ice cream, salads, raw vegetables
  • Greasy and fried foods — these are considered "damp-heat" generating
  • Sugar and sweets — dampen the Spleen, leading to phlegm accumulation
  • Alcohol — generates Damp-Heat, especially in the Lungs

This doesn't mean you need to eliminate all of these — but if you notice your asthma worsens after certain meals, the TCM dietary framework gives you a language for understanding why.

Emotional Stress

The Lungs are associated with the emotion of grief and sadness in TCM. Chronic grieving, unresolved sadness, or prolonged worry can weaken Lung Qi. Meanwhile, stress and frustration create Liver Qi Stagnation, which can "invade" the Lungs — in TCM theory, the Liver controls smooth flow everywhere, and when Liver Qi gets stuck, it can push upward into the chest, causing tightness and wheezing.

Overwork and Exhaustion

Chronic overwork depletes Kidney Qi. Since the Kidneys are responsible for "grasping" the breath, Kidney deficiency means the breath isn't held — it floats up, leading to shortness of breath and wheezing that gets worse the more tired you are.

Traditional TCM Self-Care Approaches for Asthma

The following are traditionally used practices in TCM. They are not medical recommendations — always follow your doctor's asthma management plan first.

Acupressure Points for Breathing Support

These points are traditionally used to support respiratory function between flare-ups. Apply gentle, firm pressure with your thumb for 1-2 minutes while breathing deeply.

  • Lung 1 (中府, Zhōng Fǔ) — located on the upper chest, about 2 finger-widths below the collarbone and 2 inches from the centerline. Traditionally used to down-regulate Lung Qi and relieve chest tightness.
  • Lung 7 (列缺, Liè Quē) — on the radial side of the forearm, about 1.5 inches above the wrist crease, in the depression where the muscle belly starts. One of the most commonly used Lung meridian points. Some studies suggest it may influence respiratory function.
  • Kidney 3 (太溪, Tài Xī) — in the depression between the Achilles tendon and the inner ankle bone. Used when asthma is linked to Kidney deficiency (chronic, longstanding cases with difficulty inhaling deeply).
  • Ren Mai 22 (天突, Tiān Tū) — in the hollow at the base of the throat, above the sternum. Traditionally used for coughing, wheezing, and throat tightness. Use very gentle pressure here.
  • Bladder 13 (肺俞, Fèi Shū) — on the upper back, about 2 finger-widths from the spine at the level of the space between the 3rd and 4th thoracic vertebrae. This is the "Lung Back Shu" point, traditionally used for any chronic lung condition. Ask a partner to help reach this one.
Important: Acupressure is a complementary self-care practice. It does not replace your rescue inhaler or prescribed asthma medication. During an acute asthma attack, use your medication as prescribed and seek emergency care if needed.

Dietary Support (Traditionally)

In TCM dietary therapy, foods are classified by their thermal nature and their effect on the organs. For asthma support, the traditional focus is on strengthening the Lungs and Kidneys while reducing phlegm:

  • Pears (especially stewed) — traditionally used to moisten the Lungs and clear Heat. Stewed Asian pear with a little rock sugar (冰糖炖雪梨) is a classic remedy for dry cough and wheezing.
  • White foods — in TCM color theory, white corresponds to the Metal element and the Lungs. Daikon radish, white mushrooms, cauliflower, and garlic are traditionally considered Lung-supportive.
  • Walnuts — traditionally used to support Kidney Qi, which helps "grasp" the breath. A traditional recommendation is to eat 2-3 walnuts daily, especially in winter.
  • Ginger and scallions — warming foods that help expel Cold-Phlegm. Fresh ginger tea with a bit of honey is a simple traditional practice.
  • Mushrooms (shiitake, reishi) — traditionally used to support Wei Qi (defensive Qi) and strengthen the immune barrier.
  • Oats and barley — traditionally considered to support the Spleen and reduce Dampness, indirectly reducing phlegm production.
  • Warm, cooked foods — in general, warm cooked meals are easier on the digestive system (and by extension, the Lungs) than raw, cold, or iced foods.

Breathing Practices (Traditionally Recommended)

Certain breathing patterns in Qigong are traditionally used to strengthen Lung function:

  • Extended exhalation — breathe in through your nose for 4 counts, then exhale through pursed lips for 6-8 counts. This helps move stagnant Qi out of the Lungs.
  • "The Six Healing Sounds" (六字诀) — the specific sound for the Lungs is "Sssssssss" (like a slow leak), made as you exhale fully. It's traditionally believed to release excess Heat and Phlegm from the Lungs.
  • Abdominal breathing — place a hand on your belly and breathe so that your hand rises on the inhale. This engages the diaphragm fully and is a gentle way to improve lung capacity over time.

What Research Says

Scientific exploration of TCM approaches to asthma is ongoing:

  • A 2023 systematic review of acupuncture for asthma found that acupuncture, when used alongside conventional treatment, showed potential benefits for symptom control and quality of life. The authors noted that more high-quality trials are needed.
  • A 2021 meta-analysis of Chinese herbal medicine for asthma suggested that certain formulas may help reduce asthma exacerbations and improve lung function measurements like FEV1. However, many studies had methodological limitations.
  • Acupressure at specific points (particularly Lung 1 and Ding Chuan, an extra point on the upper back) has been studied in small trials for wheeze reduction, with mixed but promising results.
  • The TCM concept of "Kidney not grasping Qi" has been explored in relation to the role of the HPA axis and adrenal function in chronic respiratory conditions, with some researchers drawing parallels between Kidney deficiency and the physiological effects of long-term corticosteroid use.

TCM is a complementary system. It works alongside, not instead of, conventional medical care. Always follow your doctor's treatment plan.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Asthma can be life-threatening. If you experience any of the following, seek emergency medical attention immediately:

  • Your rescue inhaler doesn't relieve symptoms
  • You're too breathless to speak in full sentences
  • Your lips or fingernails look blue or gray
  • You feel confused, agitated, or extremely drowsy
  • Your breathing is rapid and getting faster, not slower

This article is educational. It does not replace your asthma action plan or medical treatment. Consult your healthcare provider before making any changes to your asthma management.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Identify your pattern. Is your asthma driven by weakness (Lung or Kidney deficiency) or excess (Phlegm-Cold or Phlegm-Heat)? The pattern determines the approach. If you have a chronic, long-standing asthma with difficulty getting the breath deep enough, Kidney support may be appropriate. If your asthma flares with phlegm after eating dairy, dietary changes targeting Dampness may help.
  2. Support your Lungs between flare-ups. Gentle breathing practices, warm cooked foods, and acupressure at Lung 1 and Lung 7 are traditionally used when you're not in an acute episode.
  3. Warmth matters. In TCM theory, Cold is a primary trigger for asthma. Keeping your chest and back warm (especially in cold weather), avoiding ice-cold drinks, and eating warm foods are simple traditional practices.
  4. Work with a professional. If you're interested in exploring acupuncture or Chinese herbal medicine for asthma support, look for a licensed TCM practitioner. In the US, NCCAOM certification ensures a recognized standard of training.
  5. Track your triggers. Notice patterns: does your breathing get worse after certain foods, in cold weather, at night, or when you're stressed? These observations are valuable for both Western and TCM approaches.
