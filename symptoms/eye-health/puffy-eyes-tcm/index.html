Puffy Eyes and Eye Bags: A TCM Guide to Brighter Eyes

Symptom Guide · 8 min read

You wake up, glance in the bathroom mirror, and there they are — puffy, slightly swollen eyes that make you look tired before the day has even begun. Maybe the puffiness goes down after an hour with coffee and cold water on your face. Or maybe those under-eye bags have become a permanent fixture — a daily reminder that something isn't quite right.

If you've chalked it up to lack of sleep, salty late-night snacks, or just genetics, you're not wrong — those can all contribute. But TCM offers a deeper explanation: puffy eyes are not simply a cosmetic issue. They are a visible signal from your body's fluid management systems, and understanding that signal can help you address the root cause rather than just masking the puffiness.

Think of it this way: Your body has a sophisticated water management system — like a network of canals, pumps, and drainage channels that keep fluid moving where it needs to go. In TCM, the Spleen is the main pump that transforms fluids into usable energy, while the Kidney acts as the master regulator of water metabolism. When either system is underperforming, fluid accumulates in areas with loose tissue — and the lower eyelid, being one of the thinnest and most delicate areas of skin, is the first place this shows up.

What Western Medicine Says About Puffy Eyes

From a conventional medical perspective, periorbital puffiness (swelling around the eyes) has several common causes:

  • Fluid retention — Changes in salt and water balance can cause fluid to pool under the eyes, especially after a high-sodium meal or during hormonal shifts.
  • Sleep position and quality — Sleeping flat allows fluid to redistribute to the head. Poor sleep disrupts circulation, making it harder for fluid to drain overnight.
  • Aging and skin changes — As we age, the membrane (septum) that holds fat pads under the eyes weakens. The fat pushes forward, creating the appearance of bags or bulges.
  • Allergies — Histamine release during allergic reactions increases blood flow and fluid leakage into surrounding tissues, causing swelling around the eyes.
  • Dietary factors — High-sodium foods, alcohol, and dehydration can all disrupt the body's fluid balance, leading to morning puffiness.
  • Thyroid and kidney conditions — In some cases, persistent puffiness can signal underlying health issues that require medical evaluation.

Most of the time, occasional puffiness is harmless and self-limiting. But if it's become a regular occurrence — or if the bags under your eyes seem to be getting worse regardless of sleep and diet — it's worth looking at the deeper patterns that TCM recognizes.

How TCM Sees Puffy Eyes: The Spleen and Kidney's Role in Fluid Balance

In TCM, the area around the eyes — particularly the lower eyelid — is considered a diagnostic window into the body's internal state. The lower eyelid is directly linked to the Spleen system, which governs the transformation and transportation of fluids throughout the body.

The Spleen in TCM is responsible for what might be called your body's fluid processing plant. When you eat and drink, the Spleen extracts the usable parts (Qi and Blood) and ensures that waste fluids are directed to the right elimination pathways. When the Spleen is strong, fluids are processed efficiently and nothing accumulates where it shouldn't.

But when Spleen Qi is deficient — from poor diet, overthinking, irregular eating habits, or a constitutional weakness — the fluid processing system slows down. Unprocessed fluids turn into what TCM calls Dampness: a thick, heavy quality in the body that settles in areas of least resistance. The loose tissue under the eyes is one of those areas.

The Kidney system plays a complementary role. In TCM, the Kidney governs water metabolism at the deepest level — it's the master temperature regulator that provides the "heat" needed to transform and move fluids. When Kidney Yang (the warming, activating aspect of Kidney function) is sufficient, fluids flow freely. When Kidney Yang is deficient, water accumulates like a river that slows and pools in winter's cold.

A practical analogy: Imagine the Spleen as a water treatment plant and the Kidney as the pumping station that keeps water circulating through the pipes. If the treatment plant is sluggish (Spleen Qi deficiency), the water that enters the system doesn't get processed properly and starts to stagnate. If the pump loses power (Kidney Yang deficiency), the water doesn't circulate well and pools in low-lying areas. Puffy eyes happen when both or either of these systems is not working at full capacity.

Three Common TCM Patterns Behind Puffy Eyes

Not all puffy eyes have the same root cause. The right approach depends on which TCM pattern is at play. See which one resonates most with your experience:

1. Spleen Qi Deficiency with Dampness (脾虚湿盛)

You might have this if: Your puffy eyes are most noticeable in the morning, especially after a heavy or late dinner. You also tend to feel bloated after eating, have loose or sticky stools, feel mentally foggy, and your energy dips noticeably after meals. The puffiness may improve as the day goes on and you start moving.

The TCM view: This is the most common pattern behind puffy eyes. The Spleen is struggling to transform fluids, so unprocessed Dampness accumulates. Since the lower eyelid is the Spleen's diagnostic area in TCM, fluid waste collects here first — like a drainage system backing up at its lowest point.

Modern parallel: This pattern resembles the effects of dietary patterns that overwhelm digestive capacity — frequent large meals, high sugar intake, or irregular eating habits. Digestive enzyme activity may be suboptimal, leading to poor nutrient absorption and fluid retention that shows in the face.

2. Kidney Yang Deficiency (肾阳虚)

You might have this if: Your puffiness is accompanied by a general feeling of cold — cold hands and feet, a sensation that you never quite warm up. You may also have lower back ache, frequent urination (especially at night), low libido, and a tendency toward fatigue that feels deeper than just needing more sleep. The puffiness may persist throughout the day.

The TCM view: Kidney Yang provides the metabolic "heat" that keeps fluids moving. When Kidney Yang is insufficient, water accumulates throughout the body — and the face is often the first place this becomes visible. This pattern is more common with age, after prolonged illness, or in people who have pushed themselves too hard for too long.

Modern parallel: This pattern may correspond to a slowing of metabolic processes, including reduced renal blood flow or changes in the renin-angiotensin system that regulates fluid balance. The feeling of cold and fatigue aligns with reduced basal metabolic rate seen in some subclinical thyroid conditions.

3. Damp-Heat in the Spleen (湿热困脾)

You might have this if: Your eyes look not just puffy but also slightly reddened or irritated. The skin under the eyes may feel greasy or you're prone to styes or milia. You may also have a feeling of heaviness, a bitter taste in your mouth, acne or oily skin, and a tendency to crave fatty or spicy foods. Your tongue might feel coated.

The TCM view: When Dampness persists, it can combine with internal Heat to create Damp-Heat — a sticky, congested condition that's harder to resolve than simple Dampness. The fluid under the eyes is not just stagnant but also "hot," leading to a combination of puffiness and inflammation.

Modern parallel: This pattern shares features with low-grade systemic inflammation combined with fluid retention. The combination of puffiness and redness suggests an inflammatory component that simple water retention alone wouldn't explain — possibly linked to diet, gut health, or metabolic factors.

Dietary Strategies for Reducing Puffy Eyes

In TCM, food is one of the most powerful tools for addressing fluid imbalance. The following dietary principles target the root causes of puffy eyes — weak Spleen function, insufficient Kidney Yang, or accumulated Dampness.

Foods That Support the Spleen and Transform Dampness

  • Barley (yi ren / coix seed) — One of TCM's most important herbs for resolving Dampness. Barley tea or barley added to soups helps the body drain excess fluid. It's traditionally used for edema and puffiness.
  • Job's tears (coix seed) — Similar to barley, this grain is specifically indicated in TCM for Dampness-related swelling. Available as tea or in congee (rice porridge).
  • Pumpkin and winter squash — Sweet, warming, and supportive to the Spleen. They help strengthen the digestive system without creating Dampness.
  • Green beans and mung beans — Cooling and draining, these help clear Damp-Heat and support fluid metabolism. Mung bean soup is a traditional summer remedy for fluid retention.
  • Adzuki beans — Known in TCM for their ability to drain Dampness and promote urination, making them useful for fluid-related puffiness.
  • Turnips and daikon radish — Help the body transform and move stagnant fluids. They're particularly useful when Dampness is accompanied by digestive sluggishness.

Warming Foods for Kidney Yang Support

  • Ginger — A warming herb that helps the body move fluids and supports the Spleen and Kidney systems. Fresh ginger tea in the morning can be particularly helpful for cold-type puffiness.
  • Cinnamon — Strongly warming, cinnamon is traditionally used to support Kidney Yang and circulate Qi. A small amount in tea or cooking can help warm the system.
  • Walnuts — In TCM, walnuts tonify the Kidney and support lower back strength. They're a convenient snack that also provides healthy fats.
  • Dark leafy greens (cooked) — Kale, spinach, and chard, when lightly cooked (not raw), support Blood and Qi production without taxing the Spleen.
  • Bone broth and soups — Long-simmered soups are considered highly nutritious in TCM and are easier to digest than raw or dry foods. They support both Spleen and Kidney systems.

Foods to Reduce or Avoid

  • Raw and cold foods — Ice water, smoothies, raw salads, and cold drinks weaken the Spleen's ability to transform fluids. Room temperature or warm beverages are gentler on the digestive system.
  • Excessive dairy — Milk, cheese, and yogurt can contribute to Dampness in TCM. If puffy eyes are a chronic issue, try reducing dairy for two weeks and observe the difference.
  • Fried and greasy foods — These are difficult to digest and create internal Dampness and Heat, worsening puffiness.
  • High-sodium foods — This aligns with both Western and TCM perspectives. Processed foods, soy sauce, and salty snacks cause fluid retention.
  • Sugar and refined carbohydrates — These weaken the Spleen and promote Dampness, contributing to the overall fluid imbalance.

Acupressure Points for Eye Puffiness

Acupressure is one of the most practical self-care tools for reducing under-eye puffiness. These points help move stagnant fluids, strengthen the Spleen and Kidney systems, and promote drainage from the facial area.

⚠️ Important: Use gentle, steady pressure — never press directly on the eyeball. Wash your hands before touching your face. If acupressure causes pain or worsening symptoms, stop and consult a healthcare provider. These are complementary self-care techniques, not medical treatments.

1. Zusanli (ST-36) — The "Leg Three Miles" Point

Location: Four finger-widths below your kneecap, one finger-width toward the outside of your shin bone.

How to use: Press firmly with your thumb for 15–20 seconds on each leg. You should feel a mild ache or sensation. Repeat 3 times, breathing deeply between repetitions.

TCM function: ST-36 is one of the most important acupoints for strengthening the Spleen and Stomach. It supports the body's ability to transform fluids and generate Qi. When used regularly, it helps address the root cause of Spleen Qi deficiency — one of the main drivers of fluid retention and puffy eyes.

2. Sanyinjiao (SP-6) — The "Three Yin Intersection"

Location: Four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, just behind the shin bone on the inside of the leg.

How to use: Press firmly for 15–20 seconds on each leg. This point can be quite sensitive — adjust pressure as needed. It is contraindicated during pregnancy.

TCM function: SP-6 is the meeting point of the Spleen, Liver, and Kidney meridians. It helps regulate fluid metabolism by harmonizing all three systems. It's particularly effective for Dampness-related conditions and fluid retention.

3. Taiyuan (LU-9) — The "Great Abyss"

Location: On the wrist crease, on the thumb side, in the hollow just below the base of the thumb.

How to use: Press gently for 10–15 seconds on each wrist. This point is on the Lung meridian, which governs the skin and body surface.

TCM function: LU-9 regulates water passages throughout the body. In TCM, the Lung regulates the waterways — it helps distribute fluids to the skin surface and drain them downward. Stimulating this point supports the body's overall fluid circulation.

4. Cuanzhu (BL-2) — The "Bamboo Gathering" Point

Location: In the small hollow at the inner end of your eyebrow, right above the inner corner of your eye.

How to use: Using both thumbs or index fingers, press gently into the hollow for 10–15 seconds. Breathe deeply and release slowly. Repeat 3 times.

TCM function: BL-2 helps clear local fluid stagnation around the eyes. It's a direct point for sinus and facial drainage, making it useful for reducing visible puffiness by promoting fluid movement from the periorbital area.

Lifestyle Habits for Brighter Eyes

Beyond diet and acupressure, several lifestyle practices in TCM can help reduce puffy eyes by addressing the underlying patterns:

Sleep Position and Timing

In TCM, the hours of 11 PM to 3 AM are considered the time when the body's detoxification and renewal processes are most active — specifically governed by the Gallbladder (11 PM–1 AM) and Liver (1 AM–3 AM). Going to bed after midnight means these systems work less efficiently, and fluid metabolism suffers. Sleeping with an extra pillow to slightly elevate the head can also help prevent overnight fluid pooling around the eyes.

Morning Movement

Gentle movement in the morning — even five minutes of walking or light stretching — helps get fluids moving. In TCM, morning is when Yang Qi begins to rise and circulate. Supporting this natural upward flow with movement helps clear the stagnant Dampness that settled in the head overnight.

Warm Water Ritual

Starting the day with a cup of warm or hot water (with a slice of fresh ginger if you tend toward the cold pattern) gently activates the Spleen and Kidney systems. Cold water first thing in the morning can shock the digestive system and temporarily slow fluid metabolism. This simple practice is one of the most accessible ways to support TCM-inspired eye care.

Facial Self-Massage

Using clean fingers, gently massage the area around the eyes in small outward circles — from the inner corner along the brow bone, then from the inner corner along the lower orbital bone. This supports lymphatic drainage from the periorbital area. A few minutes of gentle massage can help reduce morning puffiness significantly.

What Research Suggests

While TCM's framework for understanding fluid metabolism has been refined over centuries, modern research is beginning to explore mechanisms that align with these traditional observations:

  • A 2018 systematic review in Medicine found that acupuncture may help improve periorbital microcirculation and reduce local fluid retention in some individuals, though larger controlled trials are needed to confirm these findings.
  • Reducing dietary sodium is well-established in medical literature as a strategy to reduce fluid retention and edema — consistent with the TCM recommendation to minimize Dampness-forming foods like excess salt, dairy, and refined sugar.
  • Traditional TCM herbal formulas used for fluid regulation (such as Wu Ling San and Fang Ji Huang Qi Tang) have been the subject of clinical research, with some studies suggesting they may influence aquaporin channels and renal fluid handling — though most research has been conducted in China and larger international trials are needed.
  • The connection between chronic stress and altered fluid distribution is supported by research on cortisol's effects on vascular permeability and lymphatic function — paralleling the TCM observation that Liver Qi stagnation can manifest as fluid accumulation around the eyes.
  • A 2019 study in Frontiers in Physiology found that sleep deprivation alters fluid distribution and impairs lymphatic clearance from the head and neck region, supporting the TCM emphasis on proper sleep timing (before midnight) for eye health.

This research is promising but preliminary. TCM is best used as a complementary perspective — one that works alongside regular eye exams and proper medical care.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ While occasional puffy eyes are usually harmless, seek medical attention if you experience: puffiness that is sudden, severe, or accompanied by pain; swelling that affects only one eye significantly more than the other; redness, warmth, or tenderness around the eye that suggests infection; puffy eyes along with swelling in your legs or ankles (this may signal a kidney or thyroid condition); or any vision changes — blurred vision, double vision, or sensitivity to light. TCM is a complementary approach — it does not replace proper medical diagnosis and treatment.

Practical Takeaways

What You Can Do Starting Today

✅ Start your morning with warm water — add fresh ginger if you tend to feel cold. Replace cold smoothies with warm congee or oatmeal for breakfast.

✅ Reduce raw and cold foods — especially in the evening. Cook your vegetables, drink room-temperature water, and avoid ice-cold drinks.

✅ Use acupressure on ST-36 and SP-6 daily — even 30 seconds on each point can support fluid metabolism over time.

✅ Elevate your head slightly while sleeping — an extra pillow can reduce overnight fluid pooling around the eyes.

✅ Do gentle facial massage in the morning — outward circular strokes around the orbital bone support lymphatic drainage.
