Dry Eyes and TCM: Natural Approaches for Lasting Moisture

Symptom Guide · 6 min read

You wake up and your eyes feel gritty — like sand is lodged under your lids. By mid-morning you've reached for eye drops twice. They provide relief for about ten minutes, then the dryness creeps back. You blink more, rub your eyes, and wonder: why won't this go away?

In Western medicine, dry eye syndrome is a chronic condition affecting an estimated 344 million people worldwide. It happens when your tear glands don't produce enough tears — or produce poor-quality tears that evaporate too quickly. Standard treatments include artificial tears, warm compresses, anti-inflammatory drops, and in more serious cases, punctal plugs to slow tear drainage. These approaches can help manage symptoms, but they often treat the surface rather than the underlying imbalance.

Traditional Chinese Medicine takes a different approach. Instead of asking "what can we put on your eyes to keep them wet?", TCM asks: "why has your body stopped producing enough moisture to begin with?"

The answers may surprise you — and they involve more than just your eyes.

How TCM Sees Eye Moisture

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, the eyes are not a self-contained organ. They are considered a direct extension of your body's fluid management systems — specifically, a network of three interconnected organ systems that work together to produce, transport, and regulate moisture throughout the body.

Think of it this way: Your body has an internal irrigation system. The Liver is the water main that directs flow, the Kidneys are the deep underground reservoir that supplies the water, and the Lungs are the sprinkler system that disperses moisture to the surface. Dry eyes happen when any part of this system isn't working properly — the main is low on pressure, the reservoir is running dry, or the sprinklers aren't spreading moisture evenly.

The Liver: Your Eye's Water Main

TCM holds that "the Liver opens into the eyes" (肝开窍于目). This is one of the oldest and most consistent observations in the tradition: the health of your eyes is directly tied to the state of your Liver Blood and Liver Yin.

Liver Blood is the substance that physically nourishes the eye tissues — the cornea, the conjunctiva, the tear glands. Liver Yin is the body's cooling, moistening fluid that keeps those tissues supple and hydrated. When Liver Blood is sufficient and Liver Yin is abundant, the eyes stay naturally moist. When either runs low, the eyes are the first place to show the deficit — just like the farthest sprinkler head in a garden is the first to sputter when water pressure drops.

The Kidneys: Your Body's Water Reservoir

Kidney Yin (sometimes called "True Yin" or "Original Yin") is the deepest source of all moistening fluids in the body. Think of it as the underground aquifer that feeds the entire irrigation system. Kidney Yin is largely inherited — you're born with a certain reserve — and it naturally depletes with age. This is why dry eyes become more common after age 40, and why TCM connects age-related dry eye to the gradual waning of Kidney Yin.

When Kidney Yin is abundant, the body has enough fluid foundation to keep all tissues — including the eyes — well-hydrated. When it's depleted, the entire system shifts toward dryness, and the eyes are among the first places this shows.

The Lungs: Your Moisture Sprinkler System

The Lungs in TCM are responsible for dispersing and descending fluids — essentially, taking the moisture produced by the Spleen and stored by the Kidneys, and circulating it throughout the body, especially to the skin, mucous membranes, and eyes. When Lung Yin is healthy, this distribution happens smoothly. When Lung Yin is depleted — from dry environments, chronic coughing, or excessive talking — the eyes lose their top-up of surface moisture.

Three TCM Patterns Behind Dry Eyes

Not all dry eyes are the same. In TCM, the treatment depends on which part of your internal irrigation system is malfunctioning. See which pattern sounds most like your experience:

1. Liver Yin and Blood Deficiency (肝阴血虚) — Gritty, Tired Eyes

You might have this if: Your eyes feel dry, gritty, or sandy — especially in the afternoon or evening. Eye drops provide brief relief but the dryness returns quickly. You also notice brittle nails, pale complexion, occasional dizziness, scanty periods (if applicable), and a tendency toward floaters. Your vision may blur or fluctuate when you're tired.

The TCM view: Liver Blood and Yin normally keep the eyes supple and nourished. Prolonged screen use, sleep deprivation (Liver Blood is restored during deep sleep), chronic stress, and diets low in blood-building foods create a cumulative deficit. The eyes — being the farthest reach of the Liver system — show the shortage first, like a sprinkler head at the far end of the garden sputtering before the closer ones.

Modern parallel: Research links meibomian gland dysfunction and reduced tear production with insufficient omega-3 intake and poor sleep quality — factors that in TCM theory fall under "depletion of nourishing substances."

2. Kidney Yin Deficiency (肾阴虚) — Dry Eyes with Age-Related Changes

You might have this if: You're over 40 and have noticed a gradual increase in eye dryness over years rather than weeks. Your eyes feel dry but not particularly gritty or inflamed. You may also have lower back ache, ringing in the ears (tinnitus), night sweats, or feel warm in the evenings. Your eyes feel less lubricated in dry or heated indoor environments.

The TCM view: Kidney Yin is the deepest source of moisturizing fluids in the body. As it naturally declines with age — and faster with chronic stress, overwork, or insufficient rest — the eyes lose their fluid foundation. This is why TCM sees age-related dry eye as a Kidney Yin issue, not just a local eye problem.

Modern parallel: The global prevalence of dry eye disease increases with age, affecting up to 33% of adults over 50. Tear production naturally declines with aging, and hormonal changes (particularly in menopause) further reduce tear quality — both consistent with the TCM concept of declining Kidney Yin.

3. Lung Yin Deficiency with Dryness (肺阴虚) — Dry Eyes with Dry Skin and Throat

You might have this if: Your eyes are dry, but so is your skin, throat, and nasal passages. You live or work in a dry environment — air-conditioned office, heated indoor spaces, or low-humidity climate. You may have a dry cough, tickly throat, or notice that your eyes feel worse in the morning after sleeping in a heated room.

The TCM view: The Lungs control the body's surface and disperse fluids to the skin, mucous membranes, and eyes. When Lung Yin is depleted — from dry air, insufficient hydration, or a prolonged illness — the body loses its ability to keep the "finishing layer" of moisture on the eyes and skin. This is distinct from Liver or Kidney patterns because it's primarily an issue of distribution rather than production.

Modern parallel: Low indoor humidity, found in heated and air-conditioned buildings, is a well-documented environmental trigger for dry eye symptoms. A 2018 study in Ocular Surface found that relative humidity below 40% significantly increased tear evaporation rates — directly supporting the TCM observation that dry environments deplete surface moisture.

4. Liver Fire Rising (肝火上炎) — Hot, Red, Burning Dry Eyes

You might have this if: Your eyes feel hot and burning — not just dry, but actively inflamed and red. Artificial tears sting rather than soothe. You may have red or bloodshot eyes, temple headaches, a bitter taste in your mouth, irritability, and feel worse after alcohol, coffee, or spicy food.

The TCM view: When Liver Qi stagnation (often from stress or frustration) persists without release, it can transform into "Fire" — an overheated state that rises upward along the Liver channel to the eyes. This heat literally burns off the fluids that should lubricate the eyes, creating a paradoxical state of burning and dryness at the same time. Cooling the heat is the priority here — not just adding moisture.

Modern parallel: Chronic stress elevates inflammatory markers in the body, including in the eye. A 2019 study in Ocular Immunology and Inflammation found that individuals with higher perceived stress scores had significantly higher tear inflammatory cytokine levels — supporting the link between emotional states and ocular surface health.

Acupressure Points for Eye Moisture

Acupressure is one of the most practical self-care tools TCM offers for dry eyes. These points help move Qi and Blood into the eye area and support the organ systems responsible for tear production. They are safe, require no equipment, and can be done at your desk or in bed.

⚠️ Important: Apply gentle, steady pressure — never press directly on the eyeball. Stop if you feel sharp pain. These are complementary self-care techniques, not medical treatments. If dry eyes are accompanied by vision changes, pain, or redness, consult an eye doctor.

1. Jingming (BL-1) — The "Bright Eyes" Point

Location: In the hollow between your nose bridge and the inner corner of your eye.

How to use: Gently press with thumb and index finger simultaneously on both sides for 10-15 seconds. Breathe deeply. Release slowly. Repeat 3 times.

TCM function: This is the most direct acupoint for eye health. It clears heat, brightens vision, and is traditionally used for dry eyes, blurry vision, and eye fatigue. In TCM theory, it helps unblock the channel that carries fluids to the eye surface.

2. Zanzhu (BL-2) — The "Bamboo Gathering" Point

Location: In the small indent at the inner end of your eyebrow, just above the bridge of your nose.

How to use: Press with both index fingers for 10 seconds. Follow with gentle circular massage for another 10 seconds. You may feel a subtle release in the muscles around your eyes.

TCM function: This point relieves frontal pressure and supports the flow of fluids and Qi to the eye area. It's especially helpful when dry eyes are accompanied by a heavy or tight feeling across the forehead.

3. Sanyinjiao (SP-6) — Three Yin Intersection

Location: About four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, just behind the shin bone — on the inside of your lower leg.

How to use: Press firmly with your thumb for 15-20 seconds while breathing slowly. Repeat on the other leg. This point may feel slightly tender — that's normal.

TCM function: This is one of TCM's most important points for nourishing Yin and Blood. It influences the Liver, Spleen, and Kidney channels simultaneously — making it highly relevant for dry eyes rooted in any of the three Yin-deficient patterns. It is contraindicated during pregnancy.

4. Zusanli (ST-36) — The "Leg Three Miles" Point

Location: About four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width toward the outside of the shin bone.

How to use: Press firmly for 15-20 seconds. Repeat on the other leg. This point works well with deep, slow breathing.

TCM function: ST-36 is the body's main point for building Qi and Blood from the food you eat. It strengthens the Spleen and Stomach — the system responsible for generating the raw materials that eventually become the fluids your eyes need. It's not a quick fix, but with consistent use over weeks, it can help rebuild the foundation of your internal irrigation system.

Nourishing Foods for Eye Moisture

In TCM, what you eat directly determines the quality and quantity of the fluids your body produces. Here are foods traditionally used to support eye moisture, organized by the pattern they address:

For Liver Blood and Yin (most common dry eye pattern)

  • Goji berries (gou qi zi) — The most famous TCM eye food. They nourish Liver Blood and brighten vision. Modern research confirms they are one of the richest natural sources of zeaxanthin, a carotenoid that accumulates in the retina. A simple goji tea (1 tbsp steeped in hot water for 5 minutes, drink the water and eat the berries) is a gentle daily practice.
  • Beets and dark leafy greens — Blood-building foods rich in iron and folate. In TCM theory, when Blood is abundant, it can flow to and nourish the eyes.
  • Mulberries (sang shen) — In TCM these are said to nourish both Liver and Kidney Yin, directly supporting eye moisture. Available dried or as a syrup.
  • Animal liver (in moderation) — Traditionally used to nourish Liver Blood. Rich in vitamin A, which is essential for tear production and corneal health.

For Kidney Yin (age-related dry eye)

  • Black sesame seeds — Nourish Kidney and Liver Yin. A tablespoon of black sesame powder stirred into warm rice porridge or oatmeal is a classic TCM breakfast for eye health.
  • Bone broth — Rich in collagen, gelatin, and minerals that support the Kidneys and build Essence. A cup of bone broth daily is considered a nourishing, restorative practice in TCM dietary therapy.
  • Walnuts — Tonify Kidney Yin. They are also a source of omega-3 fatty acids, which are associated with reduced dry eye symptoms in some studies.
  • Cooked dark beans (black beans, adzuki beans) — In TCM, black-colored foods are associated with the Water element and the Kidneys.

For Lung Yin (dryness-related dry eye)

  • Pear — TCM considers pears the most effective fruit for moistening Lung dryness. A steamed pear with a few goji berries and a small piece of rock sugar is a traditional remedy.
  • Lotus root — Moistens the Lungs and generates fluids. Lotus root soup or simply boiled lotus root slices are a gentle daily addition.
  • White fungus (tremella/yin'er) — Known as the "poor woman's bird's nest" in TCM, this is a powerful moisturizing food for both Lungs and skin. Can be made into a dessert soup with goji berries and rock sugar.
  • Cucumber and celery — High water content foods that in TCM theory help generate body fluids.

Daily Habits for Eye Hydration

Beyond specific foods and acupressure, here are TCM-informed daily practices that support long-term eye moisture:

  • Warm water, not cold. TCM emphasizes room-temperature or warm liquids over ice-cold drinks. Cold fluids can temporarily slow the body's fluid metabolism, reducing the production of the moistening substances that ultimately reach the eyes. Start your morning with a glass of warm water — it's a small shift with subtle effects.
  • Rest your eyes before midnight. Liver Blood and Yin restoration happens most efficiently during deep sleep, particularly before midnight in TCM chronobiology. If you consistently sleep past midnight, your eyes may never get the full nourishment they need — regardless of what you eat or drink.
  • Use a humidifier in dry environments. In TCM, external dryness can deplete Lung Yin and worsen eye dryness. Keeping indoor humidity above 40% directly supports your body's moisture distribution system.
  • Blink fully and frequently. From a TCM perspective, a full blink helps spread Liver Blood and Kidney fluids across the eye surface. Incomplete blinking — common during screen use — doesn't effectively distribute these fluids. Try closing your eyes completely and gently for 2-3 seconds every few minutes.
  • Manage stress to prevent Liver Fire. If your dry eyes feel hot or burning, stress management becomes eye care. A daily 5-minute breathing practice (breathe in for 4, hold for 4, out for 6) can help prevent Liver Qi from transforming into heat that dries out your eyes.

What Research Says

While TCM's framework for understanding dry eyes has existed for centuries, modern research is beginning to explore connections that align with these traditional observations:

  • A 2020 meta-analysis of 16 randomized controlled trials in Medicine found that acupuncture may improve tear film stability, increase tear production, and reduce dry eye symptoms compared to artificial tears alone — though more high-quality research is needed.
  • Goji berries (Lycium barbarum) have been shown in multiple studies to increase serum zeaxanthin levels and may help protect retinal cells from oxidative stress — research that parallels TCM's traditional use of goji for eye health.
  • Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation has been associated with improved meibomian gland function and reduced dry eye symptoms in several clinical trials — consistent with the TCM recommendation of walnuts and black sesame for eye moisture.
  • A 2019 study in Ocular Immunology and Inflammation found that chronic stress is associated with elevated inflammatory markers in tear fluid, supporting the TCM observation that emotional states directly affect eye health.

This research is promising but preliminary. TCM is a complementary perspective — it works alongside, not instead of, regular eye exams and proper medical care for dry eye syndrome.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ If you experience any of the following, seek medical attention promptly: sudden vision loss, persistent double vision, eye pain that doesn't improve with rest or lubrication, a sensation of something stuck in your eye that doesn't wash out with eye drops, eye redness accompanied by pain or light sensitivity, or any vision changes following an eye injury. Dry eye syndrome is common and usually manageable, but it's important to rule out underlying conditions such as Sjögren's syndrome, blepharitis, or meibomian gland dysfunction. Schedule an annual comprehensive eye exam even if your symptoms seem mild.
