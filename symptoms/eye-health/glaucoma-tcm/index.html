Glaucoma and TCM: Understanding Eye Pressure Through an Ancient Lens

Symptom Guide · 10 min read

Glaucoma is often called the "silent thief of sight" — and for good reason. By the time most people notice something is wrong, the condition has already caused irreversible damage to the optic nerve. It affects an estimated 80 million people worldwide, and nearly half don't even know they have it.

If you've been diagnosed with elevated intraocular pressure (IOP) or early-stage glaucoma, your ophthalmologist has likely prescribed eye drops to lower pressure and scheduled regular check-ups. That's the gold standard of care — and it's essential. But you may also be wondering: Is there more I can do? What's actually causing this pressure? Could my overall health be connected to what's happening in my eyes?

Traditional Chinese Medicine offers a different lens — one that connects eye pressure to the balance of organ systems throughout your entire body. This article explores the TCM perspective on glaucoma while emphasizing that it must always be managed in partnership with an eye care professional.

Important: Glaucoma is a serious medical condition that requires proper diagnosis and treatment by an ophthalmologist. TCM approaches discussed here are complementary — they may support overall eye health and well-being but should never replace prescribed medications or regular eye exams. If you suspect you have glaucoma, see an eye doctor immediately.

What Western Medicine Tells Us About Glaucoma

From a biomedical perspective, glaucoma is a group of eye conditions that damage the optic nerve — the cable that connects your eye to your brain. The most common form, primary open-angle glaucoma, develops gradually and painlessly. The eye's drainage system becomes less efficient over time, causing fluid (aqueous humor) to accumulate. This increases intraocular pressure, which slowly damages the optic nerve fibers.

Think of it like this: the eye continuously produces fluid, and that fluid needs an open drain to maintain stable pressure. In glaucoma, that drain gets partially clogged. Pressure builds — like water pressure in a closed pipe — and the most delicate part of the system (the optic nerve) is the first to suffer.

Key risk factors include age (over 60), family history, elevated IOP, African or Hispanic ancestry, thin corneas, and conditions like diabetes and hypertension. Treatment typically focuses on lowering IOP through medicated eye drops, laser therapy, or surgery. These approaches are well-supported by clinical evidence and remain the frontline defense against vision loss.

But a growing body of research is also looking beyond IOP alone. Studies now recognize that glaucoma involves more than just pressure — factors like reduced ocular blood flow, oxidative stress, neuroinflammation, and impaired self-regulation of blood vessels also contribute to optic nerve damage. And that's where the TCM perspective begins to offer meaningful complementary insight.

A Different Lens: How TCM Sees Glaucoma

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, the eyes are considered an external window into the body's internal state. The famous TCM classic, the Huang Di Nei Jing (The Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon), states that "the five viscera' essence all ascend to the eyes" — meaning the health of your eyes reflects the balance of your entire organ system.

For glaucoma specifically, TCM theory points to several interconnected mechanisms, unified by a central theme: what should flow freely has become blocked, and what should be cool has become heated.

Imagine the body's internal environment as a house with a plumbing system. The Liver regulates the smooth flow of Qi — it's the pressure regulator. The Kidneys provide the coolant level (Yin fluids) that keeps the system from overheating. The Spleen manages fluid metabolism — the drainage system. When any of these components malfunction, the result can manifest as rising pressure, heat, and stagnation in the uppermost part of the body: the eyes.

3 TCM Patterns Commonly Associated with Glaucoma

Which one resonates most with your overall health picture?

1. Liver Yang Rising (肝阳上亢) — Pressure, Headaches, Tinnitus

You might have this if: You experience a feeling of pressure or distension in your eyes, along with headaches (especially at the temples or sides of the head). You may have tinnitus (ringing in the ears), dizziness, a flushed face, and feel easily irritated or impatient. Your blood pressure may also run on the higher side.

TCM view: In TCM theory, Liver Yang Rising is one of the most common patterns associated with elevated intraocular pressure. The Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. When Liver Yin (the cooling, moistening aspect of the Liver) is depleted — often from chronic stress, anger, sleep deprivation, or age — the Yang (active, hot, rising) aspect can flare upward unchecked. Since the Liver channel reaches the eyes, this rising Yang directly increases pressure and heat in the visual system.

Common triggers: Chronic stress, repressed frustration, high-sodium or spicy diet, alcohol consumption, lack of restorative sleep, and age-related Yin depletion.

2. Kidney Yin Deficiency with Liver Yang Rising (肝肾阴虚) — The Age-Related Pattern

You might have this if: You're over 50 and noticing not just eye pressure but also lower back ache, weak knees, dry eyes or dry mouth, night sweats, or feeling warm in the evenings. You may have trouble sleeping or feel a persistent low-level anxiety. This pattern often combines visual changes with signs of systemic dryness and heat.

TCM view: This is perhaps the most clinically common pattern in age-related glaucoma. Kidney Yin is the body's foundational cooling and moistening substance — like the coolant in a car engine. As Kidney Yin naturally declines with age, the Liver (which is "mother" to the Kidney in TCM's Five Element framework) also loses its Yin support. This leaves Liver Yang unanchored — it rises like steam from a pot that's running low on water. The result is a combination of systemic Yin deficiency (dryness, heat signs) and local Yang rising (eye pressure).

Common triggers: Aging, chronic overwork, insufficient sleep over many years, prolonged stress, and a diet lacking nourishing, Yin-supportive foods.

Research connection: A 2021 observational study in the Journal of Traditional Chinese Medicine found that Kidney-Liver Yin deficiency was the most frequently diagnosed pattern among primary open-angle glaucoma patients in a TCM clinic setting in China.

3. Phlegm-Dampness with Blood Stasis (痰湿瘀阻) — Fluid Stagnation Pattern

You might have this if: You experience a heavy, dull sensation in your head or eyes rather than sharp pain. You may feel foggy-headed, lethargic after meals, and have a tendency toward water retention or sinus congestion. Your tongue may feel thick or coated, and your body may feel heavier than it should.

TCM view: This pattern involves two layers of obstruction. First, Phlegm-Dampness — a turbid fluid accumulation resulting from Spleen Qi deficiency (poor fluid metabolism). Second, Blood Stasis — sluggish circulation in the microvessels of the eye. Together, they create a physical obstruction within the ocular drainage system that contributes to elevated IOP. This pattern is particularly relevant because it parallels the modern understanding that impaired ocular blood flow and vascular dysregulation play a role in glaucoma progression.

Common triggers: A diet high in greasy, sweet, or dairy-heavy foods, lack of physical activity, damp climates, prolonged sitting, and underlying Spleen weakness.

Research connection: Studies using optical coherence tomography angiography (OCTA) have documented reduced blood flow in the optic nerve head and peripapillary region in glaucoma patients — findings that align with the TCM concept of Blood Stasis contributing to optic nerve damage.

Important: These patterns are diagnostic frameworks used in TCM theory, not medical diagnoses. Most people with glaucoma do not fit neatly into one pattern, and a qualified TCM practitioner can identify the specific combination that applies to your individual constitution. Never stop or adjust prescribed glaucoma medications based on a TCM pattern assessment.

How TCM Approaches Glaucoma Supportively

In the context of glaucoma, TCM's role is primarily supportive — working alongside conventional medical treatment to address underlying systemic imbalances that may contribute to disease progression. Here are approaches a TCM practitioner might discuss:

  • Acupuncture: Some preliminary research suggests acupuncture may influence intraocular pressure and ocular blood flow. A 2019 systematic review of acupuncture for glaucoma (9 studies, 422 participants) found that acupuncture was associated with a modest reduction in IOP compared to baseline, though the quality of evidence was limited by small sample sizes and short follow-up periods. Points commonly used include Taiyang (EX-HN5), Fengchi (GB-20), and distal points like Taichong (LV-3) and Taixi (KI-3). Acupuncture may also support stress reduction, which indirectly benefits the Liver Qi stagnation component of many glaucoma patterns.
  • Herbal support: Some TCM formulas are traditionally used to support patterns associated with glaucoma — such as Qi Ju Di Huang Wan (for Liver-Kidney Yin deficiency with Liver Yang rising) or Tian Ma Gou Teng Yin (for Liver Yang rising patterns). However, critical caution is needed here: some Chinese herbs can interact with glaucoma medications (particularly those affecting the autonomic nervous system or intraocular pressure). For example, licorice (gan cao) in large amounts may increase IOP or interfere with steroid metabolism. Never take TCM herbal formulas without consulting both a qualified TCM practitioner and your ophthalmologist.
  • Dietary adjustments: From a TCM perspective, foods that nourish Kidney Yin and Liver Blood may support the organ systems involved in eye health. These include cooked dark leafy greens, black sesame seeds, goji berries, mulberries, bone broth, and small amounts of organ meats in traditional diets. Anti-inflammatory foods (omega-3-rich fish, turmeric, green tea) align with both TCM's anti-Heat principles and modern understanding of neuroinflammation in glaucoma.
  • Stress management: Chronic stress is a known risk factor for elevated IOP, likely through increased cortisol and sympathetic nervous system activation. TCM practices like qigong, breathwork, and moderate exercise can help downregulate the stress response — directly addressing the Liver Qi stagnation pattern that contributes to Liver Yang Rising.
⚠️ Critical safety note: Several herbs commonly used in TCM require caution in glaucoma patients. Ephedra (ma huang) is known to increase intraocular pressure and should be strictly avoided. Aconite (fu zi) and other warming herbs may aggravate Liver Yang Rising patterns. Ginkgo biloba (while sometimes studied for ocular blood flow) can interact with blood thinners and glaucoma medications. Always disclose all supplements and herbs to your ophthalmologist.

What Research Says

Scientific interest in the connections between TCM and glaucoma is growing, though research remains preliminary:

  • A 2019 systematic review and meta-analysis (9 studies, 422 participants) in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine found that acupuncture was associated with a modest IOP-lowering effect in glaucoma patients compared to baseline, with effects appearing more pronounced in normal-tension glaucoma. Authors noted significant methodological limitations across studies.
  • An experimental study in Neural Regeneration Research (2020) found that certain TCM compounds traditionally used for Blood Stasis patterns showed potential neuroprotective effects on retinal ganglion cells in animal models — the same cells that degenerate in glaucoma.
  • OCTA imaging studies have confirmed reduced peripapillary capillary density in glaucoma patients, lending visual evidence to the concept of ocular microvascular stagnation — a key feature of the Blood Stasis pattern in TCM.
  • A 2022 review in Frontiers in Medicine explored the overlap between TCM patterns and modern biomarkers in glaucoma, noting that Liver Yang Rising patients showed higher levels of oxidative stress markers and HPA axis dysregulation compared to other TCM patterns.

This research is promising but preliminary. The strongest evidence in glaucoma management remains IOP-lowering medications, laser therapy, and surgery. TCM approaches should be considered complementary — supportive strategies that, when used safely alongside standard care, may support overall eye health and well-being.

Practical Takeaways

If you have glaucoma or elevated intraocular pressure, here are steps to discuss with your healthcare team:

  1. Follow your ophthalmologist's treatment plan. Eye drops, laser, and surgery are evidence-based and proven to slow glaucoma progression. No TCM approach replaces these. If you experience side effects from your drops, talk to your doctor — don't stop them on your own.
  2. Pay attention to stress. Chronic stress activates the sympathetic nervous system and can raise both blood pressure and intraocular pressure. Even 10 minutes of daily breathwork, walking, or gentle qigong may help regulate the stress response — which in TCM terms means supporting Liver Qi smooth flow and reducing the conditions for Yang Rising.
  3. Support Kidney Yin through diet and sleep. Kidney Yin is your body's cooling reserve — it's depleted by chronic stress, poor sleep, and aging. Prioritize 7-8 hours of quality sleep, and include Yin-nourishing foods: bone broth, black sesame, goji berries, seaweed, and cooked leafy greens.
  4. Avoid known aggravating factors. In TCM theory, alcohol, smoking, excessive caffeine, spicy foods, and fried foods can all generate or aggravate Heat and Yang Rising patterns. Reducing these may support overall balance, even if the effect on IOP is indirect.
  5. Get regular comprehensive eye exams. Glaucoma is an incurable but manageable condition when caught early. Annual or semi-annual eye exams with IOP measurement and optic nerve assessment are essential, especially if you have risk factors. TCM is a complementary perspective — it doesn't replace ophthalmological monitoring.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Glaucoma is a medical emergency of the eye. Seek immediate ophthalmological care if you experience: sudden eye pain or redness, severe headache with eye pain, nausea or vomiting accompanied by eye pain, sudden visual disturbances (blurred vision, halos around lights), or sudden vision loss in one or both eyes. These may signal acute angle-closure glaucoma, which requires urgent treatment to prevent permanent vision loss. TCM is a complementary approach — it does not replace emergency eye care or proper medical diagnosis and treatment.
