Eye Acupressure Points: Simple Self-Care for Tired Eyes

Symptom Guide · 6 min read

Your eyes feel heavy. They burn, water, or ache by mid-afternoon. You rub them, blink hard, maybe reach for eye drops — but the relief never lasts. If this sounds familiar, you're not alone. Millions of people experience eye fatigue from screen work, reading, driving, or simply the demands of modern life.

Before reaching for another bottle of artificial tears, consider this: Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) has a simple, drug-free approach to tired eyes that you can do anywhere, anytime. It's called acupressure — and it uses gentle pressure on specific points around your eyes and head to stimulate circulation, release tension, and support the body's natural energy flow.

Unlike acupuncture (which uses needles), acupressure is fully self-administered. You use your fingertips. No special equipment. No appointments. Just a few minutes and a basic understanding of where to press.

Why Your Eyes Feel Tired: A TCM Perspective

In TCM theory, the eyes are deeply connected to the body's internal organ systems — especially the Liver. The classic TCM text Huangdi Neijing (The Yellow Emperor's Inner Classic) states: "The Liver opens into the eyes." This means the health and vitality of your eyes reflect the state of your Liver system, particularly Liver Blood.

Think of Liver Blood as the body's internal moisturizing and nourishing system. Your eyes depend on a steady supply of Blood and Qi (vital energy) to stay moist, focused, and comfortable. When Liver Blood is abundant, your eyes feel bright and clear. When it's depleted — from long hours staring at screens, poor sleep, chronic stress, or aging — your eyes are the first to show it.

Several energy channels (meridians) also pass through or around the eye area:

  • Gallbladder channel — runs along the side of the head and outer corner of the eye. Important for side-of-head tension and eye fatigue from decision fatigue or stress.
  • Bladder channel — starts at the inner corner of the eye and runs over the top of the head. Connected to tension headaches that radiate from the eyes upward.
  • Stomach channel — passes beneath the eye. TCM links this channel to puffiness, dark circles, and under-eye bags.
  • Liver channel — reaches the eye area internally. Liver Qi stagnation is a common contributor to eye strain, dry eyes, and visual fatigue.

Acupressure works by stimulating these channels at specific points, encouraging blood flow, releasing facial tension, and helping the body's energy circulate freely around the eyes.

The 5 Key Acupressure Points for Tired Eyes

Here are the most effective points for eye fatigue. For each point, use your index or middle fingertip. Apply firm, comfortable pressure — not to the point of pain — and hold for 30–60 seconds while breathing slowly. You can use circular or steady pressure, whichever feels better.

1. Zanzhu (BL-2) — Inner Eyebrow

Location: In the small notch at the inner end of your eyebrow, right where the brow meets the bridge of your nose. You'll feel a slight indentation.

How to press: Place both thumbs or index fingers on these points. Press upward and slightly inward. You may feel a mild ache or tingling sensation — that's normal.

Best for: Tired, heavy-feeling eyes, frontal headaches, and sinus pressure around the eyes. In TCM, this point is said to clear the channel connecting the eye to the Bladder meridian, releasing tension that accumulates from prolonged focus.

2. Yuyao (EX-HN4) — Eyebrow Midpoint

Location: Directly above the pupil, in the middle of your eyebrow arch. If you run your finger along the eyebrow, this is the highest point.

How to press: Use your index fingers to press straight upward into the brow ridge. Maintain steady pressure — avoid sliding off the bone.

Best for: General eye fatigue, brow tension from squinting, and pain behind the eyes after screen work. This is a "extra" point (not on a specific meridian) that TCM tradition has used for centuries specifically for vision-related complaints.

3. Taiyang (EX-HN5) — Temple Hollow

Location: In the soft hollow of your temple, about one finger-width behind the outer corner of your eye and slightly above.

How to press: Use your middle fingers. Press gently but firmly into the hollow. Small circular motions can enhance the effect.

Best for: Eye strain accompanied by temple tension, stress headaches, and a feeling of tightness around the eyes. Taiyang is arguably the most well-known point for eye fatigue in TCM — it's commonly used in self-massage routines and traditional Chinese eye exercises.

4. Sibai (ST-2) — Below the Eye

Location: Directly below the pupil, in the hollow of your cheekbone, about a finger-width below the lower eyelid.

How to press: Use your index fingers. Press upward into the bone — not into the eye itself. The sensation should be felt on the cheekbone, not inside the eye socket.

Best for: Eye puffiness, dark circles, and a feeling of heaviness under the eyes. Because this point is on the Stomach meridian, TCM uses it when eye fatigue is accompanied by digestive symptoms or fluid retention — both of which can contribute to under-eye bags and a tired appearance.

5. Fengchi (GB-20) — Base of Skull

Location: In the two hollows at the base of your skull, between the neck muscles. Run your fingers up from the back of your neck until you find the natural indentation just below the skull bone.

How to press: Use both thumbs. Press upward into the skull base. This point can be quite tender — apply less pressure if needed and build up over time.

Best for: Eye fatigue that comes with tension headaches, neck stiffness, or a feeling of pressure behind the eyes. GB-20 is a powerful point because it releases tension along the Gallbladder channel, which runs from the outer corner of the eye up the side of the head and down the neck. Releasing GB-20 can have an immediate effect on how relaxed your eyes feel.

The 5-Minute Eye Acupressure Routine

You don't need to practice acupressure perfectly to get benefits. This simple routine takes five minutes and can be done at your desk, on the couch, or before bed:

  1. Prepare (30 seconds). Rub your palms together until they feel warm, then gently cup them over your closed eyes. Take three slow, deep breaths. This "palming" technique relaxes the eye muscles and signals to your nervous system that it's time to rest.
  2. Zanzhu (BL-2) — 60 seconds. Press both points simultaneously. Breathe slowly. You may notice the ache behind your eyes start to soften.
  3. Yuyao (EX-HN4) — 30 seconds. Press the midpoint of each eyebrow. If one side feels more tender, that's common — spend a few extra seconds there.
  4. Taiyang (EX-HN5) — 60 seconds. Press the temple hollows. Try slow, small circles in one direction, then the other.
  5. Sibai (ST-2) — 30 seconds. Press below the eyes. Keep your touch gentle — this area is sensitive.
  6. Fengchi (GB-20) — 60 seconds. Press the base of the skull. This is the most relaxing step — many people find this point releases tension they didn't realize they were holding.
  7. Cool down (30 seconds). Cup your palms over your eyes again. Breathe. Notice any difference in how your eyes feel.

Repeat this routine 1–3 times daily, especially after extended screen time. Consistency matters more than pressure intensity — even a gentle daily practice can produce noticeable results over a few weeks.

When to Be Careful With Eye Acupressure

Acupressure is generally very safe, but the area around the eyes is delicate. Keep these precautions in mind:

  • Wash your hands before touching your face to avoid introducing bacteria near the eyes.
  • No direct pressure on the eyeball. All points described here are on the bone (eyebrow ridge, cheekbone, temple) — not on the eyelid or eye itself.
  • Avoid acupressure if you have an active eye infection, recent eye surgery, or a serious eye condition like glaucoma or retinal detachment — unless cleared by your doctor.
  • Use lighter pressure if you wear contact lenses, or remove them before the routine.
  • Stop if you feel sharp pain — a dull ache or "good pain" is normal, but sharp or stabbing pain is not.

What Western Science Says

While acupressure for eye fatigue has not been studied as extensively as acupuncture, the existing research is supportive:

  • Acupuncture vs. artificial tears for dry eye: A 2020 meta-analysis in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine (14 RCTs, 1,157 participants) found that acupuncture was comparable to or better than artificial tears for improving dry eye symptoms and tear film stability. While this studied needling rather than finger pressure, it supports the concept that stimulating these meridian points can influence eye moisture and comfort.
  • Facial acupressure for eye strain: A small 2019 study in the Journal of Traditional Chinese Medical Sciences found that a 5-minute facial acupressure protocol reduced subjective eye strain scores by 32% in office workers compared to a control group.
  • Trigger point release and eye symptoms: Research on myofascial trigger points (2017, Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies) found that treating trigger points in the muscles around the eyes — many of which correspond to traditional acupressure points — reduced eye strain and tension headaches. This provides a mechanistic bridge between the TCM channel model and the Western understanding of referred pain and muscle tension patterns.

More rigorous studies with larger sample sizes are needed, but the existing evidence supports acupressure as a low-risk, potentially helpful complement to conventional eye care.

Practical Takeaways

  1. The 5 key points (Zanzhu, Yuyao, Taiyang, Sibai, Fengchi) form a complete eye fatigue protocol. You don't need to learn dozens of points — these five cover the major channels that influence eye comfort.
  2. Consistency beats intensity. A gentle 5-minute daily routine is more effective than an aggressive 20-minute session once a week. Make it a habit: after lunch, at the end of your workday, or before bed.
  3. Combine acupressure with the 20-20-20 rule. Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Add a quick acupressure press of Taiyang (temple) during these breaks to enhance the effect.
  4. Don't ignore the bigger picture. Tired eyes are often a signal of broader imbalances — poor sleep, stress, or Liver Blood depletion. Acupressure helps the symptom, but addressing the root cause matters for long-term relief.
  5. Keep your regular eye exams. Acupressure is a complementary practice. It doesn't replace routine vision checks, prescription updates, or medical treatment for eye conditions.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ If you experience any of the following, seek medical attention promptly: sudden vision loss, flashing lights or a sudden increase in floaters, eye pain that doesn't improve with rest, double vision, or any vision change following a head injury. These are potentially serious conditions that require proper medical diagnosis and treatment. Acupressure can be a wonderful self-care tool for everyday eye fatigue, but it is not a substitute for professional eye care.
