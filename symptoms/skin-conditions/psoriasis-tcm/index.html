Psoriasis from a TCM View: Blood Heat and Blood Stasis Patterns

Cluster · 7 min read

If you have psoriasis, you know there's nothing "just dry skin" about it. The thick, silvery plaques. The stubborn patches that refuse to budge. The flare-ups that seem to follow a stressful week, a winter chill, or a throat infection. The topical creams that manage the surface but never quite stop the next cycle from arriving.

Psoriasis affects roughly 2–3% of the global population — over 7.5 million people in the U.S. alone. It's classified as an autoimmune condition: the immune system speeds up skin cell turnover, producing new cells every 3–5 days instead of the normal 28–30 days. The result is that buildup of dead cells piling up into plaques.

But from a TCM perspective, the accelerated skin cell turnover isn't the root cause — it's a symptom. The real question is: what's driving the system to overproduce?

Two patterns dominate psoriasis in TCM theory: Blood Heat (血热) and Blood Stasis (血瘀). They often overlap — especially in chronic psoriasis — but understanding the difference can help you and your healthcare provider find the most appropriate complementary approach.

Blood Heat: The Fire Driving Rapid Cell Turnover

In TCM theory, Blood Heat is exactly what it sounds like: a state where the Blood system runs hot. Think of it like a thermostat set too high — everything in the body speeds up and inflames as a result.

When Blood Heat affects the skin, it creates red, inflamed plaques that feel warm to the touch. New plaques appear rapidly because the Heat is active and spreading. The silvery scale is thin and sheds easily — like ash from a fire — because the Heat pushes cells to the surface faster than they can mature.

Signs of Blood Heat in Psoriasis

  • Bright red plaques that look inflamed and feel warm
  • Rapidly spreading patches — new spots appear during active flare-ups
  • Thin, silvery scale that flakes off easily
  • Itching with a burning quality
  • Flare-ups triggered by stress, spicy food, alcohol, or hot weather
  • May be accompanied by thirst, irritability, a red tongue tip, or a rapid pulse (in TCM tongue/pulse diagnosis)

Where Blood Heat comes from: In TCM theory, Blood Heat can arise from several sources. Chronic stress and frustration generate Liver Heat (肝火) that enters the Blood. A diet rich in spicy, greasy, or alcohol-heavy foods creates Stomach Heat that also seeps into the circulation. Prolonged inflammation — from any cause — heats the Blood over time. And in some people, there's a constitutional tendency toward Heat that runs in families.

Analogy: Think of Blood Heat like a radiator running too hot. The skin is the radiator's surface — when the internal temperature is too high, the surface overheats and cells proliferate rapidly. Cooling the coolant (Blood) is more effective than just wiping down the radiator surface (topical creams).

Blood Stasis: The Chronic, Stubborn Side of Psoriasis

If Blood Heat is the active fire, Blood Stasis (血瘀) is the residue it leaves behind. Over time, chronic inflammation thickens the blood, slows circulation to the skin, and creates plaques that become fixed, thick, and dark.

Blood Stasis in TCM means blood that isn't flowing properly — it's sluggish, congested, or "stuck." When the skin doesn't receive fresh, nourishing blood, it can't shed dead cells properly, and the plaques become entrenched. This is why long-standing psoriasis plaques are thicker, darker, and harder to shift than newer ones.

Signs of Blood Stasis in Psoriasis

  • Dark red or purplish plaques — older patches that have lost their bright red color
  • Thick, raised, indurated plaques that feel hard to the touch
  • Slow to change — these patches persist for months or years with minimal improvement
  • Dry, cracked skin on the plaques, sometimes with fissures that bleed
  • Scaly buildup that is thick and adherent (not flaky like Blood Heat)
  • May be accompanied by joint pain (psoriatic arthritis), a dark or purplish tongue, or a choppy pulse in TCM diagnosis

How Blood Stasis develops: Blood Stasis is often the end stage of untreated or long-standing Blood Heat. The Heat inflames and damages the blood vessels over time, making circulation sluggish. Dead cells and inflammatory byproducts accumulate in the tissues. The skin becomes starved of nutrients, so it can't repair itself. Psoriatic arthritis — where the same inflammatory process affects the joints — is viewed in TCM as Blood Stasis affecting the sinews and channels.

Analogy: Imagine a river (your bloodstream). Blood Heat is like the river running fast and hot after heavy rain — carrying debris, eroding banks. Blood Stasis is like a downstream section where the debris has settled, forming a dam. The water behind the dam stagnates, becomes darker, and nothing flows through. The plaque is that stagnation made visible on the skin.

How Blood Heat and Blood Stasis Interact in Psoriasis

In practice, most people with chronic psoriasis have both patterns present in different proportions. A typical progression in TCM theory:

  1. Active phase (Blood Heat dominant): New plaques appearing rapidly, bright red, inflamed, itchy. This phase may be triggered by stress, infection (especially strep throat), or dietary triggers.
  2. Stable phase (mixed): Some plaques persist while others fade. Existing plaques darken and thicken. New flares are less frequent but still occur.
  3. Chronic phase (Blood Stasis dominant): Long-standing plaques that are thick, dark, and fixed. Minimal new activity, but the old plaques won't resolve. Psoriatic arthritis may be present.

The TCM approach differs depending on which phase is dominant. A person with active, flaring psoriasis needs cooling and clearing (addressing Blood Heat). Someone with chronic, fixed plaques needs moving and transforming (addressing Blood Stasis). Many need a combination.

Traditional Approaches in TCM for Psoriasis

TCM offers several avenues traditionally used to address the patterns underlying psoriasis. The guiding principle is to clear Heat, cool the Blood, move Stagnation, and nourish the skin — not just suppress the plaques.

Herbal Strategies for Blood Heat

For the active, Blood Heat phase of psoriasis, TCM herbal formulas traditionally focus on cooling the Blood and clearing Heat. Classic herbs used in these formulas include:

  • Sheng Di Huang (Rehmannia root, 生地黄) — A primary herb for cooling Blood Heat. In TCM theory, it clears Heat from the Blood level and nourishes Yin (body fluids), preventing the Heat from drying out the tissues.
  • Mu Dan Pi (Moutan bark, 牡丹皮) — Cools Blood Heat and helps move Blood Stasis simultaneously. A dual-action herb that addresses both the active inflammation and the stagnant residue.
  • Chi Shao (Red Peony root, 赤芍) — Cools Blood and moves Stasis. Traditionally used for any red, inflamed skin condition with a Stagnation component.
  • Zi Cao (Lithospermum root, 紫草) — A classic herb for skin conditions with Heat and redness. In TCM theory, it cools Blood, clears toxins, and promotes rash resolution.
  • Bai Xian Pi (Dictamnus bark, 白鲜皮) — Clears Damp-Heat from the skin and is traditionally used for a wide range of inflammatory skin conditions, including psoriasis.

Herbal Strategies for Blood Stasis

For the chronic, Blood Stasis phase, formulas shift toward moving Blood and softening hard plaques:

  • Dan Shen (Salvia root, 丹参) — The most important herb for activating Blood circulation and resolving Stasis. Some laboratory studies suggest it contains compounds (tanshinones) with anti-inflammatory and anti-proliferative effects, which may relate to its traditional use for chronic skin conditions.
  • Tao Ren (Peach kernel, 桃仁) and Hong Hua (Safflower, 红花) — A classic pair for breaking up Blood Stasis. Traditionally used together to move stagnant blood in chronic conditions.
  • San Leng (Scirpus, 三棱) and E Zhu (Curcuma, 莪术) — Stronger Blood-moving herbs reserved for stubborn, fixed plaques with significant Stasis. Used cautiously under professional guidance.
  • Ji Xue Teng (Spatholobus stem, 鸡血藤) — Moves Blood and nourishes it simultaneously. Particularly useful when Blood Stasis has been present long enough to deplete the Blood.
Important: Herbal medicine should always be prescribed by a qualified TCM practitioner. Many of these herbs are potent — they can interact with prescription medications (including immunosuppressants and biologics commonly used for psoriasis) and may not be appropriate for everyone. The information here describes traditional uses in TCM theory; it is not a recommendation to self-treat.

Acupuncture for Psoriasis

Acupuncture is traditionally used to regulate the flow of Qi and Blood, cool Heat, and move Stasis. Points commonly selected in TCM practice for psoriasis include:

  • LI-11 (Quchi, 曲池): A key point for clearing Heat from the skin. Used for any red, hot, inflammatory skin condition.
  • SP-10 (Xuehai, 血海): Known as the "Sea of Blood" point, traditionally used to cool and regulate Blood. Very commonly needled in skin conditions with a Blood Heat or Blood Stasis component.
  • BL-13 (Feishu, 肺俞): The Back-Shu point of the Lung, which governs the skin in TCM theory. Strengthens the Lung's ability to regulate the skin surface.
  • BL-17 (Geshu, 膈俞): The influential point for Blood (血会). Used to regulate Blood circulation and resolve Stasis throughout the body.
  • ST-36 (Zusanli, 足三里): Strengthens the body's general Qi and supports the Spleen, which produces Blood. Useful when chronic psoriasis has depleted the body's resources.
  • Local points around plaques: In some TCM approaches, thin needles are inserted around the edges of stubborn plaques to move local Qi and Blood — sometimes called "surrounding the dragon" (围刺).

Dietary Guidance from a TCM Perspective

Diet is considered one of the most powerful complementary tools for psoriasis in TCM, because it directly influences both Blood Heat and Blood Stasis:

For Blood Heat (cooling foods):

  • Cucumber, celery, watermelon, pear, and bitter melon — cooling vegetables and fruits that help lower internal Heat
  • Mung bean soup (绿豆汤) — a classic cooling food traditionally used for Heat conditions. Drink unsweetened
  • Green tea (unsweetened) — cooling and helps clear Heat from the Blood
  • Lotus root, winter melon — cooling and moistening
  • Bone broth with cooling herbs — nourishing without adding Heat

For Blood Stasis (moving foods):

  • Turmeric and ginger — mildly warming, traditionally used to move Blood and reduce stagnation
  • Dark leafy greens (spinach, kale) — Blood-building and nourishing
  • Beets and red foods — traditionally believed to support Blood health
  • Garlic and onion — in small amounts, help move circulation
  • Fish (especially cold-water fish like salmon and sardines) — provide omega-3 fatty acids, which some studies suggest may support inflammatory balance

Foods traditionally recommended to reduce:

  • Spicy foods (chili, hot sauce, excess garlic/ginger) — increase Blood Heat and can trigger flare-ups
  • Alcohol — especially beer and spirits, which generate Heat and Dampness in TCM theory
  • Red meat and shellfish — considered "hot" foods that can aggravate Blood Heat
  • Fried and greasy foods — create Damp-Heat and burden the Spleen
  • Sugar and processed foods — contribute to systemic inflammation from a TCM perspective
A note on biologics and immunosuppressants: Many people with moderate to severe psoriasis use biologic medications (like TNF-alpha inhibitors or IL-17 blockers) or other systemic treatments. TCM approaches can be used as complementary support — but only under the supervision of both your dermatologist and a qualified TCM practitioner. Some herbs may interact with immunosuppressant medications. Never stop or change prescribed treatments without consulting your doctor.

What Research Says

Modern research has begun exploring the traditional TCM approach to psoriasis:

  • A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis of Chinese herbal medicine for psoriasis found that certain herbal formulas showed promising results in reducing Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI) scores compared to placebo. The authors noted that many studies had methodological limitations and called for larger, higher-quality randomized controlled trials.
  • Laboratory studies on compounds found in Blood-cooling herbs — such as paeoniflorin from Peony root (Chi Shao) and tanshinones from Salvia (Dan Shen) — have demonstrated anti-proliferative effects on keratinocytes (skin cells) in cell culture models, suggesting a possible mechanism for reducing the rapid cell turnover seen in psoriasis.
  • Clinical observations on acupuncture for psoriasis have reported mixed but generally positive results, with some studies showing reduced itch and plaque thickness. A 2020 review suggested acupuncture may work partly through modulation of neuro-immune pathways, but the authors emphasized the need for larger trials.
  • Traditional TCM dietary principles for psoriasis — reducing inflammatory foods and increasing cooling, nutrient-dense options — align with some modern dietary recommendations for autoimmune conditions, including the anti-inflammatory diet and Mediterranean diet patterns.

This research is from a TCM theory perspective and should not be interpreted as medical endorsement. These findings provide clues for future investigation, not conclusions about treatment. Always discuss complementary approaches with your healthcare provider.

Practical Takeaways

If you're living with psoriasis, here are some things to discuss with your healthcare provider or a qualified TCM practitioner:

  1. Identify your dominant pattern. Is your psoriasis in an active, flaring phase (Blood Heat — red, spreading, burning) or a chronic, stable phase (Blood Stasis — thick, dark, fixed)? The answer guides the approach. Many people cycle between both.
  2. Start with diet. Try reducing alcohol, spicy food, and red meat for 3–4 weeks and see if your skin responds. Add cooling foods like cucumber, mung bean soup, and green tea. Diet is often the most accessible lever.
  3. Manage stress. Emotional stress is one of the most common triggers for psoriasis flare-ups. In TCM theory, stress creates Liver Qi Stagnation, which generates Heat and enters the Blood. Even 10 minutes of deep breathing, gentle stretching, or mindfulness practice may help reduce flare frequency.
  4. Consider working with a TCM practitioner. Psoriasis is a complex, chronic condition. A qualified practitioner can identify the precise combination of Blood Heat and Blood Stasis in your case, customize an herbal formula, and choose appropriate acupuncture points. Self-diagnosis and self-prescribing have real limits.
  5. Don't stop prescribed treatments. If you're using topical steroids, vitamin D analogs, or systemic medications (including biologics), do not stop them without consulting your doctor. TCM approaches are best used as a complement to, not a replacement for, medical care.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Seek medical attention if you experience signs of psoriatic arthritis — joint pain, stiffness, especially in the fingers, toes, or lower back — as early treatment can prevent joint damage. Also see a doctor if your psoriasis covers large areas of your body, becomes suddenly severe, or shows signs of skin infection (increased redness, warmth, pus, fever). If you're considering complementary approaches, discuss them with your healthcare provider to ensure they're safe alongside your current treatments. TCM is a complementary approach and does not replace dermatological or rheumatological care.
