Psoriasis from a TCM Perspective: Blood Heat, Wind Dryness, and Skin Health

Cluster Article · 7 min read

If you live with psoriasis, you know the routine: red, raised patches of skin covered with silvery scales that flake, itch, and sometimes burn. You've tried steroid creams that work for a while, then stop. You've been told it's "an overactive immune system" and handed another prescription. Maybe you've wondered why your immune system decided to attack your skin — but conventional medicine doesn't have a satisfying answer.

The standard explanation — "it's a chronic autoimmune condition with a genetic component" — describes what happens (your skin cells multiply too fast) without explaining why it started or why it flares at certain times. Stress makes it worse. Certain foods make it worse. Cold, dry weather makes it worse. But the medical model doesn't connect these dots into a coherent picture.

Traditional Chinese Medicine offers a different framework — one that sees psoriasis not as a random immune malfunction, but as a visible expression of deeper imbalances within the body. And crucially, it explains why psoriasis behaves the way it does: why the plaques are red and inflamed, why they're dry and scaly, why they come and go, and why stress and diet play such a powerful role.

Think of it this way: If your skin is your body's radiator — releasing heat and regulating temperature — then psoriasis is like a radiator that's constantly running hot. The plaques aren't the problem itself; they're the body's way of venting excess heat through the only outlet it has left. In TCM theory, the real question isn't "how do we shut off the radiator?" — it's "why is the engine overheating in the first place?"

The Western View: What Psoriasis Means in Medicine

From a biomedical perspective, psoriasis is a chronic inflammatory skin condition affecting about 2–3% of the global population. It's characterized by hyperproliferation of keratinocytes — the cells that form the outer layer of skin — which mature in days instead of weeks, accumulating as silvery plaques on the skin's surface.

The immune system plays a central role: T-cells (a type of white blood cell) become overactivated and release inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α, IL-17, and IL-23, which drive the rapid skin cell turnover. This is why treatments include topical corticosteroids (to suppress inflammation), vitamin D analogs, and newer biologic drugs that target specific immune pathways.

Psoriasis is classified into several types — plaque psoriasis (the most common, affecting 80–90% of those with the condition), guttate psoriasis (small drop-like lesions, often triggered by strep infection), inverse psoriasis (smooth red patches in skin folds), pustular psoriasis, and erythrodermic psoriasis (a rare, severe form). It's also associated with psoriatic arthritis, which causes joint inflammation in up to 30% of people with skin involvement.

Conventional treatments can be genuinely effective, especially the newer biologic therapies. But they carry significant costs — both financial (biologics can cost tens of thousands of dollars annually) and medical (immunosuppression carries its own risks). And they don't address why the immune system became dysregulated in the first place.

A Different View: How TCM Understands Psoriasis

In TCM, the skin is understood as the outermost expression of the body's internal state. It's connected to the Lung system (which governs the skin and body hair) and reflects the health of the Blood, Qi, and Yin. When the skin erupts with red, inflamed plaques, TCM looks inward for the cause.

Psoriasis in TCM is most commonly associated with three interconnected patterns:

Blood Heat (血热) — the inflammatory engine behind active, red plaques.
Wind Dryness (风燥) — the dry, scaly, flaking quality of the skin.
Blood Deficiency (血虚) — the underlying dryness and poor nourishment of the skin that makes it vulnerable.

An analogy that helps: Imagine your body's internal environment as a landscape. Blood Heat is a summer drought that dries up the rivers and cracks the earth. Wind Dryness is the hot, parching wind that dries everything further. Blood Deficiency means there wasn't enough water in the system to begin with. Psoriasis plaques are the cracked, dried-up riverbeds — the visible evidence of the internal climate.

3 Key TCM Patterns in Psoriasis

Most people with psoriasis present with a mixed pattern — often Blood Heat in the active, inflammatory phases, shifting toward Wind Dryness and Blood Deficiency as the condition becomes chronic. Understanding which pattern dominates at which stage helps explain both the symptoms and what approach might be most supportive.

1. Blood Heat (血热) — The Inflammatory Engine

You might have this if: Your plaques are bright red, actively expanding, and feel warm or hot to the touch. The itching is intense, and new spots are appearing. You may feel irritable, have a dry mouth, crave cold drinks, and experience constipation or dark urine. Flares are often triggered by spicy foods, alcohol, or emotional stress.

TCM view: Blood Heat is the most common pattern in active, progressive psoriasis. In TCM theory, heat enters the Blood through dietary factors (spicy, greasy, or warming foods), emotional stress (which generates internal heat, especially through the Liver), or environmental influences. When heat accumulates in the Blood, it "bubbles up" to the surface, manifesting as red, inflamed skin. The body is essentially trying to release the excess heat through the skin — the largest heat-exchange organ.

Traditional approach: Herbs that "cool the Blood and eliminate toxicity" (凉血解毒) — formulas traditionally used include modified Xi Jiao Di Huang Tang (Rhinoceros Horn and Rehmannia Decoction) or Liang Xue Si Wu Tang (Cool the Blood Four Substances Decoction). Cooling foods like bitter melon, cucumber, watermelon, mung beans, and green tea are traditionally emphasized. Spicy, fried, and greasy foods are minimized as they are believed to generate heat.

2. Wind Dryness (风燥) — The Dry, Scaly Surface

You might have this if: Your plaques are dry, thick, and covered with silvery scales that flake heavily. The skin feels tight and rough. Itching may be moderate but persistent, and it often worsens in dry, cold weather or heated indoor environments. The skin may crack and bleed, especially over joints. You may also experience dry eyes, dry scalp, or brittle nails.

TCM view: In TCM theory, "Wind" (风) is a pathogenic factor associated with movement, dryness, and change. Psoriasis with significant scaling and flaking is seen as Wind Dryness — the Wind carries Heat to the skin surface, where it dries out the fluids that normally keep skin supple. The silvery scales are literally dried-out skin cells that the body couldn't properly nourish. This pattern often develops after a prolonged period of Blood Heat — the fire has burned through the body's fluid reserves, leaving everything parched.

Traditional approach: Herbs that "nourish Blood, moisten Dryness, and dispel Wind" (养血润燥祛风) — such as Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis), Sheng Di (Rehmannia), and Bai Xian Pi (Dictamnus root bark) in formulas like Dang Gui Yin Zi (Angelica Decoction). Emollient topical applications are traditionally used, and humidifying the environment is recommended. Foods that nourish Yin and Blood — like bone broth, eggs, black sesame seeds, almonds, and cooked leafy greens — are emphasized.

3. Blood Deficiency (血虚) — The Underlying Vulnerability

You might have this if: The psoriasis has been present for years, with less active redness but persistent dryness, scaling, and thickened skin. The plaques may be pale pink rather than bright red. You feel generally fatigued, have pale skin, brittle nails, and may experience dizziness, poor memory, or difficulty concentrating. Your periods may be light or irregular. The skin heals slowly when it cracks.

TCM view: Blood Deficiency is often the underlying constitutional factor in chronic psoriasis. The Blood (血) in TCM theory is more than just the red fluid — it's the substance that nourishes, moistens, and anchors the body's tissues. When Blood is insufficient, the skin loses its nourishment and becomes dry, thin, and vulnerable. Without enough Blood to "anchor" the Heat in the deeper layers, it rises to the surface — creating a cycle where Deficiency and Heat feed each other. This pattern is why psoriasis can persist for years even when the acute inflammation subsides.

Traditional approach: Building Blood through nourishing herbs and foods — Shu Di Huang (cooked Rehmannia), Bai Shao (White Peony), E Jiao (Donkey-hide gelatin). Iron-rich foods, dark leafy greens, red meat (in moderation), beets, and slow-cooked soups are traditionally encouraged. Adequate rest and sleep — when the body builds Blood — is considered essential. Overwork and excessive physical activity are minimized as they deplete Blood.

Why Pattern Differentiation Matters in Psoriasis

Psoriasis demonstrates why TCM's pattern-based approach offers something genuinely different. Two people with the same Western diagnosis of plaque psoriasis may need completely different support:

  • Person A presents with bright red, rapidly spreading plaques during a period of high work stress. They've been eating more takeout and drinking alcohol to unwind. Their TCM pattern is primarily Blood Heat — the acute phase needs cooling and detoxifying approaches.
  • Person B has had stable, mildly pink plaques for 10 years, with dry, cracked skin over the elbows and knees. They feel tired and cold easily. Their TCM pattern is primarily Blood Deficiency with Wind Dryness — they need nourishing and moistening approaches, not cooling.

The same pharmaceutical cream might help both people temporarily, but the underlying imbalances are completely different. TCM's framework explains why — and points toward different long-term strategies for each person.

What Triggers Flares in TCM Terms?

One of the strengths of the TCM framework is that it explains why specific triggers cause flares — and helps people make choices that reduce flare frequency and severity.

Dietary Triggers

Spicy and warming foods (chili, garlic, ginger, lamb, alcohol, coffee) — In TCM theory, these add heat to the system. For someone with Blood Heat, they're like pouring fuel on a fire.

Greasy and fried foods — These create Damp-Heat, a thicker, stickier form of heat that's harder to clear and tends to settle in the skin.

Shellfish and seafood — Traditionally classified as "windy" or "allergenic" foods (发物) that may aggravate skin conditions by stirring up internal Wind.

Cold and raw foods — For those with underlying Blood or Yin Deficiency, these may further weaken the digestive system's ability to produce Blood and fluids.

Emotional and Environmental Triggers

Stress and frustration — In TCM, the Liver governs the smooth flow of Qi. Chronic stress causes Liver Qi stagnation, which generates internal Heat. This is why psoriasis so often flares during or after stressful periods.

Cold, dry weather — External Dryness combines with internal dryness, worsening scaling and cracking. This is why many people with psoriasis find their skin improves in summer and worsens in winter.

Infections — Especially strep throat, which is a known trigger for guttate psoriasis. In TCM terms, external pathogenic factors invade the body and generate Heat in the Blood.

Dietary Considerations from a TCM Perspective

Food choices that align with your TCM pattern may help reduce flare intensity. These are traditional dietary principles, not medical prescriptions:

Cooling Foods (for Blood Heat patterns)

  • Include: Cucumber, watermelon, bitter melon, celery, bok choy, mung beans, tofu, green tea, peppermint tea, lotus root, bamboo shoots
  • Minimize: Spicy foods (chili, garlic, ginger in large amounts), lamb, beef, alcohol, coffee, fried foods, processed foods
  • Traditional idea: Cooling foods are believed to help clear Heat from the Blood by providing a gentle "anti-inflammatory" effect from a TCM perspective

Nourishing Foods (for Blood Deficiency patterns)

  • Include: Bone broth, slow-cooked soups, dark leafy greens (kale, spinach), beets, goji berries, black sesame seeds, eggs, cooked oats, red dates, liver (in moderation)
  • Minimize: Excessive raw foods, cold drinks, excessive caffeine — these may impair the Spleen's ability to transform food into Blood and Qi
  • Traditional idea: Warm, cooked foods are easier for the digestive system to convert into Blood and fluids that nourish the skin

Moistening Foods (for Wind Dryness patterns)

  • Include: Almonds, walnuts, sesame seeds, avocado, pear, apple, cooked greens, mushrooms, seaweed, okra, aloe vera juice, honey
  • Minimize: Diuretics (excessive caffeine, alcohol), overly dry foods (crackers, chips, dry toast), dehydrating environments (dry heating, air conditioning)
  • Traditional idea: Moistening foods are believed to support the body's Yin fluids, helping the skin retain moisture from the inside out
Important note: These dietary suggestions are based on TCM theory and traditional use. They are not medical prescriptions. If you have psoriasis, work with a qualified healthcare provider — ideally one familiar with both TCM and dermatology — to develop a plan that's right for your specific presentation. Do not make significant dietary changes without considering your overall health needs, including any medications you're taking.

What Research Suggests

Modern research has begun to explore the TCM understanding of psoriasis:

  • A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found that certain TCM herbal formulas, when used alongside conventional treatments, were associated with greater improvement in Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI) scores compared to conventional treatment alone. The authors noted that larger, higher-quality trials are needed to confirm these findings.
  • Laboratory studies on individual herbs traditionally used for psoriasis — including Indigo naturalis (青黛), Smilax glabra (土茯苓), and Rehmannia glutinosa (地黄) — have demonstrated anti-inflammatory and anti-proliferative effects in cell and animal models, though human studies remain limited.
  • Research on the Blood Heat pattern in psoriasis has found that people presenting with this TCM pattern tend to have higher levels of inflammatory markers (TNF-α, IL-17, IL-23) compared to those with other patterns — suggesting that TCM pattern differentiation may have measurable biological correlates.
  • Acupuncture and acupressure studies for psoriasis-related itching and stress have shown mixed but promising results, with some studies suggesting symptom improvement that may be related to stress reduction rather than direct effects on the skin.

This research is promising but preliminary. TCM is a complementary approach and should be explored alongside, not instead of, conventional medical care.

Practical Takeaways

  1. Know your pattern. If you're exploring TCM for psoriasis, work with a qualified practitioner who can identify whether Blood Heat, Wind Dryness, Blood Deficiency — or a combination — is dominant in your case. This will guide everything else.
  2. Start with diet. The most accessible step is identifying which foods trigger your flares from a TCM perspective. Even small changes — like swapping coffee for green tea during a flare, or adding bone broth to your winter routine — can shift your internal environment.
  3. Manage stress actively. The stress-psoriasis connection is one of the most consistent findings in both TCM and conventional research. Whatever helps you regulate your nervous system — meditation, gentle movement, acupuncture, therapy — is worth treating as medicine, not optional.
  4. Moisturize from the inside out. While topical moisturizers help, TCM emphasizes that the skin's moisture comes primarily from internal nourishment. Warming, nourishing foods and adequate hydration support the body's natural moisture production.
  5. Keep working with your dermatologist. Psoriasis, especially moderate to severe cases, requires appropriate medical monitoring. TCM is a complementary approach — it doesn't replace conventional treatment, especially biologic therapies that have been carefully prescribed for your condition.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ Psoriasis requires proper medical diagnosis and monitoring. See a dermatologist or healthcare provider if you: have skin patches you haven't had diagnosed, experience joint pain or swelling (possible psoriatic arthritis), develop widespread or pustular psoriasis (which can be a medical emergency), or if your current treatment stops working. Do not discontinue prescribed medications without consulting your doctor. Psoriatic arthritis, in particular, can cause irreversible joint damage if not treated early — so any new joint symptoms should be evaluated promptly.
