Rosacea from a TCM Perspective: Stomach Heat and Facial Redness

Skin Conditions · 7 min read

You catch a glimpse of yourself in a car window and there it is again — that familiar flush creeping across your cheeks and nose. Maybe it started after a glass of wine at dinner. Maybe after a hot coffee on a stressful morning. Or maybe it just showed up for no reason at all, like it often does.

If you have rosacea, you know this scene well. The persistent redness. The visible blood vessels. The bumps that look like acne but behave differently. The way your face seems to have a mind of its own — blushing when you're not embarrassed, flushing when there's no reason to.

Rosacea affects an estimated 16 million Americans, yet its exact cause remains unclear in Western medicine. Conventional treatments — topical metronidazole, oral antibiotics, laser therapy — can help manage symptoms, but many people find the redness keeps returning, especially when triggered by common things like sun, spicy food, alcohol, or stress.

Traditional Chinese Medicine offers a different lens — one that traces rosacea's root to a specific internal imbalance and provides a framework for understanding why certain faces flush while others don't.

The Western View: What We Know About Rosacea

In conventional dermatology, rosacea is understood as a chronic inflammatory skin condition involving:

  • Vascular dysfunction: Abnormal dilation of facial blood vessels leading to persistent redness and flushing
  • Immune system involvement: Overactive immune responses, including elevated cathelicidin (an antimicrobial peptide) and abnormal Toll-like receptor signaling
  • Demodex mites: Higher-than-normal levels of skin mites may contribute to inflammation in some individuals
  • Triggers: Sun exposure, temperature extremes, spicy foods, alcohol, caffeine, stress, and certain skincare products

Treatment typically aims to control symptoms: topical medications to reduce inflammation, oral antibiotics to manage pustules, and laser or light therapy to address visible blood vessels. These can be effective, but they don't address why the vascular system behaves this way in the first place.

A Different Lens: The Face as a Mirror of the Stomach

This is where TCM offers a fresh perspective. In Traditional Chinese Medicine, rosacea is most often understood as a manifestation of Stomach Heat (胃热, Wèi Rè) — an imbalance in the Stomach system that sends excess Heat upward to the face.

Key insight: The Stomach meridian (足阳明胃经) is one of the major energy pathways in the body, and it runs directly through the face — across the cheeks, the sides of the nose, and around the mouth. These are precisely the areas where rosacea redness and flushing typically appear.

Think of it this way: In TCM theory, the Stomach doesn't just digest food — it also governs the "descending" of Qi (energy). When the Stomach is functioning smoothly, Qi and fluids move downward, keeping Heat away from the face. But when the Stomach system becomes imbalanced — from diet, stress, or constitutional factors — Heat rises instead of descending. This ascending Heat travels up the Stomach meridian and "flares" through the facial skin, creating persistent redness and flushing.

This explains a pattern many people with rosacea recognize: spicy foods and hot beverages trigger flushing. In TCM terms, these foods add "Heat" to an already-heated Stomach system, causing a visible upward flare.

Why Stomach Heat? The Deeper Connections

Several factors in modern life are traditionally understood to contribute to Stomach Heat:

  • Diet: Spicy foods, alcohol, coffee, fried foods, and excessive meat consumption are all considered "Heat-producing" in TCM food energetics. In a balanced digestive system, these foods can be processed without issue. But in a constitution that's already warm, or when these foods are consumed heavily, they gradually "heat up" the Stomach system.
  • Stress and emotional tension: In TCM, stress and frustration affect the Liver system. When the Liver is constrained (Liver Qi Stagnation), it can generate Heat that "invades" the Stomach, creating a pattern called Liver-Heat invading the Stomach (肝胃郁热). This is common in people whose rosacea flares during stressful periods.
  • Constitutional predisposition: Some people have a naturally "hotter" constitution — they tend to feel warm, prefer cold drinks, and have a redder complexion. This isn't inherently unhealthy, but it means their Stomach system may be more prone to accumulating Heat.
  • Irregular eating habits: Skipping meals, eating late at night, or eating on the run can disrupt the Stomach's natural rhythm, leading to Stomach dysfunction and the accumulation of Heat.

Beyond Stomach Heat: Other Patterns That Can Look Like Rosacea

While Stomach Heat is the most common pattern associated with rosacea in TCM, other patterns can present with similar facial redness. A TCM practitioner would differentiate these based on the specific characteristics of the redness and accompanying symptoms:

Stomach Heat (胃热) — The Classic Rosacea Pattern

Key features: Persistent redness on the cheeks, nose, and central face. Flushing triggered by spicy foods, alcohol, or hot drinks. May include visible blood vessels (telangiectasia). You might also experience thirst, a preference for cold drinks, and occasional indigestion or acid reflux.

Liver-Heat (肝火) — Stress-Triggered Flushing

Key features: Redness that flares noticeably during or after stressful events. The face may feel hot and look redder, especially on the sides of the face and temples (the Liver meridian area). May be accompanied by irritability, headaches, and a bitter taste in the mouth.

Damp-Heat (湿热) — Rosacea with Pustules

Key features: Redness accompanied by papules, pustules, and oily skin. The face looks greasy and may feel heavy or congested. Digestive symptoms — bloating, loose stools, a feeling of heaviness — are common. This pattern is more common in people whose rosacea includes the acne-like bumps (rosacea subtype 2).

Yin Deficiency with Empty-Heat (阴虚内热) — Dry Facial Flushing

Key features: Redness that looks drier, less oily. The face may feel hot but the skin also feels dry or thin. Flushing tends to be worse in the afternoon or evening. Other symptoms include dry eyes, night sweats, and a sensation of heat in the palms and soles.

What TCM Traditional Approaches May Offer

It's important to be clear: TCM is not a cure for rosacea, and these are traditional perspectives, not medical claims. However, the TCM framework offers several avenues that some people find helpful when used alongside conventional care:

1. Cooling Dietary Principles

In TCM, diet is seen as foundational for managing Stomach Heat. The principle is simple: reduce foods that add Heat, and increase foods that cool.

Foods traditionally considered cooling (beneficial for Stomach Heat patterns):

  • Cucumber, celery, lettuce, bitter melon
  • Watermelon, pear, apple, banana
  • Mung beans, tofu, soybean sprouts
  • Green tea, chrysanthemum tea, peppermint tea
  • Rice, barley, millet

Foods traditionally considered Heating (to moderate for rosacea-prone individuals):

  • Spicy foods — chili peppers, hot sauce, curry
  • Alcohol, especially red wine
  • Coffee and strong black tea
  • Fried and greasy foods
  • Red meat, lamb, and game meat
  • Garlic, ginger, and cinnamon (in large quantities)

This doesn't mean you must eliminate these foods entirely — but in TCM thinking, reducing them when rosacea is active may help reduce the "fuel" that feeds facial Heat.

2. Acupressure Points for Facial Cooling

Several acupressure points are traditionally chosen for patterns involving Heat rising to the face. Applying gentle pressure to these points may help redirect the flow of Qi and encourage a cooling effect:

  • ST44 (Neiting) — The Stomach's Cooling Point: Located on the top of the foot, between the second and third toes, about half an inch back from the webbing. In TCM theory, this is the "Ying-Spring" point of the Stomach meridian — a point traditionally used to clear Heat from the Stomach. Gentle thumb pressure for 1-2 minutes on each foot may be beneficial.
  • LI4 (Hegu) — The Face's Master Point: The webbing between the thumb and index finger. Widely used in acupuncture for facial conditions, LI4 is traditionally believed to help regulate the face and move Qi. Deep, circular massage for 1-2 minutes.
  • ST36 (Zusanli) — The Stomach's Toning Point: Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width to the outside of the shinbone. While this point primarily strengthens the Stomach digestive function, a well-functioning Stomach is less likely to accumulate Heat. Firm pressure for 2-3 minutes on each leg.

3. Skincare from a TCM Perspective

TCM skincare principles for rosacea emphasize gentleness and cooling over aggressive treatments:

  • Avoid heat-aggravating ingredients: Harsh scrubs, strong chemical peels, and hot steam treatments may worsen redness by adding more "Heat" to the skin.
  • Cooling topical approaches: Some TCM herbs traditionally used in cooling formulas — like cucumber extract, aloe vera, and pearl powder — have been applied topically for centuries as soothing, cooling agents.
  • Protect from wind and sun: In TCM, wind and heat are considered external pathogens that can worsen facial redness. Sun protection and protection from harsh winds are important, which aligns well with conventional dermatological advice.
Note: TCM topical herbs should be chosen carefully for rosacea-prone skin. Some herbs that are medicinally powerful may be too strong for sensitive, reddened facial skin. Always test any new topical product on a small area first, and consult a qualified TCM practitioner before using internal herbal formulas.

What About TCM Herbal Formulas?

In clinical TCM practice, herbal formulas for rosacea are typically customized to the individual pattern. While we won't prescribe specific formulas here (that requires a qualified practitioner's evaluation), it may be helpful to know the types of herbs traditionally used in Stomach Heat patterns:

  • Herbs that clear Stomach Heat: Gypsum (石膏, Shí Gāo), Anemarrhena (知母, Zhī Mǔ), Gardenia (栀子, Zhī Zǐ)
  • Herbs that cool the Blood: Rehmannia (生地黄, Shēng Dì Huáng), Red Peony (赤芍, Chì Sháo), Moutan Bark (牡丹皮, Mǔ Dān Pí)
  • Herbs that guide Heat downward: Rhubarb (大黄, Dà Huáng) — in small amounts, used in combination formulas to encourage descending movement

A well-known TCM formula framework for Stomach Heat is Qing Wei San (清胃散, "Clear the Stomach Powder"), traditionally formulated for patterns involving Heat ascending from the Stomach to the face. Modern practitioners may modify this formula based on the individual's specific presentation.

Important safety note: Never self-prescribe TCM herbs. Some herbs can interact with prescription medications, and incorrect dosage or combination can be harmful. A licensed TCM practitioner can evaluate your specific pattern and create a safe, individualized formula. Pregnant women, people on blood-thinning medication, and those with chronic health conditions should be especially cautious.

What Research Says About TCM and Rosacea

Research on TCM for rosacea specifically is still emerging, but some studies have explored related approaches:

  • A 2024 systematic review of Chinese herbal medicine for rosacea published in Frontiers in Medicine found that certain herbal formulas, used alone or alongside conventional treatments, showed potential in reducing erythema (redness) and papulopustular lesions, though the authors called for more rigorous randomized controlled trials.
  • Research on individual herbs traditionally used for rosacea — such as extracts from Gardenia (栀子), Phellodendron (黄柏), and Licorice (甘草) — has demonstrated anti-inflammatory and vasoactive properties in laboratory and animal models.
  • A small clinical study on acupuncture for facial redness found that participants who received pattern-specific acupuncture reported reduced flushing frequency and intensity compared to a sham acupuncture group.
  • The overlap between TCM dietary recommendations for Stomach Heat and conventional rosacea trigger-avoidance diets (low-spice, low-alcohol, low-caffeine) is striking, suggesting that both traditions converge on similar practical advice despite different theoretical foundations.

This research is preliminary but encouraging. TCM is a complementary approach intended to work alongside, not replace, conventional dermatological care.

Practical Takeaways

If rosacea is affecting your quality of life, here are some things you can discuss with your healthcare provider:

  1. Notice the triggers through a TCM lens. What makes your face flush? Spicy food (Stomach Heat)? Stress (Liver Heat)? Sun exposure (External Heat)? Keep a simple journal for 2 weeks — the pattern may be clearer than you think.
  2. Try the cooling diet for 3 weeks. Reduce spicy foods, alcohol, and hot beverages. Increase cooling foods like cucumber, watermelon, and leafy greens. Many people see a noticeable difference in baseline redness within a few weeks.
  3. Explore acupressure. The points listed above (ST44, LI4, ST36) are simple to try at home. Gentle, consistent pressure once or twice a day may support the body's natural cooling mechanisms.
  4. Work with a qualified TCM practitioner who has experience with skin conditions. A practitioner can determine your specific pattern — is it pure Stomach Heat, or is there Liver involvement, Yin Deficiency, or Damp-Heat — and create a tailored approach.
  5. Be patient with supplementation. If you're taking TCM herbs, understand that dietary and herbal approaches typically take 4-12 weeks to show meaningful changes. TCM treats root patterns, not just surface symptoms.

When to See a Doctor

⚠️ While rosacea itself is not dangerous, some conditions can look like rosacea but require different treatment. See a dermatologist if: you experience eye irritation, burning, or dryness (possible ocular rosacea); the redness is on one side of the face only (rashes should be roughly symmetrical); you have a thick, bumpy texture on the nose (rhinophyma, which requires medical evaluation); or your symptoms are causing significant distress or affecting your vision. TCM is a complementary approach and does not replace dermatological care or emergency medical treatment.

Summary: The TCM View at a Glance

From a TCM perspective, rosacea is not simply a skin condition — it's a visible signal that the internal environment needs rebalancing. The persistent facial redness tells a story: Stomach Heat is rising, and the face — where the Stomach meridian makes its most visible journey — is showing what's happening inside.

By cooling the Stomach, managing dietary triggers, addressing emotional factors that feed Heat, and supporting the body's natural descending function, the TCM framework offers a way to address rosacea not as a surface problem to be suppressed, but as an internal imbalance to be gently and persistently corrected.

This is not a quick fix, and it's not a replacement for medical care. But for those who've been told "there's nothing more to do" or "it's just the way your skin is," the TCM perspective offers a new question to explore:

Not just "what should I put on my face?" — but "what is my face telling me about my internal balance?"
