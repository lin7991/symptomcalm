The Six Excesses: Understanding Internal Stagnation Patterns in TCM

TCM Basics · 9 min read

You feel stuck. Not just emotionally — physically stuck. There's a pressure in your chest, a knot in your stomach, a heaviness in your body that won't lift. You've tried to relax, to eat better, to move more, but the sensation of being blocked persists.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, this feeling of being "stuck" has a name. Actually, it has six names — the Six Excesses (Liù Yù, 六郁), sometimes called the Six Stagnations or Six Depressions. These are internal patterns of blockage that can affect different systems of the body, and understanding them can shed light on why you might feel stuck in ways that don't quite fit a conventional diagnosis.

Think of the Six Excesses as six different ways your internal plumbing can get clogged. The same overall concept — "stuck" — but depending on what is stuck, the symptoms, causes, and solutions look very different. Qi stagnation is like a kinked hose. Blood stagnation is like a frozen pipe. Food stagnation is like a clogged kitchen sink. Each requires a different approach.

External Pathogens vs. Internal Excesses: What's the Difference?

Before diving into the Six Excesses, it's worth clarifying how they differ from a related TCM concept: the Six Pathogens (also called the Six Pernicious Influences).

🔬 Six Pathogens (External) vs. Six Excesses (Internal)

  • Six Pathogens (Wind, Cold, Summer Heat, Dampness, Dryness, Fire) are external climatic factors that invade the body from the outside — like catching a cold from exposure to wind and cold weather
  • Six Excesses (Qi, Blood, Dampness, Phlegm, Food, Fire Stagnation) are internal patterns that develop when the body's own systems become blocked or imbalanced — like developing chronic tension from unrelenting stress

In practice, the two can interact. Prolonged external Dampness (living in a humid climate) can eventually create internal Dampness Excess (chronic sluggish digestion). But they start from different places and require different strategies. For a deep dive into external pathogens, see our guide on the Six Pathogens. This article focuses on the internal patterns.

The Concept of "Excess" in TCM

In TCM theory, health exists on a spectrum between deficiency (xū, 虚) and excess (shí, 实). Deficiency means something is lacking — not enough Qi, not enough blood, not enough warmth. Excess means something is overabundant or stuck — too much Qi in one place, too much fluid that can't move, too much heat that can't escape.

The Six Excesses describe different types of "too much" — different substances or forces that have accumulated in the body and aren't flowing properly. Each one has a characteristic set of signs, and people often experience a combination of two or more.

The Six Excesses, Explained

1. Qi Stagnation (Qì Yù, 气郁)

Qi stagnation is the most common Excess in modern life. It occurs when your body's vital energy — Qi — gets stuck and can't flow smoothly. The root cause in TCM theory is almost always emotional: chronic stress, suppressed anger, unresolved frustration, or grief that hasn't been processed.

Common signs:

  • A sensation of pressure or tightness in the chest and throat (sometimes called "plum pit" sensation — feeling like something is stuck in the throat)
  • Irritability, mood swings, feeling easily frustrated
  • Sighing frequently (the body's natural attempt to release trapped Qi)
  • Bloating and digestive discomfort that worsens with stress
  • Premenstrual breast tenderness and mood changes
  • Pain that comes and goes, shifts location, or feels "moving"

Qi stagnation often underlies many of the other Excesses — when Qi can't move, it can't push blood, transform fluids, or move food through the digestive tract. This is why TCM places so much emphasis on emotional wellbeing as a foundation for physical health.

For a deeper look at this specific pattern, see our dedicated guide on Qi Stagnation.

2. Blood Stagnation (Xuè Yù, 血郁)

If Qi is the force that moves things, Blood is the substance that nourishes. When Qi stagnation persists, it can eventually lead to Blood stagnation — a more stubborn, physically grounded form of blockage.

Common signs:

  • Fixed, sharp, stabbing pain in a specific location (not wandering like Qi stagnation pain)
  • Dark complexion or dark circles under the eyes
  • Purple or dark-colored lips and tongue (especially with dark spots on the tongue body)
  • Varicose veins or spider veins
  • Painful menstrual periods with dark blood clots
  • Old injuries that ache in cold weather
  • Lumps or masses under the skin (in more advanced cases)

Blood stagnation is considered more serious than Qi stagnation because it's harder to shift. In TCM theory, chronic Blood stagnation can contribute to the formation of deeper tissue changes. This is why modern research has explored connections between TCM Blood stagnation and coagulation disorders, microcirculatory dysfunction, and fibrotic processes.

For a comprehensive overview, see our guide on Blood Stasis in TCM.

3. Dampness Stagnation (Shī Yù, 湿郁)

Dampness stagnation occurs when fluids in the body become thick and heavy instead of thin and flowing. In TCM theory, this usually starts with a Spleen system that's too weak to properly transform and transport fluids.

Common signs:

  • Heavy, foggy head — like you're thinking through cotton wool
  • A feeling of heaviness in the body, especially the limbs
  • Sluggish digestion, bloating after meals
  • Loose or sticky stools
  • A thick, greasy coating on the tongue
  • Joint pain that feels heavy and fixed
  • Weight gain around the abdomen that's resistant to diet and exercise

Dampness stagnation responds well to dietary changes — reducing dairy, sugar, and fried foods while increasing warm, cooked meals and certain grains like barley and adzuki beans. For a complete guide, see our dedicated article on Dampness in TCM.

4. Phlegm Stagnation (Tán Yù, 痰郁)

Dampness Left unchecked, Dampness can condense into Phlegm — a thicker, stickier, more stubborn form of stagnation. In TCM theory, Phlegm isn't just what you cough up from a cold. It includes "invisible phlegm" that can accumulate anywhere in the body.

Common signs:

  • Dizziness or vertigo
  • A sensation of oppression in the chest
  • Chronic cough with thick sputum
  • Nausea or a feeling of fullness that won't go away
  • Subcutaneous lumps or nodules (lipomas, cysts)
  • Brain fog that feels particularly thick and stubborn
  • A very thick, greasy tongue coating that's hard to scrape away

Phlegm stagnation is considered one of the more complex Excess patterns in TCM, precisely because it's harder to resolve. It can take longer to clear and often requires both dietary change and herbal support.

5. Food Stagnation (Shí Yù, 食郁)

Food stagnation is exactly what it sounds like — food that hasn't been properly digested and is sitting in the digestive tract, fermenting and causing problems. In TCM theory, this happens when the Spleen and Stomach are overwhelmed by the quantity or quality of what you've eaten.

Common signs:

  • Feeling uncomfortably full for hours after eating
  • Sour belching or acid reflux
  • Nausea or a sensation of food "stuck" in the stomach
  • Foul-smelling gas or stool
  • A thick, greasy coating on the tongue (often yellow if Heat is also present)
  • Disturbed sleep due to digestive discomfort

Food stagnation is often temporary and responds well to simple measures: eating smaller meals, chewing thoroughly, adding digestive-supporting spices like ginger and fennel, and occasionally skipping a meal to give the system a break (intermittent fasting aligns well with this TCM concept).

6. Fire Stagnation (Huǒ Yù, 火郁)

Fire stagnation occurs when Heat builds up inside the body and can't escape. Think of it as internal pressure that keeps rising — the lid is on tight, and the steam has nowhere to go. This can happen when Qi stagnation generates friction, when Dampness ferments into Heat, or when emotional tension creates inner fire.

Common signs:

  • Irritability, agitation, feeling "hot under the collar"
  • Facial flushing or red eyes
  • Bitter taste in the mouth, bad breath
  • Constipation with dry, hard stools
  • Dark yellow urine
  • Acne or skin rashes that are red and inflamed
  • Insomnia — mind racing at night, unable to settle down
  • A red tongue with a yellow coating

Fire stagnation often coexists with Qi stagnation (the stress creates heat) or Dampness stagnation (creating Damp-Heat). Stress management, cooling foods (like cucumber, watermelon, and leafy greens), and adequate hydration are core strategies.

How the Six Excesses Interact

In real life, people rarely experience just one Excess in isolation. The patterns commonly combine and feed into each other:

🔄 Common Excess Combinations

  • Qi Stagnation → Blood Stagnation: When Qi can't move for a long time, Blood eventually stops too. This is why chronic stress (Qi stagnation) is linked to painful periods (Blood stagnation) in TCM gynecology
  • Qi Stagnation → Fire Stagnation: Trapped emotional energy generates heat. Think of someone who's chronically irritated and also suffers from acid reflux and facial flushing
  • Dampness Stagnation → Phlegm Stagnation: Thin fluids become thick over time. This progression often takes months or years
  • Food Stagnation + Dampness → Heat: Undigested food in the gut can generate heat and inflammation, creating a Damp-Heat pattern
  • Fire Stagnation → Insomnia: Unreleased heat in the upper body disturbs the Mind (Shen), making it hard to fall asleep

What Modern Research Suggests

The TCM concept of internal stagnation is being examined through modern research lenses, with some interesting areas of convergence:

  • Qi stagnation and the autonomic nervous system: Some researchers have drawn parallels between Qi stagnation patterns and sympathetic nervous system dominance — the fight-or-flight response being chronically activated, leading to tension, poor circulation, and digestive inhibition
  • Blood stagnation and microcirculation: Studies have explored whether TCM Blood stagnation patterns correspond to measurable changes in microcirculatory blood flow, platelet aggregation, and vascular reactivity
  • Dampness-Phlegm excess and metabolic markers: Research published in the Journal of Integrative Medicine has suggested that the TCM Dampness-Phlegm pattern may correlate with elevations in triglycerides, total cholesterol, and fasting blood glucose — linking the concept to what Western medicine would call metabolic syndrome

These are emerging areas of investigation, not established conclusions. The value of the Six Excesses framework is not that it maps perfectly onto lab values, but that it offers a useful way to understand and categorize different types of "stuckness" in the body — each with a different root cause and different practical strategies.

Practical Takeaways: How to Work With the Six Excesses Framework

💡 Using the Six Excesses as a Self-Reflection Tool

If you suspect you might be experiencing an internal stagnation pattern, here's a practical approach:

  1. Identify the feeling: Is it pressure and tension (Qi stagnation)? Sharp, fixed pain (Blood stagnation)? Heavy and foggy (Dampness)? Thick and oppressive (Phlegm)? Full and sour (Food)? Hot and agitated (Fire)?
  2. Look for root causes: Has there been prolonged emotional stress (Qi/Fire)? A diet high in damp-forming foods (Dampness/Phlegm)? Overeating or poor chewing habits (Food)?
  3. Try the corresponding lifestyle approach: Gentle movement and emotional expression for Qi stagnation (walking, stretching, journaling). Warming circulation for Blood stagnation (massage, gentle heat, movement). Dietary adjustment for Dampness and Food stagnation (see the dampness guide for specifics). Cooling and calming practices for Fire stagnation
  4. Work with a practitioner: A qualified TCM practitioner can determine which Excesses are present in your unique combination through tongue and pulse diagnosis, and can offer individualized guidance including acupuncture and herbal support

When to See a Doctor

The patterns described in this article — chronic pain, persistent digestive issues, chest tightness, insomnia, weight changes, skin problems — can also be caused by medical conditions that require proper diagnosis and treatment. Please consult a healthcare provider if you experience:

  • Severe or worsening pain at any location
  • Chest pain, tightness, or shortness of breath
  • Unexplained weight loss or gain
  • Blood in your stool, urine, or vomit
  • Persistent fever
  • Any new lump or mass under the skin
  • Symptoms that significantly affect your daily functioning or quality of life

TCM offers a complementary framework for understanding patterns of internal stagnation, but it's not a replacement for medical evaluation. The goal is to give you a useful lens for noticing what's happening in your body, not to replace professional judgment.
