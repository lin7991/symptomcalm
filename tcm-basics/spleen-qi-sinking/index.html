Spleen Qi Sinking: When Your Energy Feels Like It's Dragging

TCM Basics · 7 min read

You know that feeling — like everything in your body is slowly sliding downward. A heavy, bearing-down sensation in your abdomen. Your energy feels like it's draining out through your feet. You feel tired, but not in a "I need sleep" way — more like your whole body has become a sack of potatoes that gravity is winning against.

Most people blame posture, age, or just having a "lazy day." But in TCM theory, this constellation of symptoms points to a specific and well-described pattern: Spleen Qi Sinking (also called Spleen Qi Fall).

Think of Spleen Qi Sinking like a tent losing its center pole. When the pole is strong, the tent stands tall, walls taut, everything in its place. When the pole weakens, the fabric starts sagging — the center droops, the sides go slack, and the whole structure presses downward. The Spleen is your body's center pole. When its Qi is strong, everything stays lifted. When Qi sinks, everything follows.

This article explores what Spleen Qi Sinking is from a TCM perspective, how to recognize it, and what traditional approaches may be considered. For a broader overview of the Spleen's role in TCM, see our guide on The Spleen in TCM: Your Body's Digestion and Energy Center.

What Is Spleen Qi Sinking?

In TCM theory, the Spleen has a specific function called the raising (or lifting) action. This is the Spleen's job of keeping things in their proper place — holding the internal organs in position, keeping the tissues toned, and maintaining upward energy flow in the body.

Spleen Qi Sinking (Pi Qi Xia Xian, 脾气下陷) is what happens when this raising function weakens. The Qi that should be lifting and supporting instead starts to sink downward, pulling everything with it.

Think of it as the Spleen's anti-gravity mechanism failing. Instead of being held up and supported, the body's structures experience a downward drag. From a TCM perspective, this isn't just tiredness — it's a specific pattern with recognizable physical signs.

Spleen Qi Sinking is considered a progression of regular Spleen Qi deficiency. A person may start with basic Spleen Qi deficiency symptoms (fatigue, bloating, loose stools) and as the condition deepens, the sinking aspect becomes more pronounced.

How Does Spleen Qi Become "Sinking"?

Spleen Qi Sinking doesn't happen overnight. In TCM theory, it develops gradually as the Spleen is repeatedly taxed. The same habits that weaken Spleen Qi in general — when sustained over time — can eventually lead to the sinking pattern:

🍽️ Chronic Overwork and Irregular Eating

The Spleen thrives on regularity. Skipping meals, relying on processed convenience foods, and eating under constant stress are thought in TCM theory to slowly deplete Spleen Qi. When this goes on for months or years, the deficiency can progress to the sinking stage.

🤰 Childbirth and Heavy Lifting

In TCM theory, childbirth naturally weakens the body's Qi, including the Spleen. Repeated pregnancies or physically demanding work immediately after delivery may contribute to Spleen Qi Sinking. This is why postpartum care in Chinese tradition emphasizes rest, warming foods, and avoiding heavy exertion.

⏳ Chronic Illness or Long-Term Spleen Deficiency

Any condition that creates long-term Spleen Qi deficiency — chronic digestive issues, prolonged antibiotic use, or long-standing emotional strain — can eventually progress to sinking if the underlying pattern is not addressed from a TCM perspective.

🧠 Excessive Worry and Overthinking

In TCM theory, the emotion associated with the Spleen is pensiveness — excessive thinking, worrying, or mental rumination. Years of mental overwork (studying, data analysis, worrying) are believed to gradually wear down Spleen Qi, potentially contributing to the sinking pattern.

It's worth noting that from a TCM perspective, not everyone with Spleen Qi deficiency develops sinking. The sinking pattern represents a specific progression that involves both the quantity of Qi (not enough) and its direction (moving downward instead of upward).

What Does Spleen Qi Sinking Feel Like?

The hallmark of Spleen Qi Sinking is the sensation of downward pressure or heaviness. People experiencing this pattern from a TCM perspective might describe:

Sensation How People Describe It
Bearing-down feeling A sense of pressure, dragging, or fullness in the lower abdomen — like something is being pulled downward by gravity.
Anal or pelvic heaviness A feeling of downward pressure in the rectal or pelvic area, especially after standing for long periods.
Deep, unrelenting fatigue Not just "tired" — a bone-deep exhaustion that feels worse when standing and somewhat better when lying down.
Loose stools or diarrhea Frequent, loose bowel movements — as if the body can't hold things in. Often urgent.
Shortness of breath Since Lung Qi also relies on Spleen Qi's lifting action, weak Spleen Qi may contribute to shallow breathing or feeling like you can't get a full breath.
Prolonged recovery Feeling like it takes forever to bounce back from illness, exertion, or even a long day.
The most distinct clue: The sensation typically gets worse with prolonged standing or walking, and improves significantly when lying down. If you notice that your energy and comfort level dramatically shifts between standing and lying flat, Spleen Qi Sinking is worth exploring from a TCM perspective.

Beyond Heaviness: Less Obvious Signs

Not everyone experiences the classic bearing-down feeling. Some signs of Spleen Qi Sinking from a TCM perspective are subtler:

  • Chronic hemorrhoids — In TCM theory, hemorrhoids are sometimes associated with Spleen Qi Sinking, as the weakened holding function contributes to downward pressure on the rectal vessels.
  • A feeling of organ "dropping" — Some people describe a vague sense that their organs are shifting or dropping, even when no medical prolapse is diagnosed.
  • Weak, breathy voice — The Spleen is considered the root of post-natal Qi (the energy we generate after birth). When Spleen Qi sinks, the voice may lack projection and power.
  • Varicose veins — From a TCM perspective, the Spleen's failure to hold blood in the vessels and raise fluids can contribute to blood pooling in the lower extremities.
  • Difficulty holding urine — Though the Kidney system governs urination in TCM theory, Spleen Qi Sinking can compound issues of urinary control by failing to lift and support the pelvic structures.

The Spleen Qi Sinking Connection to Organ Prolapse

In Western medicine, organ prolapse occurs when the muscles and tissues that hold an organ in place weaken, and the organ slips downward. Common types include uterine prolapse, bladder prolapse (cystocele), and rectal prolapse.

From a TCM perspective, Spleen Qi Sinking is the framework that describes this tendency. While not every case of Spleen Qi Sinking involves a diagnosed prolapse, the TCM pattern and the Western medical condition share the same mechanical logic: something that should be held up is dropping down.

In TCM theory, the Spleen's raising function is central to maintaining organ position. When this function is compromised — whether through chronic Qi deficiency, physical strain, or age-related decline — the organs lack the support they need to stay in place.

Important distinction: It's crucial to understand that Spleen Qi Sinking is a TCM pattern description, not a Western medical diagnosis. A person can have strong Spleen Qi Sinking signs from a TCM perspective while having no diagnosed prolapse through Western medicine — and vice versa. These are two different frameworks looking at overlapping phenomenon.

Dietary Approaches in TCM for Spleen Qi Sinking

In TCM dietary theory, foods that support the Spleen's raising function share a few characteristics: they are warm, easy to digest, and often have a slightly sweet (but not sugary) taste. These are traditional dietary principles, not medical prescriptions.

Warming, Cooked Meals

Cold and raw foods are traditionally thought to require more digestive effort from the Spleen. For Spleen Qi Sinking, warm cooked foods are generally preferred in TCM dietary theory: soups, stews, congees (rice porridge), steamed vegetables, and slow-cooked grains.

Root Vegetables and Grains

In TCM food energetics, foods that grow downward (root vegetables) are thought to have an upward-lifting quality when eaten. Potatoes, sweet potatoes, carrots, yams, and pumpkin are traditionally used. Grains like millet, oats, and white rice are also considered Spleen-friendly.

Key Herbs in TCM Formulas

In TCM herbal theory, certain herbs are specifically considered to have a "lifting" or "raising" quality. These are traditionally used in combination formulas, not as single supplements:

  • Astragalus (Huang Qi, 黄芪) — Considered one of the primary herbs for raising Spleen Qi. It is traditionally used in TCM for Spleen Qi deficiency with sinking.
  • Codonopsis (Dang Shen, 党参) — A milder Qi tonic often used as a gentler alternative to ginseng for Spleen Qi patterns.
  • Atractylodes (Bai Zhu, 白术) — Traditionally used to strengthen the Spleen and dry Dampness, often combined with Astragalus for sinking patterns.
  • Cimicifuga (Sheng Ma, 升麻) — An herb specifically considered to have an "ascending" quality in TCM theory, traditionally used in sinking patterns.
  • Bupleurum (Chai Hu, 柴胡) — Known for its lifting and spreading action on Liver Qi, sometimes included in formulas addressing sinking.
⚠️ Herbal caution: These herbs are part of complex traditional formulas prescribed by qualified TCM practitioners. Self-prescribing individual herbs — especially strong Qi lifters like Astragalus or Cimicifuga — is not recommended. Always consult both a licensed TCM practitioner and your primary healthcare provider before taking any herbal preparations.

Foods to Consider Moderating

In TCM dietary theory, the following are traditionally considered potentially taxing for a weakened Spleen:

  • Cold drinks and iced beverages
  • Raw salads and cold fruits (especially in large quantities)
  • Excessive dairy (thought to create Dampness)
  • Heavy, greasy, or fried foods
  • Refined sugar and sweetened processed foods

These are not "bad" foods in a universal sense — the TCM framework simply suggests that when the Spleen is already depleted, easier-to-digest options may be more appropriate.

Lifestyle and Movement Practices

Beyond diet, some lifestyle adjustments are traditionally considered supportive for Spleen Qi Sinking in the TCM framework:

Rest When You Need It

The most consistent recommendation across TCM traditions for sinking patterns is adequate rest. Since sinking is an advanced form of Qi deficiency, pushing through fatigue may worsen the pattern. In TCM theory, the body repairs Qi during rest — especially during afternoon rest (12:00–2:00 PM, when Heart Qi governs in the organ clock).

Gentle Lifting Exercises

Paradoxically, certain gentle movements traditionally practiced in Qi Gong and TCM exercise systems are thought to support the Spleen's lifting action. These include slow, controlled movements that engage the pelvic floor and lower abdominal muscles — similar to Kegel exercises but integrated into a whole-body movement practice.

Avoid Prolonged Standing

From a TCM perspective, prolonged standing aggravates Spleen Qi Sinking because it works against the weakened raising function. Taking breaks to lie down or recline, using a footrest while sitting, and avoiding long periods of standing still may be helpful.

Warmth and Abdominal Support

In TCM theory, the Spleen prefers warmth. Using a warm compress or hot water bottle on the lower abdomen, wearing a supportive belly band, and keeping the abdomen covered in cold weather are traditional self-care practices.

Acupuncture and Acupressure Points

In TCM clinical practice, acupuncture is traditionally used to tonify Spleen Qi and address sinking patterns. Some acupoints commonly used include:

  • ST36 (Zusanli) — On the lower leg, below the knee. One of the most well-known points for strengthening Spleen and Stomach Qi in TCM.
  • SP6 (Sanyinjiao) — On the inner lower leg. Traditionally used to support the Spleen, Liver, and Kidney systems.
  • CV6 (Qihai) — On the lower abdomen. Known as the "Sea of Qi," traditionally used for Qi deficiency patterns.
  • CV4 (Guanyuan) — Below CV6, on the lower abdomen. Traditionally used to tonify Qi in deficiency patterns.
  • DU20 (Baihui) — On the top of the head. In TCM theory, this point is specifically considered to have an ascending, lifting action — it's often used for sinking patterns including Spleen Qi Sinking.

These points should be stimulated by a licensed acupuncturist. For home use, gentle acupressure (applying firm but comfortable pressure with the fingertip for 30–60 seconds on each point) is sometimes suggested as a self-care practice, though evidence for its effectiveness is limited.

What the Research Says

While Spleen Qi Sinking is a TCM pattern diagnosis that doesn't map directly onto Western medical categories, some research has explored related concepts:

  • A 2018 systematic review published in Medicine suggested that certain TCM herbal formulas traditionally used for Spleen Qi deficiency may show potential benefits for digestive function, though the authors noted the need for larger, higher-quality trials (PMID: 29768351).
  • Research on Astragalus (Huang Qi), a key herb in sinking-pattern formulas, has explored its effects on immune function and muscle metabolism. A 2016 review in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology noted preliminary evidence suggesting it may have tonifying properties, while emphasizing that most studies were preclinical (PMID: 27196281).
  • Clinical studies on acupoint ST36 (Zusanli) have investigated its potential effects on digestive motility and fatigue. A 2021 meta-analysis in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine suggested that ST36 stimulation may have measurable effects on gastric function, though the quality of available trials varied significantly.
Research note: It's important to be transparent here — TCM pattern-based research faces significant methodological challenges. Spleen Qi Sinking as a diagnosis cannot be directly studied in a double-blind randomized trial because it relies on pattern differentiation that Western medicine doesn't recognize. The research that exists looks at individual herbs or points, not the integrated TCM diagnostic framework. This doesn't invalidate the framework — it just means the evidence base is at an early stage using research methods designed for a different medical paradigm.

Practical Takeaways

If you recognize some of the signs of Spleen Qi Sinking described above, here are some things to consider discussing with your healthcare provider:

  1. Notice your patterns. Does your heaviness or fatigue get worse with standing and better with lying down? This directional quality is the most specific clue from a TCM perspective.
  2. Consider your diet's temperature. If your diet is heavy on cold foods, raw salads, and iced drinks, shifting toward warm, cooked meals for a period may be worth exploring.
  3. Address chronic digestive issues. Ongoing loose stools, bloating, and poor digestion are early signs of Spleen Qi deficiency that can, from a TCM perspective, precede the sinking pattern.
  4. Look at your pelvic floor health. If you notice signs of pelvic pressure or reduced organ support, a pelvic floor physical therapist (a Western medical professional) can offer assessment and exercises that may support the same areas TCM attributes to Spleen function.
  5. Consult a licensed TCM practitioner. Spleen Qi Sinking is a specific diagnosis within TCM pattern differentiation. A qualified practitioner can assess whether this pattern fits your presentation and recommend appropriate acupuncture or herbal support.
  6. Remember the big picture. TCM views Spleen Qi Sinking as a pattern that develops over time. Addressing it traditionally involves sustained changes to diet, lifestyle, and stress management — not quick fixes.

When to See a Doctor

The symptoms described in this article can overlap with conditions requiring medical attention. Please consult a healthcare provider if you experience:

  • A sensation of a bulge or pressure in the pelvic area
  • Difficulty passing urine or stool
  • Blood in your stool or urine
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Severe or persistent abdominal pain
  • A feeling that something is "coming out" of the vagina or rectum
  • New or worsening incontinence
  • Pain during intercourse that is new or worsening

These symptoms may indicate conditions such as pelvic organ prolapse, hernias, or other medical concerns that require proper diagnosis and treatment. TCM is a complementary perspective and is not a substitute for professional medical evaluation.
