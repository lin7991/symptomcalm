骨关节炎的中医解读：通过肝肾健康理解退行性病变

症状指南 · 8 分钟阅读

You've probably heard it before: osteoarthritis is just "wear and tear." Years of use slowly grind down the cartilage in your joints, and eventually, bone rubs against bone. The solution? Pain relievers, anti-inflammatories, physical therapy, and — when nothing else works — joint replacement surgery.

But if you're living with osteoarthritis, you know it's not that simple. The pain in your knees, hips, or hands doesn't follow a clean mechanical logic. Some days are worse than others. Cold weather triggers it. Stress makes it flare. And no amount of rest seems to reverse the gradual stiffening that has taken over your joints.

The frustration is real. Osteoarthritis affects over 32 million adults in the United States alone. It's the most common form of arthritis, yet the conventional toolkit — NSAIDs, corticosteroid injections, and eventually surgery — largely focuses on symptom management rather than addressing why the degeneration happened in the first place.

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) offers a different way of looking at osteoarthritis. Instead of seeing it purely as mechanical wear, TCM asks a deeper question: What has left these joints vulnerable to degeneration?

The Western View: Cartilage Breakdown and Inflammation

In conventional medicine, osteoarthritis (OA) is understood as a progressive condition where the protective cartilage at the ends of bones gradually thins and breaks down. Unlike rheumatoid arthritis — which is driven by systemic autoimmune inflammation — OA is classified as a non-inflammatory or "degenerative" condition, though low-grade inflammation is now recognized as a contributing factor.

Key risk factors include:

  • Age: Cartilage becomes less resilient over decades of use
  • Mechanical stress: Obesity, repetitive joint loading, previous injury
  • Genetics: Some people inherit weaker cartilage structure
  • Biomechanics: Joint misalignment that concentrates pressure unevenly

Standard treatments — acetaminophen, NSAIDs, topical analgesics, physical therapy, weight management, and eventually joint replacement — can be effective for symptom management. But they don't typically address the systemic factors that may underlie why one person's joints degenerate faster than another's.

A Different Map: Osteoarthritis Through the TCM Lens

TCM doesn't have a single word for "osteoarthritis." Instead, it classifies joint issues under the broader framework of Bi Syndrome (痹证) — obstruction in the channels. But while inflammatory arthritis patterns involve external pathogens like Wind, Cold, and Damp invading the joints, osteoarthritis has a different center of gravity.

The key insight: In TCM theory, osteoarthritis is primarily a condition of internal decline — specifically, the gradual depletion of Kidney Essence (Jing) and Liver Blood as we age. The degeneration isn't just about a lifetime of mechanical wear. It's about the body's fundamental reserve systems — the ones responsible for building and maintaining bone, cartilage, tendons, and ligaments — running low on resources.

Think of it like this: imagine your joints as hinges on a door. The conventional "wear and tear" model says the hinge wears out because you've opened and closed the door a million times. The TCM model asks a different question: Is the frame still strong? Are the hinge screws tight? Is the wood around the screws still solid? If the supporting structure has weakened, even normal use will accelerate wear.

The Three TCM Patterns Most Relevant to Osteoarthritis

While every individual's presentation is unique, three patterns are particularly relevant to understanding osteoarthritis from a TCM perspective:

1. Kidney Jing Deficiency (肾精亏虚) — The Root Pattern

What it means: In TCM, the Kidneys store Jing (Essence) — a deep reserve of vitality inherited from your parents and supplemented throughout life. The Kidneys are said to "govern the bones." When Kidney Jing is abundant, the bones are strong, and the marrow is full. When it declines — which it naturally does with age — the bones become brittle and the joints lose their structural integrity.

You might have this if: You're over 50 and experiencing gradual, bilateral joint degeneration (both knees, both hips). You also notice lower back ache, weak knees, feeling colder than you used to, and possibly some hearing loss or memory changes. Your energy levels have been declining.

TCM view: Kidney Jing deficiency is the "soil" in which osteoarthritis develops. Even if other patterns are present, this underlying deficiency is what makes the joints vulnerable to degeneration in the first place.

2. Liver Blood Deficiency (肝血亏虚) — The Supporting Structure

What it means: The Liver stores Blood and governs the tendons and ligaments. Healthy, well-nourished tendons keep joints stable and flexible. When Liver Blood is insufficient, the tendons become dry, tight, and brittle — they lose their elasticity and can no longer support the joint properly.

You might have this if: Your joints feel stiff (especially in the morning or after rest), your muscles feel tight, and you notice cramping. You may also experience dry eyes, brittle nails, dizziness, or irregular periods (for women). Your stiffness improves with gentle movement but returns when you're still.

TCM view: Liver Blood deficiency doesn't directly cause cartilage loss. But it creates the conditions — tight tendons, reduced blood flow to the joint area, poor lubrication — that accelerate mechanical wear. Think of it as the ligament and tendon system failing to properly stabilize the joint, leading to uneven stress on the cartilage.

3. Blood Stasis (血瘀) — The Chronic Obstruction

What it means: Over time, poor circulation in and around the joint creates Blood Stasis. This is both a consequence of the other patterns and a driver of further degeneration.

You might have this if: Your pain is sharp, fixed in one spot, and worse with pressure or prolonged inactivity. The joint may feel "stuck" and needs to be warmed up before it moves freely. You may have bony enlargement (Heberden's or Bouchard's nodes in the fingers). The classic "gelling" phenomenon — stiffness after sitting that improves after a few minutes of movement — is a hallmark.

TCM view: Blood Stasis is the "log jam" in the river. When Qi and Blood cannot flow freely through the channels around a joint, the tissues become starved of oxygen and nutrients. Metabolic waste products accumulate. Over years, this stagnant environment accelerates cartilage breakdown and promotes the formation of osteophytes (bone spurs) — which TCM interprets as the body's attempt to stabilize a joint that the soft tissues can no longer support.

How These Patterns Connect: The Osteoarthritis Cascade

In a TCM framework, these three patterns don't operate in isolation. They form a cascade:

  1. Kidney Jing declines with age → bones lose density and resilience
  2. Liver Blood becomes insufficient → tendons tighten, joint stability decreases, nutrition to the joint space diminishes
  3. Qi and Blood circulation slows → stagnation develops in the joint channels
  4. Blood Stasis sets in → chronic pain, stiffness, further degeneration

This cascade explains why osteoarthritis tends to worsen gradually over years, and why addressing the underlying deficiencies — not just the pain — is central to the TCM approach.

Important distinction: Unlike rheumatoid arthritis or acute gout flares — which TCM sees as involving active inflammatory pathogens (Wind, Heat, Damp-Heat) — osteoarthritis is understood primarily as a deficiency and stagnation pattern. The treatment strategy is therefore different: instead of expelling invaders, the focus is on nourishing the foundations (Kidney and Liver) and moving the stagnant Blood.

How TCM Traditionally Approaches Osteoarthritis

A TCM approach to osteoarthritis centers on nourishing the Kidney and Liver while moving Blood Stasis. Some approaches traditionally used include:

Herbal Medicine

Several Chinese herbal formulas are traditionally used for osteoarthritis patterns. Du Huo Ji Sheng Tang (Angelica pubescens and Loranthus decoction) is one of the most well-known — traditionally prescribed for Bi Syndrome involving Kidney and Liver deficiency with associated lower back and knee pain. Key herbs like Du Huo, Sang Ji Sheng (mulberry mistletoe), and Niu Xi (achyranthes) are considered to support bone health and circulation in TCM theory.

A 2016 systematic review in Phytomedicine noted that several herbs commonly used in TCM for osteoarthritis — including Curcuma longa (turmeric), Boswellia serrata (frankincense), and Angelica sinensis (Dong Quai) — contain compounds that have demonstrated anti-inflammatory and chondroprotective effects in laboratory studies.

Acupuncture

Acupuncture is traditionally used to move Qi and Blood through stagnant channels, potentially helping with pain and local circulation. Some studies suggest acupuncture may influence pain perception through endorphin release and modulation of pain pathways. Points commonly selected for osteoarthritis include local points around the affected joint (like ST35 and EX-LE5 around the knee, LI15 around the shoulder) combined with distal points on the Kidney, Liver, and Spleen meridians.

Moxibustion

Moxibustion — the gentle warming of acupuncture points with burning mugwort — is particularly relevant for osteoarthritis. Since the pattern involves Cold-type degeneration (stagnation that improves with warmth), applying heat to points around the affected joint and to Kidney-strengthening points on the lower back (like BL23 Shenshu) may help support circulation in TCM theory.

Dietary Therapy

In TCM dietary therapy, foods that nourish Kidney and Liver and move Blood are emphasized:

  • Dark leafy greens (especially in bone broth) — traditionally considered to nourish Blood
  • Black sesame seeds and walnuts — traditionally thought to support Kidney Jing
  • Bone broth and gelatin-rich foods — traditionally used to support joints
  • Turmeric, ginger, and cinnamon — warming spices that may help move circulation
  • Dark-colored beans (black beans, kidney beans) — considered Kidney-nourishing foods

Foods that may be reduced include excessive raw foods (which can be harder on the Spleen in TCM theory), cold beverages, and foods that create Dampness (dairy, fried foods, refined sugar).

Gentle Movement: Tai Chi and Qi Gong

Slow, circular movement is considered particularly beneficial for osteoarthritis in TCM. Tai Chi and Qi Gong are traditionally thought to circulate Qi and Blood through the joints without the impact that can aggravate degenerated cartilage. A 2021 meta-analysis in JAMA Internal Medicine found that Tai Chi may improve pain, physical function, and quality of life in people with chronic musculoskeletal conditions.

Integrating TCM With Conventional Osteoarthritis Care

Osteoarthritis is a complex condition, and the most effective approach often combines multiple strategies. TCM perspectives can complement standard care in several ways:

  • Pain management: Acupuncture and herbal therapy may offer additional pain management options alongside conventional medications
  • Slowing progression: The TCM focus on nourishing Kidney and Liver may support long-term joint health by addressing systemic factors that contribute to cartilage breakdown
  • Movement support: Tai Chi and Qi Gong provide gentle, joint-friendly movement that may improve range of motion and reduce stiffness
  • Dietary support: TCM dietary recommendations often align with anti-inflammatory eating patterns that support weight management — a key factor in reducing joint load
Important: Osteoarthritis is a progressive structural condition. TCM approaches are best used as a complementary perspective alongside — not instead of — evidence-based care. If you have severe OA with bone-on-bone changes, joint replacement may still be the most appropriate option. A TCM practitioner can work with your orthopedist or rheumatologist to support your overall joint health.

What Research Says About TCM and Osteoarthritis

Scientific interest in TCM approaches for osteoarthritis has grown considerably. A 2018 meta-analysis of 29 randomized controlled trials published in JAMA Network Open found that acupuncture was associated with reduced pain and improved function in chronic pain conditions, including osteoarthritis. The effect was strongest at 3-12 months compared to sham acupuncture, suggesting sustained benefit.

A systematic review in Phytomedicine (2016) examined 14 randomized trials on Chinese herbal medicine for osteoarthritis and found that several herbal formulas — particularly those containing herbs traditionally used to strengthen Kidney and Liver — showed potential benefits for pain reduction and function improvement compared to placebo or conventional medications.

A 2021 review in Frontiers in Pharmacology highlighted that the multi-target nature of TCM herbal formulas may be particularly suited to complex degenerative conditions like osteoarthritis, where oxidative stress, inflammation, and metabolic dysfunction interact. The review noted that several single compounds from TCM herbs — including curcumin (from turmeric) and tanshinone (from Salvia miltiorrhiza) — show chondroprotective effects in preclinical studies.

It's worth noting that while these findings are promising, many studies have methodological limitations — small sample sizes, short duration, and variability in treatment protocols. Larger, more rigorous trials are needed to confirm these effects.

Practical Takeaways

1. Understand the cascade. Osteoarthritis in TCM theory isn't just about "worn out" joints — it's about the gradual depletion of the systems that maintain joint health (Kidney Jing for bones, Liver Blood for tendons). Supporting these systems may help slow the progression.

2. Movement is medicine — the right kind. Gentle, circular, low-impact movement (Tai Chi, Qi Gong, walking, swimming) is traditionally recommended over high-impact exercise that can accelerate cartilage wear.

3. Warmth helps. Since osteoarthritis involves stagnation, warmth (moxibustion, warm baths, heating pads) may support circulation around affected joints. Gentle warming before movement can reduce morning stiffness.

4. Dietary support matters. Bone broth, dark leafy greens, black sesame seeds, and warming spices are traditionally emphasized. Reducing cold foods, raw vegetables, and dampness-producing foods (dairy, sugar, fried foods) may support the Spleen's ability to produce Qi and Blood.

5. Work with your medical team. TCM approaches are complementary, not alternative. Tell both your orthopedist and your TCM practitioner about all treatments you're using.

When to See a Doctor Immediately

⚠️ If you experience sudden worsening of joint pain, a joint that becomes hot, red, and swollen (especially if only one joint is affected), fever with joint pain, inability to bear weight on a joint after an injury, or severe pain that doesn't respond to rest, seek medical evaluation. These could indicate infection, fracture, gout flare, or other conditions requiring immediate attention.

If you have advanced osteoarthritis with bone-on-bone changes and significant functional limitation, consult an orthopedist. Joint replacement may be the most appropriate option to restore mobility and quality of life.
